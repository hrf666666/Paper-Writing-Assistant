# 5. Conclusion

Traditional light field depth estimation fundamentally struggles in non-Lambertian scenes due to the inherent breakdown of photometric consistency assumptions. To address this, we proposed the Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model, a physics-inspired framework that bridges rigorous electromagnetic wave optics and learnable geometric representations by abstracting complex light-matter interactions into two explicitly modeled, physically interpretable masks.

The primary contributions and findings of this work are threefold. First, we introduced the Dual-Mask Physical Model, comprising the Medium Mask $r(x,y)=a(x,y)/\lambda$ for quantifying dominant interaction types and the Angular Direction Mask $V(x,y,\theta_i)$ for characterizing wave vector deflection. This formulation provides the first unified theoretical framework that naturally reduces to the Lambertian special case while explicitly modeling non-Lambertian phenomena. Second, we developed a physically consistent differentiable rendering layer enforcing electromagnetic optical principles—including Fresnel reflectance and Rayleigh/Mie scattering phase functions—which constrained the physical consistency loss below $1\times10^{-4}$ and fundamentally eliminated the photometric consistency failures that plague traditional multi-view stereo methods. Third, we designed a three-stage physics-guided training pipeline. Extensive experiments demonstrated that this curriculum strategy achieves robust generalization across diverse environments, systematically reducing the validation MAE from $\le 0.05$ on HCI4D (Stage 1) to $\le 0.04$ on synthetic non-Lambertian data (Stage 2), and ultimately to $\le 0.03$ on challenging real-world target scenes (Stage 3). The proposed method consistently outperformed state-of-the-art baselines across MAE, RMSE, MSE, and Bad Pixel Ratio metrics, particularly in severe specular and scattering scenarios.

Despite these advances, the proposed framework exhibits certain limitations. The current parameterization of the Medium Mask assumes a single dominant interaction type per spatial location, which may oversimplify sub-pixel-level material mixing where complex inter-reflections, subsurface scattering, and specular highlights co-occur simultaneously. Furthermore, while the differentiable rendering layer enforces Fresnel and Rayleigh/Mie constraints, it does not explicitly model highly complex subsurface scattering or anisotropic participating media, which require higher-dimensional phase function representations beyond the current angular direction mask. Finally, the three-stage training pipeline, although robust and automated, incurs considerable computational overhead during the physics-based synthetic data rendering and sequential fine-tuning phases, which may limit its efficiency for rapid deployment to entirely novel environments without pre-rendered synthetic counterparts.

Building upon these limitations, future research will focus on extending the Dual-Mask Physical Model to accommodate spatially heterogeneous material mixtures at the sub-pixel level, potentially through a probabilistic mixture of interaction models rather than a single dominant type. We also plan to integrate higher-dimensional scattering phase functions to explicitly capture subsurface scattering and anisotropic media, further tightening the adherence to rigorous electromagnetic wave optics. Additionally, exploring self-supervised or zero-shot domain adaptation techniques could mitigate the reliance on physics-based synthetic rendering in Stage 2, significantly accelerating the training pipeline while preserving the physical consistency required for extreme non-Lambertian scenes.
