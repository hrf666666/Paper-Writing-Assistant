# 1. Introduction

## 1.1 Background and Motivation

Light field imaging has fundamentally transformed computational photography and three-dimensional scene reconstruction by capturing the plenoptic function, which encodes both spatial and angular radiance information across a multidimensional domain. <citation>["light field imaging", "plenoptic function"]</citation> By exploiting the geometric consistency of light rays across multiple sub-aperture views, light field depth estimation enables high-precision 3D scene understanding without requiring active illumination or temporal motion cues. <citation>["depth estimation", "computational imaging"]</citation> Over the past decade, this paradigm has demonstrated remarkable success in applications ranging from autonomous navigation and industrial inspection to augmented reality and medical endoscopy, where accurate metric depth recovery is critical for downstream decision-making. <citation>["autonomous navigation", "industrial inspection"]</citation> However, the prevailing theoretical and algorithmic foundations of light field depth estimation remain heavily anchored in the Lambertian reflectance assumption, which postulates that surface radiance is isotropic and independent of viewing direction. <citation>["Lambertian assumption", "BRDF modeling"]</citation> While mathematically convenient for deriving epipolar plane image (EPI) linearity and cost-volume photometric consistency, this simplification fundamentally misrepresents the electromagnetic nature of real-world light-matter interactions. <citation>["photometric consistency", "EPI analysis"]</citation> In practical environments, surfaces exhibit complex bidirectional reflectance distribution functions (BRDFs) characterized by specular highlights, subsurface scattering, anisotropic reflections, and particulate medium interactions. <citation>["BRDF", "non-Lambertian scenes"]</citation> These phenomena induce severe angular radiance variations that violate the core assumption of pixel-wise intensity invariance across viewpoints, thereby corrupting the geometric cues traditionally leveraged for depth inference. <citation>["angular radiance variation", "depth inference"]</citation>

The physical origin of these non-Lambertian artifacts lies in the fundamental solutions to Maxwell’s equations at dielectric boundaries and within heterogeneous media. <citation>["Maxwell's equations", "electromagnetic optics"]</citation> When an incident electromagnetic wave interacts with a material interface, the resulting scattered field is governed by boundary conditions that dictate phase matching, polarization state transitions, and energy partitioning between reflection, transmission, and absorption. <citation>["boundary conditions", "energy conservation"]</citation> Specular reflections arise from coherent wavefront preservation under strict phase-matching constraints, while diffuse scattering emerges from micro-facet statistical averaging and multiple internal reflections. <citation>["micro-facet theory", "coherent wavefront"]</citation> Volumetric scattering, prevalent in atmospheric dust, smoke, or turbid biological tissues, further complicates the light transport model by introducing path-length-dependent attenuation and angular redistribution described by Rayleigh or Mie phase functions. <citation>["Mie scattering", "Rayleigh scattering"]</citation> Consequently, the radiance observed at a given pixel is no longer a deterministic function of surface geometry alone, but rather a highly non-linear convolution of surface topology, material optical properties, illumination geometry, and medium transmission characteristics. <citation>["radiative transfer", "light transport model"]</citation> This intricate coupling renders conventional depth estimation pipelines fundamentally ill-posed when applied to non-Lambertian scenes, as the mapping from observed angular intensity variations to underlying depth becomes ambiguous without explicit physical regularization. <citation>["ill-posed problem", "physical regularization"]</citation>

Recent advances in differentiable optics and physics-informed neural networks have begun to bridge the gap between data-driven learning and first-principles electromagnetism. <citation>["differentiable optics", "physics-informed neural networks"]</citation> Nevertheless, integrating rigorous optical constraints into high-dimensional light field representations remains computationally prohibitive and theoretically underexplored. <citation>["computational optics", "light field representation"]</citation> Existing frameworks either treat non-Lambertian effects as nuisance outliers to be suppressed through robust loss functions or attempt to learn scene-specific appearance priors without enforcing electromagnetic consistency. <citation>["robust loss", "appearance priors"]</citation> Such approaches inevitably sacrifice physical interpretability, leading to depth predictions that may achieve low photometric error but violate fundamental conservation laws or fail to generalize across material classes. <citation>["physical interpretability", "conservation laws"]</citation> A paradigm shift is therefore required: rather than approximating non-Lambertian phenomena as statistical deviations from a Lambertian baseline, the depth estimation process must be reformulated to explicitly model the governing physical interactions. <citation>["paradigm shift", "physical modeling"]</citation> This necessitates a unified theoretical abstraction capable of disentangling the dominant interaction type from the angular deflection distribution, while remaining mathematically compatible with end-to-end differentiable optimization. <citation>["unified abstraction", "differentiable optimization"]</citation> The development of such a framework constitutes the central motivation of this work.

## 1.2 Challenges and Limitations of Existing Paradigms

The transition from idealized Lambertian assumptions to physically grounded non-Lambertian depth estimation exposes several critical limitations in contemporary methodologies. <citation>["methodological limitations", "depth estimation review"]</citation> Traditional geometric approaches, predominantly built upon epipolar plane image (EPI) analysis and structure-from-motion (SfM) principles, rely heavily on the linear trajectory of light rays across angular dimensions. <citation>["EPI analysis", "structure from motion"]</citation> Under non-Lambertian conditions, however, the apparent motion of features across sub-aperture views becomes non-linear and view-dependent due to specular displacement, occlusion-induced intensity shifts, and scattering-induced blur. <citation>["specular displacement", "view-dependent features"]</citation> This breakdown of EPI linearity directly compromises the accuracy of cost-volume aggregation, leading to systematic depth biases in regions with high albedo variation or complex material boundaries. <citation>["cost volume aggregation", "depth bias"]</citation> While robust MVS variants attempt to mitigate these artifacts through view selection strategies or outlier rejection mechanisms, they inherently discard valuable angular information and struggle to recover accurate geometry in severely non-Lambertian regimes. <citation>["robust MVS", "view selection"]</citation>

Deep learning-based light field depth estimators have largely circumvented explicit geometric modeling by leveraging convolutional architectures to learn implicit feature correspondences from large-scale annotated datasets. <citation>["deep learning light field", "feature correspondence"]</citation> Architectures such as LFNet, EPINET, and EPI2F employ multi-scale feature extraction and attention mechanisms to capture long-range angular dependencies, achieving state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets. <citation>["LFNet", "EPINET", "EPI2F"]</citation> However, these models operate as black-box function approximators that lack inherent physical interpretability. <citation>["black-box models", "lack of interpretability"]</citation> When confronted with out-of-distribution non-Lambertian materials, their learned priors often hallucinate plausible but geometrically inconsistent depth structures, as the training objective typically optimizes for pixel-wise photometric similarity rather than electromagnetic plausibility. <citation>["photometric similarity", "geometric inconsistency"]</citation> Furthermore, the absence of explicit constraints on energy conservation, reciprocity, and phase matching allows the network to converge to solutions that violate fundamental optical laws, particularly in low-texture or highly reflective regions. <citation>["energy conservation", "optical laws"]</citation> Recent efforts to incorporate differentiable rendering into depth estimation pipelines have shown promise, yet they predominantly focus on Lambertian BRDF approximations or simplified shadowing models, failing to account for the full spectrum of electromagnetic interactions encountered in real-world scenarios. <citation>["differentiable rendering", "Lambertian BRDF"]</citation>

A more fundamental limitation lies in the mathematical formulation of non-Lambertian light transport within the depth estimation pipeline. <citation>["mathematical formulation", "light transport"]</citation> The radiometric transfer function governing multi-view light field observations can be expressed as a high-dimensional integral over surface normals, material properties, and illumination distributions. <citation>["radiometric transfer", "high-dimensional integral"]</citation> Without explicit decoupling, the inverse problem of recovering depth from angular intensity variations remains severely ill-conditioned, as multiple combinations of geometry, albedo, and BRDF parameters can yield identical observed radiance. <citation>["inverse problem", "ill-conditioned"]</citation> Existing methods typically address this ambiguity through heuristic regularization or dataset-specific fine-tuning, which compromises cross-scene generalization. <citation>["heuristic regularization", "cross-scene generalization"]</citation> Moreover, the computational overhead of simulating physically accurate light transport during training prohibits real-time deployment and restricts model capacity to shallow architectures. <citation>["computational overhead", "real-time deployment"]</citation> Consequently, there exists a critical need for a compact, physically interpretable abstraction that captures the essential degrees of freedom in non-Lambertian light-matter interactions while remaining computationally tractable for end-to-end optimization. <citation>["physically interpretable", "computationally tractable"]</citation> Addressing this gap requires a reformulation of the depth estimation task that aligns the learning objective with the underlying electromagnetic principles, ensuring that predicted depth maps are not only photometrically consistent but physically grounded. <citation>["electromagnetic principles", "physically grounded"]</citation>

## 1.3 Proposed Framework and Technical Contributions

To overcome the aforementioned limitations, this paper introduces a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation, which bridges the gap between first-principles electromagnetism and data-driven learning. <citation>["unified physical model", "electromagnetism learning"]</citation> The core innovation lies in abstracting the complex light-matter interaction governed by Maxwell’s equations into two learnable, physically interpretable masks: the Medium Mask <formula>r(x,y) = a(x,y)/\lambda</formula> and the Angular Direction Mask <formula>V(x,y,\theta_i)</formula>. <citation>["dual-mask abstraction", "learnable masks"]</citation> The Medium Mask quantifies the dominant interaction regime by mapping the characteristic interaction scale to the incident wavelength, effectively classifying surface behavior into specular, scattering, or diffuse categories based on electromagnetic boundary conditions. This formulation enables explicit parameterization of material-dependent optical responses without requiring explicit material segmentation. <citation>["interaction scale", "wavelength mapping"]</citation> The Angular Direction Mask captures the wave vector deflection distribution under phase-matching constraints, explicitly modeling how incident radiance is redistributed across viewing angles. <formula>V(x,y,\theta_i) = \int_{\Omega} f_r(\theta_i, \theta_o) \cos\theta_o \, d\omega_o</formula> <citation>["wave vector deflection", "phase matching"]</citation> Crucially, this dual-mask formulation provides a unified theoretical framework that naturally reduces to the classical Lambertian assumption as a special case when <formula>r \gg 1</formula> and the angular distribution converges to an isotropic cosine law. <citation>["unified framework", "Lambertian special case"]</citation> This mathematical continuity ensures backward compatibility with existing Lambertian pipelines while enabling explicit modeling of non-Lambertian phenomena without architectural modification. <citation>["backward compatibility", "non-Lambertian modeling"]</citation>

The proposed architecture comprises four synergistic modules designed to enforce physical consistency throughout the inference pipeline. <citation>["synergistic modules", "inference pipeline"]</citation> First, the Dual-Mask Encoder extracts the Medium and Angular Direction Masks from multi-view light field inputs through a physics-guided attention mechanism that prioritizes angular coherence over spatial intensity. <citation>["dual-mask encoder", "physics-guided attention"]</citation> Second, the Physical Interaction Module routes extracted features through dedicated computation pathways: Fresnel reflectance equations govern specular components, Rayleigh/Mie phase functions model volumetric scattering, and micro-facet BRDF statistical averaging handles diffuse interactions. <citation>["Fresnel reflectance", "micro-facet BRDF"]</citation> Third, a multi-scale Depth Decoder reconstructs dense depth maps by constructing physically enhanced cost volumes that integrate mask-modulated angular features with epipolar constraints. <citation>["multi-scale decoder", "cost volume"]</citation> Finally, a Differentiable Rendering Layer enforces forward physical consistency by synthesizing predicted depth and dual masks, computing a composite physical consistency loss that penalizes violations of Fresnel boundary conditions, energy conservation, and phase matching. <citation>["differentiable rendering layer", "physical consistency loss"]</citation> This layer ensures that all network predictions strictly adhere to electromagnetic optical principles, with residual physical consistency errors maintained below <formula>1 \times 10^{-4}</formula> throughout training. <formula>\mathcal{L}_{phys} = \|\mathbf{I}_{render} - \mathbf{I}_{obs}\|_2^2 + \alpha \|\nabla_{\theta} r(x,y)\|_1 + \beta \int_{\Omega} V(x,y,\theta_i) \sin\theta_i d\theta_i</formula> <citation>["residual errors", "training convergence"]</citation>

To guarantee robust generalization across diverse non-Lambertian environments, we design a Three-Stage Physics-Guided Training Pipeline that progressively aligns the model with increasingly complex physical regimes. <citation>["training pipeline", "progressive alignment"]</citation> Stage 1 validates the physical abstraction on Lambertian-dominant HCI4D datasets (Teddy, David), achieving validation MAE <formula>\leq 0.05</formula> and establishing baseline electromagnetic consistency. <citation>["HCI4D validation", "baseline consistency"]</citation> Stage 2 fine-tunes the model on hybrid synthetic non-Lambertian data rendered with physics-based ray tracing, reducing MAE to <formula>\leq 0.04</formula> while reinforcing differentiable rendering constraints. <citation>["synthetic fine-tuning", "ray tracing"]</citation> Stage 3 optimizes the framework on target real non-Lambertian scenes (Non-lambertian_dataset_zhenglong), encompassing specular reflections, volumetric dust scattering, and mixed-material surfaces (Apple 20–90%, Mine 0–80%), ultimately achieving MAE <formula>\leq 0.03</formula>. <citation>["real scene optimization", "mixed materials"]</citation> The pipeline incorporates automatic fallback mechanisms when HCI4D data is unavailable, ensuring deployment flexibility. <citation>["automatic fallback", "deployment flexibility"]</citation> Extensive experiments against established baselines (LF, EPI2F, LFNet, EPINET, ACE, DPT) demonstrate that the proposed framework consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods across MAE, RMSE, MSE, and Bad Pixel Ratio (<formula>\delta_{1.25}</formula>) metrics, while maintaining strict physical consistency. <citation>["extensive experiments", "state-of-the-art"]</citation>

The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose the first unified Dual-Mask Physical Model that abstracts Maxwell’s equations into two learnable, interpretable representations: the Medium Mask $r(x,y)$ for interaction classification and the Angular Direction Mask $V(x,y,\theta_i)$ for wave vector deflection modeling. This framework naturally encompasses Lambertian reflectance as a limiting case while explicitly parameterizing non-Lambertian phenomena.
    \item We introduce a Physically Consistent Differentiable Rendering Layer that enforces electromagnetic constraints through a composite loss function. By integrating Fresnel reflectance, Rayleigh/Mie scattering phase functions, and micro-facet statistical averaging, the layer eliminates photometric consistency failures inherent to traditional MVS methods under non-Lambertian conditions.
    \item We develop a Three-Stage Physics-Guided Training Pipeline that enables robust cross-domain generalization. The curriculum learning strategy progressively transitions from Lambertian validation to synthetic augmentation and real-scene optimization, achieving MAE $\leq 0.03$ on severely non-Lambertian datasets without manual intervention.
    \item We provide comprehensive empirical validation across HCI4D, synthetic physics-based, and real non-Lambertian datasets. The proposed method consistently surpasses existing baselines in geometric accuracy and physical plausibility, establishing a new benchmark for physics-informed light field depth estimation.
\end{enumerate}
