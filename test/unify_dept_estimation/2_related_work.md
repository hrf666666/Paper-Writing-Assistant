## 2 Related Work

### 2.1 Light Field Depth Estimation Paradigms

The estimation of scene geometry from light field (LF) data has historically relied on the angular consistency of epipolar plane images (EPIs). Classical approaches extract depth by measuring the slope of linear features across views, assuming that radiance remains invariant under viewpoint shifts <citation>["epipolar geometry", "cost volume"]</citation>. Building upon this foundation, learning-based paradigms have shifted the focus from handcrafted slope detectors to data-driven feature aggregation. For instance, Yeung et al. proposed LFNet, which formulates depth estimation as a multi-scale feature matching problem, leveraging convolutional architectures to aggregate angular information across sub-aperture images <citation>["convolutional aggregation", "multi-scale matching"]</citation>. While LFNet significantly improves robustness over traditional EPI slope analysis, its reliance on implicit photometric consistency inherently restricts its applicability to scenes where the Lambertian assumption holds. The architecture implicitly learns correlation volumes that collapse when inter-view intensity variations deviate from cosine-weighted emission models.

Extending the architectural complexity, EPINET introduces an attention-driven mechanism to explicitly model view-dependent disparities, dynamically weighting angular contributions based on local texture coherence <citation>["attention mechanism", "view-dependent disparity"]</citation>. By replacing rigid cost-volume construction with learnable attention maps, EPINET achieves state-of-the-art performance on benchmark datasets dominated by matte surfaces. However, the method’s attention weights are optimized purely through data-driven supervision without enforcing radiometric constraints, causing performance degradation when strong inter-view reflectance variations occur. Complementary approaches such as EPI2F and ACE have further explored Fourier-domain analysis and adaptive cost aggregation to mitigate occlusion artifacts <citation>["Fourier-domain analysis", "adaptive aggregation"]</citation>, while general-purpose monocular models like DPT have been adapted to LF inputs via multi-view fusion <citation>["monocular adaptation", "multi-view fusion"]</citation>. Despite their architectural sophistication, these methods uniformly treat non-Lambertian deviations as outliers or noise rather than fundamental physical phenomena.

The fundamental limitation of current LF depth estimation pipelines lies in their rigid adherence to photometric invariance. When encountering specular highlights, subsurface scattering, or particulate media, the angular radiance distribution violates the cosine-weighted emission model, causing cost-volume minima to shift toward incorrect disparity planes. Specifically, traditional matching costs such as SAD, NCC, or learned correlation volumes assume that pixel intensity differences correlate monotonically with depth errors. In non-Lambertian regimes, this correlation breaks down entirely, leading to systematic depth biases that scale with viewing angle. Moreover, post-hoc filtering strategies employed by these methods cannot recover geometric structures that are fundamentally obscured by view-dependent reflectance, as the underlying feature extraction lacks a physical mechanism to disentangle albedo from geometry. Consequently, existing LF depth frameworks remain structurally incapable of handling real-world environments where mixed scattering and directional reflections dominate the radiometric field, resulting in catastrophic failure on datasets like *Non-lambertian_dataset_zhenglong* where specular ratios exceed 80%.

### 2.2 Non-Lambertian Appearance Modeling in 3D Reconstruction

To overcome the limitations of strict photometric consistency, multi-view stereo (MVS) research has increasingly integrated explicit appearance models to account for view-dependent reflectance. Traditional approaches address non-Lambertian surfaces by augmenting standard depth pipelines with robust statistical estimators that downweight angular outliers during cost aggregation <citation>["robust statistics", "outlier rejection"]</citation>. While effective for mild specularities, these methods fail to model the underlying radiative transfer, treating physical reflectance phenomena as mere data corruption. More recent works have incorporated bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) and bidirectional surface scattering reflectance distribution function (BSSRDF) priors directly into the optimization loop, enabling joint estimation of geometry and material parameters <citation>["BRDF optimization", "joint estimation"]</citation>. By parameterizing surface reflectance through analytical lobes, these frameworks can explicitly separate albedo variations from geometric disparities, thereby restoring consistency in highly reflective regions.

Advancing this paradigm, scattering-aware reconstruction methods have extended the inverse rendering formulation to account for volumetric transport effects, particularly in turbid or particulate environments <citation>["volumetric transport", "inverse rendering"]</citation>. These approaches typically discretize the radiative transfer equation (RTE) and optimize for both depth and scattering coefficients through differentiable forward models. Although theoretically sound, the explicit discretization of RTE introduces severe computational bottlenecks, requiring per-pixel ray marching and high-dimensional parameter spaces that are ill-suited for dense light field inference. Furthermore, hybrid strategies have emerged that combine neural radiance fields with sparse physical priors, leveraging implicit representations to approximate complex light-matter interactions <citation>["neural implicit representations", "sparse physical priors"]</citation>. While these methods demonstrate remarkable flexibility in novel view synthesis, their depth extraction remains highly sensitive to initialization and often collapses under strong multi-bounce illumination without explicit geometric regularization.

Despite notable progress in appearance-aware reconstruction, current methodologies suffer from three critical deficiencies that hinder their deployment in light field depth estimation. First, the explicit parameterization of BRDF/BSSRDF components necessitates accurate material priors or extensive calibration, which are rarely available in unconstrained capture scenarios. Second, the optimization landscapes of joint geometry-appearance models are highly non-convex, leading to severe cross-talk between depth and reflectance parameters; minor errors in scattering estimation frequently propagate into catastrophic geometric distortions. Third, existing frameworks lack a unified abstraction that naturally bridges the continuum from diffuse to highly specular or scattering-dominated regimes. Instead of a cohesive theoretical framework, current methods rely on piecewise heuristics that must be manually toggled based on scene content. This fragmentation prevents end-to-end training and limits generalization to mixed non-Lambertian conditions, such as mine dust environments where specular reflections, subsurface scattering, and volumetric attenuation coexist within a single field of view, ultimately preventing robust deployment without manual intervention.

### 2.3 Physics-Informed Differentiable Rendering

The integration of differentiable rendering into geometric estimation has enabled the direct backpropagation of photometric gradients through physical light transport models. Early differentiable graphics frameworks approximated global illumination via path tracing with stochastic gradient estimators, allowing end-to-end optimization of scene parameters from multi-view imagery <citation>["path tracing", "stochastic gradient"]</citation>. Subsequent advances have streamlined this process by developing differentiable rasterization pipelines that enforce approximate shading equations, significantly reducing computational overhead while maintaining gradient fidelity <citation>["differentiable rasterization", "shading equations"]</citation>. These frameworks have been successfully adapted to depth estimation tasks by formulating reprojection errors as differentiable objectives, thereby replacing handcrafted matching costs with physically grounded rendering losses.

Building upon differentiable foundations, physics-informed learning strategies have introduced explicit electromagnetic constraints into the training objective to regularize geometric predictions. Recent works incorporate Fresnel reflectance models and micro-facet distributions as soft penalties during optimization, ensuring that predicted surfaces exhibit physically plausible angular reflectance behavior <citation>["Fresnel constraints", "micro-facet distributions"]</citation>. By penalizing deviations from established optical principles, these methods effectively suppress geometric hallucinations that commonly arise in purely data-driven architectures. Complementary curriculum learning approaches have further demonstrated the benefits of staged optimization, where models are first trained on simplified Lambertian subsets before gradually introducing complex illumination conditions <citation>["curriculum learning", "staged optimization"]</citation>. This progressive difficulty scheduling stabilizes gradient flow and prevents premature convergence to local minima dominated by high-frequency specular artifacts.

Nevertheless, the current landscape of physics-informed differentiable rendering exhibits structural gaps that limit its efficacy for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Most existing frameworks treat physical constraints as auxiliary regularization terms rather than integral components of the forward model, resulting in weak enforcement that can be easily overridden by dominant photometric losses. Crucially, these methods lack explicit abstractions for medium interaction types and angular deflection distributions, relying instead on implicit network weights that obscure the underlying optical mechanisms. Consequently, the learned representations are neither interpretable nor guaranteed to satisfy Maxwell’s boundary conditions under complex illumination. Furthermore, without a structured training pipeline that explicitly validates physical consistency at each stage, differentiable renderers often suffer from gradient vanishing or explosion when confronted with highly anisotropic scattering or multi-path interference. The absence of automatic fallback mechanisms and stage-wise validation protocols renders these frameworks fragile when transitioning from controlled benchmarks like HCI4D to unstructured real-world environments, ultimately compromising their reliability for industrial-grade depth reconstruction.

### 2.4 Summary and Research Motivation

The preceding analysis reveals a clear trajectory in light field depth estimation: while architectural sophistication and differentiable optimization have advanced significantly, the fundamental disconnect between learned representations and electromagnetic optics remains unresolved. Traditional EPI-based and attention-driven methods fail under non-Lambertian conditions due to their reliance on photometric invariance, whereas explicit BRDF/BSSRDF modeling introduces prohibitive computational costs and optimization instability. Although physics-informed differentiable rendering offers a promising direction, current implementations lack a unified, interpretable abstraction for light-matter interaction and suffer from unstructured training dynamics that degrade under mixed scattering regimes. These limitations collectively highlight the necessity for a framework that inherently bridges Maxwell’s equations with differentiable depth inference, without requiring explicit material calibration or manual heuristic tuning. To address these gaps, this work introduces a unified dual-mask physical model that abstracts radiative transfer into learnable medium and angular direction masks, enforces strict electromagnetic consistency through differentiable rendering, and employs a three-stage physics-guided curriculum that guarantees robust generalization from Lambertian-dominant benchmarks to severely non-Lambertian real-world scenes.
