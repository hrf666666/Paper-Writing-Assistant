# Universal Academic Writing Discipline

These rules are venue-independent. They apply to every chapter, every paragraph, every sentence.

## 1. Information Density

Every sentence must do one of three things:
- Advance an argument
- Introduce evidence
- Specify a mechanism

Sentences that do none of these are deleted. Specifically:

- No throat-clearing: "It is important to note that...", "It should be mentioned that...", "Worth highlighting is..."
- No mechanical signposts: "This raises the question of...", "In what follows...", "Having established X, we now turn to Y..."
- Do not recap the previous paragraph's conclusion as a lead-in
- Do not end with empty statements: never write "this contributes to our understanding of..." — state exactly what it contributes
- No "wheel-spinning": do not restate what was just said within the same paragraph

## 2. Logical Cohesion

Logical relations between sentences and paragraphs must be explicit, but carried by substance, not by connectors.

Paragraph level:
- First sentence establishes the paragraph's logical position (topic sentence as signpost)
- Summary judgment first, evidence follows to support it
- Paragraphs are vector relations (contrast, specification, deepening, mechanism), not parallel lists
- Do not use the "one paragraph on A, one on B, together these..." structure
- End paragraphs with an analytical statement, not a dangling narration

Sentence level:
- Each sentence continues, turns, or deepens the previous one
- Do not use "Furthermore", "Additionally", "Moreover", "In addition" to mask logical gaps
- Pronouns (this, these, it) must lock onto their antecedent immediately
- Chain of reasoning must not skip steps
- Cohesion through logic, not through keyword repetition

## 3. Tone and Register

British academic restraint. Cool, restrained, understated.

- No American boosters: really, very, hugely, remarkably, strikingly, notably
- No hedging-as-politeness: I think, I feel, in my opinion, to be honest
- No stakes-raising adverbs: Unsurprisingly, Interestingly, Indeed, Of course, Naturally
- No exclamation marks, rhetorical questions, or suspense constructions
- No editorializing: in results sections, let numbers speak for themselves
- No promotional language: do not use novel, unique, important contribution as self-praise
- No overclaim signals: do not use prove, demonstrate conclusively, definitively, the cause; use consistent with, indicates, the evidence supports
- Present tense for findings, past tense for events
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon words over Latin: use not utilise, show not demonstrate, start not commence, about not regarding (except for established technical terms)
- Avoid Latin abbreviations (e.g., cf., viz.) in body text; footnotes are acceptable
- Minimize nominalization: "this analysis examines"优于"the examination of this analysis"
- Use passive voice sparingly: only when the agent is unimportant or foregrounding the object
- Understate, never overstate

## 4. Lexical and Syntactic Prohibitions

The following are hard-banned in all prose:

- Em-dashes: use commas or parentheses instead
- First person singular (I): use "we" for the research team where necessary, but minimize
- Bullet points in prose body text
- "i.e.": use a comma or "namely"
- "taken together" or "Together," as sentence opener
- "genuinely", "honestly", "straightforward"
- Filler adverbs: crucially, importantly, indeed
- Semicolons and colons only at true logical hinges; prefer periods otherwise

## 5. Section-Level Division of Labor

- Results section: strictly descriptive, no mechanism discussion
- Discussion/Analysis section: discuss mechanisms but do not forward-reference specific content from later sections (light signposting is acceptable)
- No backward cross-references in results
- Footnotes: 3 sentences max; longer content belongs in body text
- Footnotes must not repeat body arguments; purely bibliographic or supplementary new information only

## 6. Citation Integration (Oxford Style)

- Follow Oxford citation style: citations are noun-level participants in sentences (e.g., "As demonstrated by Smith [1], ...")
- Citations must be woven naturally into sentence structure, not piled at sentence ends
- Every citation must serve a rhetorical purpose: support, contrast, or attribution
- Do not cite without engaging: "Several methods exist [1-5]" is unacceptable — specify what they do and how they differ
- Citation density targets per section: Introduction 5-10, Related Work 15-30, Methodology 3-8, Experiments 5-10, Conclusion 0-2

## 7. Numbers, Units, Dates

- Use % universally; consistent across text, tables, and figures
- Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above
- Years, statistics, and data always use numerals
- Spaces around equals signs: X = Y
- Date ranges use unspaced en-dash: 1840–2010

## 8. Figures and Tables

- Captions in sentence case, no trailing period
- Source/Notes in italics
- Write "Author's construction" or "Author's calculations" (not "Calculated by the author")
- Reproduced figures: "Reproduced from..."
- Data from others: "Original data from..."
- Scale bars and technical notes go in Notes, not in caption

## 9. Argument Structure (Reviewer-Proof)

When the system generates content, it must anticipate reviewer scrutiny:

- Logical chain: identify where reference is ambiguous, where transitions are missing, where reasoning skips steps
- Content precision: flag claims that are too strong, numbers that could be challenged, data points inconsistent with body claims
- Literature positioning: how you frame yourself vs. prior work; where contributions are understated or overstated
- Overclaim watch: which phrasing promises more than evidence supports

## 10. Self-Diagnostic Rules

Before finalizing any section, verify:

- Every paragraph has a topic sentence that establishes logical position
- No paragraph is a parallel list without a vector relation (contrast, specification, deepening, mechanism)
- No sentence uses a connector (Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally) that masks a logical gap
- All pronouns (this, these, it) have an unambiguous antecedent within 2 sentences
- No claim uses "proves" or "demonstrates conclusively" without decisive evidence
- Numbers in Abstract match numbers in Experiments exactly
- No Chinese characters remain anywhere in the output
