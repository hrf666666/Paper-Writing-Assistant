# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Traditional Light Field Depth Estimation Methods

Conventional light field depth estimation algorithms primarily extract geometric cues from epipolar plane images (EPIs) by leveraging the photo-consistency assumption. A representative approach in this category employs the structure tensor to analyze the local orientation of EPI lines <citation>["EPI structure tensor", "light field depth estimation"]</citation>. By computing the gradient structure tensor across the spatial and angular dimensions, the dominant eigenvector corresponds to the line slope, which is inversely proportional to the scene depth. This geometric feature extraction mechanism yields accurate depth maps for regions with distinct textures and diffuse reflectance, establishing the EPI slope as a standard cue for light field analysis.

While EPI slope analysis performs well in textured regions, it degrades in textureless or occluded areas where linear structures become ambiguous. To address this limitation, subsequent methods integrate multiple physical cues, specifically combining defocus and correspondence information <citation>["defocus and correspondence cues", "depth from defocus"]</citation>. In this framework, a depth-from-defocus cue is derived by measuring the variance of refocused images across different depth planes, while a depth-from-correspondence cue is computed using multi-view stereo matching. These complementary cues are then fused within a Markov Random Field (MRF) optimization framework. The integration of defocus cues enhances depth estimation in weakly textured regions, whereas correspondence cues maintain boundary sharpness, demonstrating the benefit of combining diverse geometric and photometric features.

The discrete angular resolution of practical light field cameras (e.g., $9 \times 9$ or $17 \times 17$) strictly limits the depth resolution of both EPI slope and standard stereo matching techniques. To overcome this quantization bottleneck, advanced multi-view stereo (MVS) approaches introduce sub-pixel phase shifting to achieve continuous depth estimation <citation>["sub-pixel phase shift", "multi-view stereo light field"]</citation>. By applying sub-pixel translations to the sub-aperture images and evaluating the photo-consistency cost across shifted views, these methods construct a high-resolution cost volume. A robust cost aggregation strategy, often guided by edge-aware filtering, is subsequently applied to infer sub-pixel accurate disparities. This approach improves depth resolution and reduces the staircase artifacts commonly observed in discrete angular sampling.

Despite their contributions to geometric feature extraction, these traditional methods share structural limitations rooted in their underlying physical assumptions. First, they depend heavily on the Lambertian surface assumption, presuming that pixel intensity remains constant across viewpoints. When encountering non-Lambertian surfaces such as specular or scattering materials, photo-consistency is violated, and the EPI line structures are distorted or destroyed, leading to failure in depth calculation. Second, these approaches lack a physical-level decoupling of illumination, depth, and material properties. The handcrafted geometric features, such as structure tensors or variance metrics, possess limited representational capacity for complex materials under discrete angular resolutions, failing to distinguish between environmental lighting, parallax shifts, and material reflections. Consequently, their robustness deteriorates considerably in complex mixed scenes. Unlike these conventional techniques that treat non-Lambertian reflections as outliers, our three-layer angular signal decomposition physical model explicitly decouples illumination, depth, and material information from both physical and geometric perspectives. Building upon this theoretical foundation, the adaptive geometric dual-mask routing architecture processes Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions uniformly, overcoming the bottleneck of single EPI models in complex real-world environments.

## 2.2 Deep Learning-based General Light Field Depth Estimation

Deep learning techniques have significantly improved light field depth estimation by replacing handcrafted geometric features with data-driven representations. Early convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures predominantly focused on epipolar plane images (EPIs) to exploit local linear structures. A representative approach, EPINet <citation>["EPINet", "light field depth estimation"]</citation>, extracts directional EPI patches and employs a multi-stream CNN to regress disparity values. By learning the mapping between EPI slopes and depth directly from data, this method achieves high accuracy in textured Lambertian regions. Subsequent variants enhance this paradigm by incorporating deformable convolutions or multi-scale feature extraction to improve robustness against occlusions and textureless areas <citation>["deformable EPI", "multi-scale light field"]</citation>. These EPI-centric models demonstrate that deep networks can effectively capture local geometric cues without explicit structure tensor computations.

While EPI-based networks excel at capturing local angular variations, they often struggle with global context integration. To address this limitation, researchers have developed architectures that process sub-aperture images (SAIs) or macropixels to fuse spatial and angular information effectively. For instance, MacPI <citation>["MacPI", "macropixel light field"]</citation> utilizes a grid-like input representation of macropixels and applies 3D convolutions to simultaneously extract spatial textures and angular disparities. More recently, Transformer-based architectures have been introduced to model long-range dependencies across the light field. LF-Transformer <citation>["LF-Transformer", "light field attention"]</citation> employs a customized self-attention mechanism that computes correlations across both spatial and angular dimensions, enabling the network to aggregate global photo-consistency cues and refine depth boundaries in complex scenes. These spatial-angular fusion methods yield significant improvements in overall depth map quality by leveraging the full 4D light field structure.

To further improve robustness in challenging scenarios, recent studies have integrated cost volume formulations and attention mechanisms into light field networks. Approaches constructing 4D cost volumes $C(x, y, u, v, d)$ <citation>["4D cost volume", "light field stereo"]</citation> evaluate feature similarities across all viewpoints before applying 3D regularization networks. By explicitly measuring multi-view photo-consistency, these cost-volume-based methods mitigate depth ambiguities in occluded regions and achieve high accuracy on standard synthetic benchmarks. The integration of channel and spatial attention modules further allows these networks to dynamically weight reliable viewpoints, suppressing noisy signals from occluded or inconsistent views <citation>["attention mechanism", "viewpoint weighting"]</citation>.

Despite these architectural advancements, general deep learning-based light field depth estimation methods exhibit inherent limitations when confronted with non-Lambertian surfaces and mixed real-world scenes. First, these networks predominantly treat light field imaging as a black-box end-to-end mapping, lacking physical priors such as angular signal decomposition. By relying solely on data-driven fitting of EPI slopes or cost volume matching, the models possess poor interpretability and collapse when physical assumptions, such as photo-consistency, are violated by specular or scattering reflections. Second, existing architectures employ a single, unified network branch to process all pixels identically. This homogeneous processing fails to adapt to the distinct reflectance properties of Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions within mixed scenes, leading to significant accuracy degradation in non-Lambertian areas where linear EPI structures are absent. Finally, during cross-domain training—such as transitioning from purely Lambertian synthetic datasets to mixed urban environments—these models lack effective domain balancing strategies. Consequently, the networks tend to overfit to the dominant Lambertian pixels, resulting in insufficient generalization and significant performance drops on the minority non-Lambertian domains.

## 2.3 Deep Learning-based Light Field Depth Estimation for Non-Lambertian and Complex Scenes

While conventional EPI-based networks rely heavily on the Lambertian assumption, real-world scenes frequently contain non-Lambertian materials that violate photo-consistency. To mitigate the adverse effects of specular and translucent reflections, several approaches treat non-Lambertian regions as outliers and employ masking or filtering mechanisms. For instance, confidence-aware light field depth estimation methods <citation>["confidence mask", "light field depth estimation"]</citation> generate a reliability map to identify and suppress specular highlights before disparity regression. By assigning near-zero weights to high-variance angular pixels, these networks prevent corrupted EPI structures from degrading the overall depth map. Similarly, specular-robust architectures <citation>["specular robust", "epipolar plane image"]</citation> utilize a pre-trained highlight detection module to mask out non-Lambertian pixels, effectively converting the problem into a sparse depth estimation task. Although these masking strategies improve robustness in mixed scenes, they inherently discard the rich material and geometric information embedded within the non-Lambertian reflections.

Beyond simple masking, other researchers attempt to separate diffuse and specular components by leveraging physical or frequency-domain priors. Angular spectrum analysis methods <citation>["angular spectrum", "reflection separation"]</citation> decompose the light field into diffuse and specular layers by exploiting their distinct frequency signatures in the angular domain. By applying bandpass filters to the angular frequency spectrum, these models isolate the low-frequency diffuse component for depth estimation while treating the high-frequency specular component as a separate reflection layer. Tensor decomposition approaches <citation>["tensor decomposition", "light field reflection"]</citation> further model the light field as a sum of low-rank diffuse and sparse specular tensors, formulated as $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{diffuse} + \mathcal{L}_{specular}$. While these decomposition techniques provide a theoretical basis for reflection separation, their reliance on continuous frequency assumptions often limits their discriminative power when applied to light fields with discrete and limited angular resolutions.

The acute scarcity of ground-truth depth data for non-Lambertian surfaces presents a significant challenge for data-driven models. To address this data imbalance, domain adaptation and specialized sampling strategies have been introduced. Synthetic-to-real adaptation frameworks <citation>["domain adaptation", "light field depth"]</citation> pre-train networks on large-scale synthetic Lambertian datasets and fine-tune them on unlabeled real-world scenes using unsupervised consistency losses. Alternatively, hard example mining techniques <citation>["hard example mining", "complex scenes"]</citation> dynamically adjust the sampling probability during training to focus on regions with high prediction errors, such as occlusions and specularities. Despite these efforts, the domain gap between synthetic Lambertian priors and real-world non-Lambertian physics remains substantial, often leading to unstable convergence and suboptimal mean absolute error (MAE) in complex mixed environments.

Despite the progress made by these approaches, existing methods for handling non-Lambertian regions exhibit several shared limitations. First, existing methods handling non-Lambertian regions predominantly treat them as "noise" for simple masking or filtering, failing to genuinely comprehend and utilize the geometric characteristics of non-Lambertian reflections—such as the bimodal distribution in EPIs—from a physical mechanism perspective, particularly the material information residing in residual components. Second, there is a lack of a unified dual-branch routing architecture to process both Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions with high precision simultaneously; current models often sacrifice Lambertian performance to improve non-Lambertian accuracy, preventing truly unified processing. Third, given the extreme scarcity of non-Lambertian training data, current methods lack effective mechanisms such as domain-balanced sampling combined with weighted random sampling, resulting in unstable training and unsatisfactory MAE in real-world mixed scenes.

To overcome these bottlenecks, transitioning from heuristic masking to physics-driven unified processing is essential. Establishing a three-layer angular signal decomposition physical model thoroughly decouples illumination, depth, and material information, demonstrating that geometric features possess greater discriminative power than spectral features under discrete angular resolutions. Building upon this physical prior, an adaptive geometric dual-mask routing architecture overcomes the failure of single EPI models on non-Lambertian surfaces, enabling unified and adaptive processing that significantly enhances depth estimation accuracy and robustness in complex real-world mixed scenes.

Although existing masking and filtering strategies mitigate non-Lambertian outliers by suppressing corrupted angular pixels, they inherently lack a rigorous physical formulation to disentangle material properties from geometric structures, inevitably causing information loss and performance degradation in complex mixed scenes. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by introducing a three-layer angular signal decomposition model to theoretically decouple illumination, depth, and material priors via discriminative geometric features, coupled with an adaptive geometric dual-mask routing architecture to seamlessly unify the processing of both Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions.
