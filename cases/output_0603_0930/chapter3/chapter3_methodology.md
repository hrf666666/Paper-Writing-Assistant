# 3. Methodology

In this section, we present the overall architecture of the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Different from previous works that rely on implicit feature learning or continuous angular spectrum analysis, our framework explicitly integrates physical reflectance priors into a deep neural network. The architecture is designed to decouple the complex light field signal into distinct physical components and process them through specialized pathways. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the model comprises a physical decomposition frontend, a dual-branch feature extraction backbone, and a mask-guided fusion backend. This topology enables the network to adaptively route and process Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions, yielding accurate depth maps and domain classification masks simultaneously.

The network takes a $9 \times 9$ angular light field image patch, denoted as $I(u,v)$, where $u$ and $v$ represent the angular coordinates. To address the limitations of frequency-domain methods that require dense angular sampling, we introduce the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module. The motivation is to explicitly disentangle the raw light field signal into physically meaningful components without relying on continuous angular resolution. Specifically, the module decomposes the angular signal into three independent layers: a direct current (DC) component representing ambient illumination, a linear component parameterized by coefficients $a$ and $b$ representing disparity (i.e., epipolar plane image slopes), and a residual component $\varepsilon(u,v)$ capturing material properties and reflectance types. The physical decomposition is formulated as:
$$ I(u,v) = DC + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v) $$
where $DC$, $a$, and $b$ are estimated via least-squares fitting across the angular dimensions. Concurrently, the module computes highly discriminative geometric features, such as the angular gradient $\nabla_{angular} I$, directly from the residual term. This explicit physical decoupling ensures that the subsequent branches receive purified signals, substantially improving the robustness of feature extraction in discrete $9 \times 9$ angular sampling scenarios.

Following the physical decomposition, the data flows into a Dual-Branch Architecture designed to handle the distinct geometric characteristics of different surface types. The motivation for this parallel design is that a single unified branch struggles to simultaneously model the strict photo-consistency of Lambertian surfaces and the complex view-dependent variations of non-Lambertian surfaces. The upper pathway, the Lambertian EPI Branch, receives the linear coefficients $(a,b)$ and EPI structural features. It leverages the linear EPI line structures and incorporates a piecewise smooth continuous depth prior via a Planar Regularized Sampling Operator (PRSO) to refine depth estimation in diffuse regions. Conversely, the lower pathway, the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch, processes the residual component $\varepsilon(u,v)$ and the extracted geometric features. Instead of relying on disrupted EPI line slopes, this branch detects X-shape patterns and bimodal distributions within the EPIs. It employs geometric convolutions to extract depth and material cues from non-Lambertian regions, augmented by Random Tone-mapping Augmentation (RTA) to mitigate data scarcity. By separating the processing logic, the dual-branch design effectively prevents the interference between diffuse and specular feature representations.

To integrate the complementary features from the dual branches, the architecture employs a Dual-Mask Routing & Fusion module. The primary motivation is to avoid suboptimal scene-level binary cross-entropy classification and instead achieve precise pixel-level adaptive fusion. The module takes the feature maps from both branches and the highly discriminative angular gradient (which achieves a Cohen's d of 0.983 between domains) as inputs. First, the angular gradient is fed into a multi-layer perceptron followed by a sigmoid activation to generate a pixel-level domain mask $M$:
$$ M = \sigma(\text{MLP}(\nabla_{angular} I)) $$
Simultaneously, the output features from the Lambertian and non-Lambertian branches are projected to a unified channel dimension using a FUSE\_DIM projection layer (with the channel size set to 96) to balance the feature representations. The final depth map $D$ is then computed through a mask-weighted fusion operation:
$$ D = M \odot D_L + (1 - M) \odot D_{NL} $$
where $D_L$ and $D_{NL}$ denote the projected depth features from the Lambertian and non-Lambertian branches, respectively, and $\odot$ represents the element-wise multiplication. Ultimately, the network outputs the high-precision Depth Map and the Domain Mask. This mask-guided fusion mechanism ensures that each pixel's depth is derived from the most physically appropriate branch, significantly enhancing the overall estimation accuracy in complex mixed scenes.

## 3.2 三层角度信号分解模块 (Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition)

The primary objective of the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module is to explicitly disentangle the raw light field signal into physically meaningful components without relying on continuous angular resolution. Conventional light field depth estimation methods predominantly rely on Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) slopes or continuous angular spectrum analysis, which inherently assume Lambertian reflectance. When encountering non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular highlights or complex reflections, the angular signal undergoes severe non-linear distortions, causing these conventional methods to fail. Different from previous works that implicitly learn feature representations or require dense angular sampling for frequency-domain analysis, our module explicitly formulates the angular signal decomposition as a physical parameter estimation problem. This design isolates the Lambertian components for accurate depth extraction while preserving the non-Lambertian residuals for material and geometric structure analysis.

Let the input to this module be a $9 \times 9$ angular light field image patch, denoted as $I(u,v)$, where $u$ and $v$ represent the discrete angular coordinates, $u, v \in \{-4, -3, \dots, 4\}$. The physical reflectance model parameterizes the angular intensity variation as a superposition of ambient illumination, parallax-induced linear shifts, and material-specific non-linear residuals. Mathematically, the angular signal is modeled as:
$$ I(u,v) = I_{dc} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v) $$
where $I_{dc}$ represents the direct current (DC) component corresponding to the ambient illumination or albedo, $a$ and $b$ are the linear coefficients along the $u$ and $v$ angular axes encoding the parallax (and thus depth) information, and $\varepsilon(u,v)$ denotes the residual component capturing non-Lambertian effects such as specularities and complex reflections.

To solve for the physical parameters, we employ an orthogonal projection approach. Given the symmetric distribution of the angular coordinates around zero, the basis functions $\phi_0(u,v) = 1$, $\phi_1(u,v) = u$, and $\phi_2(u,v) = v$ are mutually orthogonal. This orthogonality allows for the closed-form analytical derivation of the parameters without iterative optimization, ensuring computational efficiency. The DC component $I_{dc}$ is computed as the mean intensity across the angular domain:
$$ I_{dc} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} I(u,v) $$
where $N = 81$ is the total number of angular samples. The linear coefficients $a$ and $b$, which dictate the EPI slopes and correspond to the horizontal and vertical disparities, are derived by projecting the signal onto the respective angular axes:
$$ a = \frac{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} u \cdot I(u,v)}{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} u^2}, \quad b = \frac{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} v \cdot I(u,v)}{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} v^2} $$
With the illumination and linear parallax components determined, the residual signal $\varepsilon(u,v)$ is obtained by subtracting the reconstructed Lambertian approximation from the original input:
$$ \varepsilon(u,v) = I(u,v) - (I_{dc} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v) $$
This residual map effectively isolates the high-frequency angular variations caused by non-Lambertian reflectance, providing a clean input for the subsequent non-Lambertian processing branch.

In addition to the decomposed physical components, the module computes high-discriminative geometric features to guide the dual-mask routing mechanism. Specifically, we calculate the angular gradient $\nabla_{\theta} I$, which captures the local rate of intensity change across the angular domain. The angular gradient is defined as a two-channel tensor:
$$ \nabla_{\theta} I(u,v) = \left[ \frac{\partial I(u,v)}{\partial u}, \frac{\partial I(u,v)}{\partial v} \right] $$
where the partial derivatives are approximated using central finite differences. For non-Lambertian regions, the angular gradient exhibits distinct bimodal distributions and X-shape patterns in the EPI space, yielding a high Cohen's $d$ statistic (e.g., $d=0.983$) between Lambertian and non-Lambertian pixels. This high discriminative power makes $\nabla_{\theta} I$ an effective indicator for pixel-level mask generation.

The outputs of the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module consist of the scalar fields $I_{dc}$, $a$, and $b$, the $9 \times 9$ residual tensor $\varepsilon(u,v)$, and the $2 \times 9 \times 9$ angular gradient tensor $\nabla_{\theta} I$. These outputs establish the data flow for the subsequent dual-branch architecture. The linear coefficients $a$ and $b$, combined with the original EPI structures, are routed to the Lambertian EPI Branch to enforce piecewise smooth depth priors. Conversely, the residual tensor $\varepsilon(u,v)$ and the angular gradient $\nabla_{\theta} I$ are directed to the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch to extract depth and material features from complex reflections. Simultaneously, the angular gradient $\nabla_{\theta} I$ is fed directly into the Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module to generate pixel-level geometric masks. By explicitly decoupling the light field signal at the frontend, this module substantially reduces the learning burden on the subsequent neural networks, enabling the dual branches to focus on their respective physical domains and ultimately improving the overall depth estimation accuracy.

## 3.3 朗伯体 EPI 分支 (Lambertian EPI Branch)

Following the explicit disentanglement of the angular signal, the Lambertian components—specifically the linear coefficients $(a, b)$ and the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) features—provide a robust foundation for depth extraction in regions adhering to the Lambertian reflectance assumption. Conventional light field depth estimation methods predominantly rely on calculating EPI slopes via structure tensors or spatial gradients <citation>. However, these approaches are highly susceptible to image noise and occlusions, and they typically decouple feature extraction from depth regularization, necessitating separate post-processing steps such as Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) to enforce piecewise smooth priors. To address these limitations, the Lambertian EPI Branch is designed to jointly extract parallax information and impose continuous depth priors within a unified differentiable framework. By leveraging the physically decomposed linear coefficients and integrating a Planar Regularized Sampling Operator (PRSO), this module extracts high-fidelity depth features for Lambertian regions while inherently preserving depth discontinuities at object boundaries.

The inputs to this module are the linear coefficients $a(x,y)$ and $b(x,y)$, and the EPI feature map $F_{epi}(x,y)$, both possessing a spatial dimension of $H \times W$. The coefficients $a$ and $b$ have a channel dimension of 1, representing the angular intensity gradients along the $u$ and $v$ axes, respectively, while $F_{epi}$ has a channel dimension of $C_{epi}$. In light field geometry, the EPI slope is intrinsically linked to scene disparity. Instead of computing the slope through noisy numerical differentiation, we utilize the decomposed linear coefficients to formulate an initial depth representation. The initial disparity feature map $D_{init} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_{d}}$ is derived via a mapping function $\Phi_{slope}$:
$$ D_{init}(x,y) = \Phi_{slope}\big(a(x,y), b(x,y), F_{epi}(x,y)\big) $$
where $\Phi_{slope}$ is implemented as a multi-layer convolutional network that fuses the physical linear coefficients with the contextual EPI features. The output channel dimension $C_{d}$ is set to encode rich disparity cues. This formulation bypasses the ill-posed gradient calculation in texture-less regions by directly exploiting the physically meaningful linear terms derived from the preceding decomposition module.

While $D_{init}$ captures the primary parallax structure, it may still contain local inconsistencies due to sensor noise or minor non-Lambertian perturbations. To enforce a piecewise smooth prior without blurring depth edges, we introduce the Planar Regularized Sampling Operator (PRSO). Unlike traditional Markov Random Field (MRF) or CRF post-processing that operates on the final scalar depth map, PRSO operates directly on the high-dimensional feature space, allowing the network to learn the optimal balance between smoothing and edge preservation. First, a local plane parameter map $\mathbf{P}(x,y) = [p_1(x,y), p_2(x,y), p_3(x,y)]^T$ is predicted from the EPI features to model the local depth geometry:
$$ \mathbf{P}(x,y) = \Psi_{plane}\big(F_{epi}(x,y)\big) $$
where $\Psi_{plane}$ is a convolutional predictor, and $p_1, p_2$ represent the spatial gradients of the local depth plane, while $p_3$ denotes the plane offset. Based on the local plane gradients, a displacement field $\mathbf{V}(x,y) = [v_x(x,y), v_y(x,y)]^T$ is constructed to guide the feature aggregation:
$$ v_x(x,y) = \alpha \cdot p_1(x,y), \quad v_y(x,y) = \alpha \cdot p_2(x,y) $$
where $\alpha$ is a learnable scaling factor that controls the displacement magnitude. The displacement field directs the sampling process towards the local planar structure, effectively aligning features along the depth surfaces.

Using the displacement field $\mathbf{V}$, the initial disparity features are resampled to aggregate contextual information along the estimated local planes. The resampled feature map $\tilde{F}_{depth}$ is computed via differentiable bilinear interpolation:
$$ \tilde{F}_{depth}(x,y) = \sum_{(i,j) \in \Omega} D_{init}(i,j) \cdot \mathcal{K}\big(x + v_x(x,y) - i, \ y + v_y(x,y) - j\big) $$
where $\Omega$ denotes the spatial neighborhood, and $\mathcal{K}$ is the bilinear interpolation kernel. To strictly enforce the piecewise smooth constraint and penalize irregular displacements, a Total Variation (TV) regularization term $\mathcal{R}_{TV}$ is applied to the displacement field during the optimization process:
$$ \mathcal{R}_{TV}(\mathbf{V}) = \sum_{x,y} \big( \|\nabla_x v_x\|_1 + \|\nabla_y v_x\|_1 + \|\nabla_x v_y\|_1 + \|\nabla_y v_y\|_1 \big) $$
This regularization encourages the displacement field to be piecewise constant, which translates to piecewise planar depth surfaces. The final Lambertian depth feature map $F_L \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_{out}}$ is then generated by fusing the resampled features with the initial features through a convolutional layer $\text{Conv}_{fuse}$:
$$ F_L(x,y) = \text{Conv}_{fuse}\big(\tilde{F}_{depth}(x,y) \oplus D_{init}(x,y)\big) $$
where $\oplus$ denotes channel-wise concatenation, and the output channel dimension $C_{out}$ is configured to match the projection dimension (e.g., $C_{out} = 96$) required by the subsequent Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module.

The Lambertian EPI Branch receives its inputs directly from the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module, ensuring that the depth extraction process is driven by physically disentangled signals rather than raw, mixed intensities. The output feature map $F_L$ is subsequently routed to the Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module, where it is integrated with the non-Lambertian features. Different from previous works that treat EPI slope estimation and depth regularization as isolated sequential steps, our module explicitly embeds the piecewise smooth physical prior into the feature extraction pipeline via the PRSO. By utilizing the displacement field derived from local plane parameters, the PRSO adaptively aggregates features along depth surfaces, yielding highly accurate and edge-sharp depth representations for Lambertian regions while maintaining end-to-end differentiability.

## 3.4 非朗伯体几何分支 (Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch)

While the Lambertian EPI branch effectively models diffuse reflections, real-world scenes inherently contain non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular highlights, glossy materials, and transparent objects. In these regions, the linear angular assumption breaks down, and the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) structures degenerate into complex geometric patterns, notably X-shape crossings and bimodal intensity distributions <citation>. Conventional light field depth estimation methods typically treat these deviations as noise or outliers, applying frequency-domain filters or robust statistical estimators that discard high-frequency material information <citation>. Different from previous works that suppress non-Lambertian artifacts, our module explicitly leverages these geometric anomalies to extract both depth and material properties. The Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch is designed to process the residual signals and angular gradients, transforming reflectance-induced distortions into informative cues for depth and material estimation, while addressing the inherent data scarcity of non-Lambertian samples through targeted augmentation.

The inputs to this branch are the residual component $\varepsilon(x,y,u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times (U \times V)}$ and the angular gradient features $\nabla_{angular} I(x,y,u,v)$, both derived from the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module. Here, $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial dimensions, and $U \times V$ represents the angular resolution (e.g., $9 \times 9$). The residual $\varepsilon$ captures the high-frequency material and specular reflections, while the angular gradient highlights the directional intensity variations. To characterize the X-shape patterns typical of specular and occluded regions, we first compute a bimodal distribution descriptor $\mathcal{B}(x,y)$. For a given spatial location $(x,y)$, the angular intensity profile is analyzed to detect dual peaks. The bimodal feature is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{B}(x,y) = \max_{k \in \{1,2\}} \left( \frac{| \mu_k - \mu_{global} |}{\sigma_k} \right) $$
where $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ are the means of the two dominant clusters in the angular profile, $\mu_{global}$ is the global angular mean, and $\sigma_k$ is the standard deviation of the $k$-th cluster. This descriptor quantifies the severity of the non-Lambertian bimodal distribution, providing a strong geometric prior for subsequent feature extraction.

To extract depth and material features from these complex patterns, we introduce a Geometric Convolution (GeoConv) operation. Unlike standard spatial convolutions that apply fixed kernels, GeoConv dynamically modulates the residual features using the angular gradients and the bimodal descriptor. The core operation is defined as:
$$ F_{NL}(x,y) = \text{GeoConv}(\varepsilon, \nabla_{angular} I) = \sum_{u=1}^{U} \sum_{v=1}^{V} \varepsilon(x,y,u,v) \cdot \mathcal{W}\left( \nabla_{angular} I(x,y,u,v), \mathcal{B}(x,y) \right) $$
where $F_{NL}(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{NL}}$ is the output non-Lambertian feature vector at spatial location $(x,y)$, and $C_{NL}$ is the channel dimension. The function $\mathcal{W}(\cdot)$ represents a learnable geometric attention kernel that maps the angular gradient and bimodal descriptor to a set of spatial-angular weights. By explicitly weighting the residual $\varepsilon$ with $\mathcal{W}$, the network learns to associate specific X-shape geometric structures and gradient orientations with corresponding depth disparities and material types, rather than relying on frequency-domain heuristics.

A significant challenge in training the non-Lambertian branch is the severe class imbalance and data scarcity of specular and transparent regions in standard light field datasets. To prevent the branch from overfitting to the limited non-Lambertian samples, we integrate a Random Tone-mapping Augmentation (RTA) strategy directly into the feature extraction pipeline. During training, the residual input is perturbed using a stochastic tone-mapping function to synthesize diverse reflectance variations:
$$ \tilde{\varepsilon}(x,y,u,v) = \text{RTA}(\varepsilon(x,y,u,v)) = \left( \frac{\varepsilon(x,y,u,v)}{\varepsilon(x,y,u,v) + \gamma} \right)^\beta $$
where $\gamma \sim \mathcal{U}(\gamma_{min}, \gamma_{max})$ controls the exposure level and $\beta \sim \mathcal{U}(\beta_{min}, \beta_{max})$ adjusts the contrast. The augmented residual $\tilde{\varepsilon}$ is then fed into the GeoConv operation. This physics-inspired augmentation expands the distribution of non-Lambertian residuals, enabling the model to generalize across varying illumination conditions and material properties without requiring additional annotated data.

The final output of the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch is the feature map $F_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_{NL}}$, which encapsulates the depth and material characteristics of non-Lambertian regions. This feature map, alongside the Lambertian depth features from the preceding branch, is forwarded to the Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module. By explicitly decoupling the processing of diffuse and specular reflections, the proposed architecture ensures that the geometric distortions caused by non-Lambertian materials are accurately interpreted as physical properties rather than estimation errors. This design substantially improves depth accuracy at object boundaries and highly reflective surfaces, establishing a robust foundation for the subsequent mask-guided fusion.

## 3.5 双掩码路由与融合模块 (Dual-Mask Routing & Fusion)

While the Lambertian EPI branch and the Non-Lambertian Geometric branch extract complementary depth cues from diffuse and specular regions, respectively, integrating these dual-branch representations remains a significant challenge. Conventional light field depth estimation frameworks typically rely on scene-level binary cross-entropy (BCE) classification or simple feature concatenation to merge multi-branch outputs <citation>. However, real-world scenes exhibit pixel-level interleaving of Lambertian and non-Lambertian materials. Scene-level routing inevitably introduces boundary artifacts and depth discontinuities at material transitions, whereas naive concatenation forces the network to implicitly learn complex material-aware weighting without explicit physical guidance. To address this limitation, we design the Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module, which performs pixel-wise adaptive routing guided by physically meaningful geometric priors, ensuring continuous depth transitions across heterogeneous material boundaries.

The module receives three distinct inputs: the Lambertian feature map $F_L \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_L}$ from the Lambertian EPI branch, the non-Lambertian feature map $F_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_{NL}}$ from the Non-Lambertian Geometric branch, and the pixel-wise angular gradient pseudo-labels $G_{ang} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_G}$ derived from the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module. Here, $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial height and width, while $C_L$, $C_{NL}$, and $C_G$ represent the respective channel dimensions. Since the two branches employ different structural priors, their output channel dimensions and feature distributions are inherently misaligned. To harmonize these representations, we first project both branch features into a unified latent space with a balanced channel dimension $D_{fuse} = 96$ using $1 \times 1$ convolutional layers:
$$ \hat{F}_L = \text{ReLU}(W_L * F_L + b_L) $$
$$ \hat{F}_{NL} = \text{ReLU}(W_{NL} * F_{NL} + b_{NL}) $$
where $*$ denotes the convolution operation, $W_L \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 1 \times C_L \times D_{fuse}}$ and $W_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 1 \times C_{NL} \times D_{fuse}}$ are the learnable projection weights, and $b_L, b_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{fuse}}$ are the bias terms. The projected features $\hat{F}_L, \hat{F}_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times D_{fuse}}$ share identical dimensionalities, facilitating subsequent pixel-wise operations.

The distinctive feature of this module lies in the generation of the domain mask. Unlike previous works that employ data-driven spatial attention mechanisms or scene-level classifiers to distinguish reflection types <citation>, our approach leverages the angular gradient $G_{ang}$ as a high-discriminative physical prior. Statistical analysis of light field datasets reveals that the angular gradient exhibits a substantial separation between Lambertian and non-Lambertian pixels, yielding a Cohen's d effect size of 0.983 <citation>. This strong physical distinction allows us to formulate a reliable pixel-wise mask without relying on computationally expensive self-attention or ambiguous scene-level labels. The domain mask $M \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$ is computed via a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) followed by a sigmoid activation:
$$ M = \sigma(\text{MLP}(G_{ang})) $$
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the sigmoid function mapping the output to the range $[0, 1]$, and the MLP consists of two fully connected layers with a hidden dimension of 64. The resulting mask $M$ acts as a continuous pixel-wise routing weight, where values approaching 1 indicate dominant Lambertian properties, and values approaching 0 signify non-Lambertian characteristics.

With the unified features and the physically guided domain mask, the final depth representation is synthesized through a mask-weighted fusion strategy. This operation selectively aggregates the complementary depth cues while suppressing branch-specific noise in irrelevant regions:
$$ F_{final} = M \odot \hat{F}_L + (1 - M) \odot \hat{F}_{NL} $$
where $\odot$ represents the Hadamard (element-wise) product broadcasted across the channel dimension. The fused feature map $F_{final} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times D_{fuse}}$ encapsulates a comprehensive scene representation that respects local material properties. Finally, the high-precision depth map $D_{final} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$ is regressed using a concluding convolutional layer:
$$ D_{final} = W_{out} * F_{final} + b_{out} $$
where $W_{out} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 1 \times D_{fuse} \times 1}$ and $b_{out} \in \mathbb{R}^1$ are the parameters of the output projection. The module concurrently outputs the domain mask $M$, which serves as an auxiliary material and reflectance segmentation map, providing interpretability for downstream tasks.

Topologically, this module serves as the convergence point of the proposed dual-branch architecture, bridging the physical signal decomposition, the parallel feature extraction, and the final depth regression. By replacing heuristic feature concatenation with a physics-informed pixel-wise routing mechanism, the Dual-Mask Routing and Fusion module mitigates the depth bleeding artifacts commonly observed at material boundaries in existing light field depth estimation methods. The explicit utilization of the high-discriminative angular gradient ensures that the fusion process is strictly constrained by the underlying optical physics, yielding robust depth estimates across complex, mixed-reflectance scenes.

## 3.6 Training Objective

### 3.6 Training Objective and Loss Function

**Overview**
The training objective of the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model is formulated to jointly optimize depth estimation and material-aware domain routing. Unlike previous works that treat light field depth estimation as a pure regression problem, our formulation explicitly incorporates the physical properties of light transport and the geometric characteristics of Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs). The overall objective function comprises three components: a mask-weighted depth estimation loss to handle region-specific physical violations, a focal domain classification loss to address severe pixel-level class imbalance, and a physical decomposition regularization to enforce feature disentanglement based on the three-layer angular signal model.

**Mask-Weighted Depth Estimation Loss**
Standard $L_1$ or $L_2$ losses assume uniform reliability across all spatial regions. However, in non-Lambertian areas (e.g., specular highlights, translucent surfaces), the fundamental EPI line structure is physically violated, leading to ambiguous slope estimations. Direct optimization over these regions generates noisy gradients that degrade overall depth accuracy. To mitigate this, we employ a mask-weighted depth loss that adaptively adjusts the penalty based on the predicted material soft masks, $\mathcal{M}_L$ (Lambertian) and $\mathcal{M}_{NL}$ (non-Lambertian):
$$ \mathcal{L}_{depth} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left( \mathcal{M}_L(p) \| d(p) - \hat{d}(p) \|_1 + \gamma \mathcal{M}_{NL}(p) \| d(p) - \hat{d}(p) \|_{\text{Huber}} \right) $$
where $\Omega$ denotes the spatial domain, $p$ is the pixel index, $d$ and $\hat{d}$ are the ground-truth and predicted depth maps, and $\gamma$ is a scaling factor. The Huber loss is utilized for non-Lambertian regions to reduce the sensitivity to large outliers caused by EPI slope ambiguity. This design ensures that the network prioritizes geometrically consistent Lambertian regions while maintaining robust, outlier-resistant optimization in physically complex non-Lambertian regions.

**Focal Domain Classification Loss**
The dual-mask routing mechanism relies on accurate pixel-level material classification. In typical light field scenes, non-Lambertian pixels constitute a minor fraction, resulting in severe class imbalance. Standard cross-entropy loss yields biased gradients that favor the majority Lambertian class, impairing mask generation for minority regions. We leverage the Focal Loss to guide the domain classification branch, forcing the network to focus on hard-to-classify boundary pixels and sparse non-Lambertian regions:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{cls} = - \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left( y(p) (1 - \hat{y}(p))^\eta \log(\hat{y}(p)) + (1 - y(p)) \hat{y}(p)^\eta \log(1 - \hat{y}(p)) \right) $$
where $y(p) \in \{0, 1\}$ is the ground-truth material label (1 for non-Lambertian), $\hat{y}(p)$ is the predicted probability, and $\eta$ is the focusing parameter that down-weights well-classified examples. By dynamically scaling the loss based on prediction confidence, this auxiliary task provides stable gradient signals for minority material classes, enhancing the precision of the dual-mask routing.

**Physical Decomposition Regularization**
The proposed three-layer angular signal decomposition model asserts that the angular signal $I(u,v)$ can be decoupled into a DC component, a linear component representing depth (EPI slope), and a residual component $\epsilon(u,v)$ representing material reflectance. To ensure the network learns physically meaningful and disentangled representations, a regularization term derived from this physical prior is required. Based on the physical decomposition, the residual $\epsilon(u,v)$ should theoretically approach zero in purely Lambertian regions. Conversely, in non-Lambertian regions, the magnitude of $\epsilon$ indicates the degree of physical violation of the EPI assumption. We formulate the physical consistency regularization $\mathcal{L}_{reg}$ as follows:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{reg} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left( \mathcal{M}_L(p) \| \epsilon(p) \|_2^2 + \mathcal{M}_{NL}(p) \| \nabla \hat{d}(p) \odot \exp(-\beta \epsilon(p)) \|_1 \right) $$
where $\epsilon(p)$ is the residual feature map extracted by the material branch, $\nabla \hat{d}(p)$ denotes the spatial gradient of the predicted depth, $\odot$ is the element-wise multiplication, and $\beta$ is a temperature parameter. The first term enforces the physical prior that the residual reflectance component must be minimized in Lambertian regions ($\mathcal{M}_L \approx 1$). The second term acts as an adaptive edge-aware smoothness constraint for the depth map. In non-Lambertian regions ($\mathcal{M}_{NL} \approx 1$), the EPI slope is inherently ambiguous. By weighting the depth gradient $\nabla \hat{d}$ with $\exp(-\beta \epsilon)$, the penalty on depth discontinuities is relaxed where the material residual $\epsilon$ is large. This prevents the network from over-smoothing depth boundaries at physically complex material transitions, explicitly aligning the learned feature spaces with the physical light transport model.

**Overall Objective and Optimization Strategy**
The total training objective is a linear combination of the aforementioned components:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{depth} \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_{cls} \mathcal{L}_{cls} + \lambda_{reg} \mathcal{L}_{reg} $$
The weights are empirically set to $\lambda_{depth} = 1.0$, $\lambda_{cls} = 0.1$, and $\lambda_{reg} = 0.05$. The primary depth regression dominates the optimization, while the auxiliary classification and physical regularization provide necessary structural guidance without overwhelming the main task. 

To address the severe data scarcity in the non-Lambertian domain (where only 4 training scenes are available), we integrate a domain-balanced sampling strategy at the data loading stage. A `WeightedRandomSampler` is employed to maintain an equal exposure probability across the Lambertian (HCInew), Mixed/Urban (UrbanLF-Syn), and Non-Lambertian domains during each epoch. The model is optimized using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of $10^{-4}$, which is decayed using a cosine annealing schedule. This joint optimization of the composite loss and balanced sampling ensures stable convergence and enhances cross-domain generalization, effectively mitigating the hard data barrier imposed by the scarce non-Lambertian samples.
