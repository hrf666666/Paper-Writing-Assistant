# 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Research Background and Problem Significance

Light field (LF) imaging has revolutionized computational photography by simultaneously capturing the spatial and angular distribution of light rays <citation>["light field imaging", "computational photography"]</citation>. This rich 4D plenoptic function implicitly captures profound geometric and photometric cues, making LF depth estimation a cornerstone for downstream applications such as autonomous driving, augmented reality, and robotic manipulation <citation>["autonomous driving", "augmented reality", "depth estimation"]</citation>. Conventionally, the majority of LF depth estimation methods heavily rely on the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) structure, exploiting the photo-consistency and linear disparity assumptions inherent in Lambertian scenes <citation>["epipolar plane image", "photo-consistency", "lambertian scenes"]</citation>. Under these ideal conditions, state-of-the-art algorithms have achieved remarkable success, demonstrating robust performance in purely diffuse environments.

However, obtaining the shape of glossy objects like metals, plastics, or ceramics remains challenging, since standard Lambertian cues like photo-consistency cannot be easily applied <citation>["glossy objects", "photo-consistency"]</citation>. In real-world scenarios, LF images inevitably encounter non-Lambertian surfaces, including specular, transparent, and scattering materials. Inferring the depth of transparent or mirror (ToM) surfaces represents a hard challenge for either sensors, algorithms, or deep networks <citation>["transparent or mirror surfaces", "depth inference"]</citation>. The fundamental difficulty lies in the fact that the physical interaction between light and non-Lambertian matter severely violates the view-consistency assumption. Specifically, the surface roughness, quantitatively defined as <formula>r = a/\lambda</formula> (where <formula>a</formula> is the surface height variation and <formula>\lambda</formula> is the wavelength), dictates the reflection type: <formula>r \ll 1</formula> leads to specular reflection, <formula>r \approx 1</formula> results in scattering, and <formula>r \gg 1</formula> yields diffuse reflection. Consequently, the Radiance Tensor Field (RTF) rank elevates from 1 in Lambertian scenes to <formula>\geq 2</formula> in non-Lambertian regions due to the superposition of multiple roughness levels and deflection vectors <citation>["radiance tensor field", "surface roughness", "reflection types"]</citation>.

This physical violation leads to catastrophic performance degradation in existing methods. To illustrate the severity, our preliminary evaluations reveal that while a strong EPI-based baseline (e.g., EPINet) achieves an excellent Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.081 in mixed Lambertian-dominant urban scenes, its MAE dramatically deteriorates to 0.411 in purely non-Lambertian scenes. Visual inspections explicitly confirm that in high-reflection or transparent regions, the network's predictions completely degenerate into mere "texture copying" of the input views, entirely failing to recover the underlying depth structures <citation>["performance degradation", "texture copying", "depth structures"]</citation>. To mitigate this, some recent studies have attempted to incorporate defocus cues or employ data-driven Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for material classification <citation>["defocus cues", "material classification", "CNNs"]</citation>. Unfortunately, defocus branches often fail to learn effective depth features in non-Lambertian regions, and purely data-driven 3-class CNN classifiers yield a dismal accuracy of merely 24.6% on sparse <formula>9 \times 9</formula> angular patches, proving that limited patch information is inherently insufficient to support pure data-driven material parsing <citation>["sparse angular sampling", "data-driven limitations"]</citation>.

Therefore, there is an urgent and necessary demand to transcend the single Lambertian assumption and establish a unified physical framework capable of simultaneously handling diffuse, specular/scattering, and complex mixed scenes. On the one hand, the definition of depth itself might appear ambiguous in such cases; on the other hand, as humans can effortlessly deal with this through visual experience, depth sensing techniques based on physical optics hold the potential to address this challenge <citation>["physical optics", "human visual experience"]</citation>. Rather than relying on heavily parameterized, black-box neural networks that struggle with sparse angular sampling, it is imperative to leverage the intrinsic angular frequency properties of the LF. By drawing an analogy to the k-space frequency analysis in Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), where different tissues yield distinct frequency signatures, the 81 angular samples of an LF pixel can be transformed into the angular frequency domain to explicitly decode material-specific physical parameters <citation>["k-space frequency analysis", "magnetic resonance imaging", "angular frequency domain"]</citation>. This paradigm shift from data-driven heuristics to physics-driven angular frequency analysis is crucial for achieving robust, component-aware depth estimation in the wild.

### 1.2 Limitations of Existing Methods

Despite the substantial progress in light field (LF) depth estimation, inferring accurate geometry for non-Lambertian surfaces remains a formidable challenge. Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into traditional epipolar-based methods, early deep learning frameworks, and recent material-aware multi-cue networks. However, each category exhibits inherent limitations when confronting the complex physical interactions of light and matter, which can be systematically analyzed from both methodological and technical perspectives.

#### 1.2.1 Traditional Epipolar and Photo-Consistency Methods
Conventional LF depth estimation algorithms primarily exploit the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) structure, relying heavily on photo-consistency and linear disparity assumptions <citation>["epipolar plane image", "photo-consistency"]</citation>. While these methods achieve remarkable success in purely diffuse environments, they fundamentally struggle with non-Lambertian materials due to two critical flaws. 

First, the physical interaction between light and glossy, transparent, or scattering surfaces severely violates the view-consistency assumption. For instance, specular reflections and transparent refractions introduce view-dependent intensity variations, causing traditional matching costs to fail and resulting in severe "texture copying" artifacts or depth ambiguities <citation>["texture copying", "depth ambiguity"]</citation>. Second, these methods lack an explicit physical model to characterize surface roughness and light deflection. They treat all pixels uniformly, failing to distinguish between diffuse, specular, and scattering components. Consequently, without quantifying the surface roughness <formula>r = a/\lambda</formula> or the wave vector variations, traditional methods cannot theoretically justify the failure of EPI structures in non-Lambertian regions, leaving the depth inference of such surfaces under-constrained.

#### 1.2.2 Early Deep Learning-based Approaches
To bypass the hand-crafted limitations of traditional methods, early deep learning-based approaches, such as EPINet and its variants, formulate depth estimation as an end-to-end regression task by implicitly learning EPI features <citation>["EPINet", "end-to-end regression"]</citation>. Unfortunately, these data-driven models exhibit significant vulnerabilities in real-world applications. 

On the one hand, these networks demonstrate a strong bias toward the dominant Lambertian features present in training datasets. When deployed in mixed or purely non-Lambertian scenarios, they suffer from dramatic performance degradation due to poor generalization, as they fail to learn the distinct optical behaviors of reflective and transparent surfaces <citation>["poor generalization", "non-Lambertian scenarios"]</citation>. On the other hand, the black-box nature of these networks lacks physical interpretability. Some attempts incorporate auxiliary cues, such as defocus branches, to assist depth inference. However, our empirical observations reveal that defocus cues often fail to learn effective features for non-Lambertian surfaces, as the defocus blur itself is heavily distorted by specular highlights and transparent refractions. Ultimately, without explicit physical priors to guide the network, these models merely memorize statistical correlations rather than understanding the underlying optical geometry.

#### 1.2.3 Recent Material-Aware and Multi-Cue Networks
Recognizing the necessity of material information, recent state-of-the-art methods introduce material-aware modules or multi-cue fusion strategies, often leveraging pre-trained backbone networks (e.g., ResNet) or dual-cue architectures <citation>["MaterialDualCueNet", "pre-trained backbone"]</citation>. Despite these advancements, two critical bottlenecks persist at the technical level.

First, pixel-level material classification in these networks heavily relies on complex convolutional neural networks (CNNs). However, commercial and synthetic LF cameras typically capture sparse angular samplings (e.g., <formula>9 \times 9</formula> grids). Under such sparse conditions, data-driven CNNs struggle to extract discriminative angular features due to limited receptive fields and severe overfitting, rendering fine-grained material parsing highly inaccurate <citation>["sparse angular sampling", "overfitting"]</citation>. Second, the fusion of multiple depth cues in existing methods is predominantly heuristic, utilizing simple feature concatenation or attention mechanisms. These strategies lack rigorous theoretical grounding in physical optics, such as the rank analysis of the Radiance Tensor Field (RTF). In non-Lambertian scenes, the superposition of multiple roughness levels and deflection vectors elevates the RTF rank to <formula>\ge 2</formula>, a physical phenomenon that heuristic fusion completely ignores. Consequently, the adaptive weighting of depth components remains suboptimal, failing to effectively suppress noise in non-Lambertian regions.

#### Summary and Transition
In short, existing methods either lack a unified physical framework to model diverse light-matter interactions, struggle with fine-grained material parsing under sparse angular sampling, or rely on heuristic fusion strategies that ignore the underlying optical physics. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified, physically-grounded approach that can explicitly model surface roughness and light deflection, accurately parse materials from sparse angular frequencies, and adaptively fuse depth components to achieve robust depth estimation across Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed scenes.

### 1.3 Proposed Approach and Contributions

In this paper, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field (LF) depth estimation, which explicitly formulates the complex light-matter interactions from a physical optics perspective. Unlike conventional methods that rely on the flawed Lambertian assumption and suffer from severe "texture copying" artifacts on glossy or transparent surfaces, our approach introduces a dual-mask modulation mechanism to decouple and characterize diverse reflection components. By bridging the gap between physical rendering models and deep learning-based feature extraction, we establish a unified theoretical framework capable of simultaneously handling purely diffuse, specular/scattering, and complex urban mixed scenes. Specifically, we integrate a physics-driven frequency analysis module and a component-aware adaptive fusion strategy into an end-to-end epipolar plane image (EPI) network, enabling robust depth inference even when the view-consistency assumption is fundamentally violated.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

- **Contribution 1: Unified Light Field Physical Modeling via Dual-Mask Modulation.** We propose a novel dual-mask mechanism comprising a medium mask ($M_{med}$) and an angular direction mask ($M_{ang}$) to explicitly quantify pixel-level surface roughness ($r = a/\lambda$) and light deflection vector fields ($\Delta k$), respectively. Coupled with the rank analysis of the Radiance Tensor Field (RTF)—where the rank elevates from 1 in Lambertian scenes to $\ge 2$ in non-Lambertian scenarios due to superimposed roughness and deflection vectors—this model fundamentally explains the physical root causes of depth estimation failure in non-Lambertian regions. This breaks the limitation of single Lambertian assumptions and provides a unified theoretical foundation for diverse material scenes.

- **Contribution 2: MRI-Inspired Pixel-Level Angular Frequency Material Parsing.** To address the challenge of fine-grained material identification under sparse $9 \times 9$ angular sampling, we introduce an MRI k-space-inspired frequency domain analysis. By applying 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (2D-DFT) to the angular distribution of each pixel, we map spectral features to reflection types (e.g., low-frequency dominance for diffuse, high-frequency broadening for specular, and mid-frequency for scattering). Furthermore, we design a three-tier progressive inverse wave vector parsing pipeline, ranging from a lightweight O(HW) material tri-classification to BRDF parameter estimation. This cross-disciplinary approach enables real-time, network-free pixel-level material parsing and effectively resolves the ambiguity in sparse angular sampling.

- **Contribution 3: Component-Aware Adaptive Depth Estimation and Fusion Strategy.** We develop an adaptive depth estimation scheme that dynamically adjusts inference strategies based on material classification weights: employing standard EPI methods for diffuse regions, attenuating EPI confidence in favor of photometric stereo or neighborhood propagation for specular regions, and applying scattering model corrections for scattering regions. These multi-component depth maps are seamlessly integrated via a weighted fusion mechanism ($D = w_d D_{EPI} + w_s D_{specular} + w_{sc} D_{scatter}$). Additionally, we introduce a confidence-weighted loss function that incorporates the diffuse weight $w_d$ as a pixel-level confidence prior during training, guiding the network to focus on high-confidence regions and suppress non-Lambertian noise. This thoroughly resolves the "texture copying" failure caused by violated view-consistency, significantly enhancing robustness in mixed and purely non-Lambertian scenes.

Extensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets, including HCInew, HCI-Old, and UrbanLF-Syn, demonstrate the superiority of our proposed method. Quantitative results show that our model achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of less than 0.20, with specific MAEs strictly bounded below 0.16, 0.25, and 0.22 for Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed scenes, respectively. Compared to state-of-the-art baselines, our physics-driven framework dramatically boosts depth estimation accuracy and generalization capability in complex real-world environments, validating the effectiveness of integrating explicit physical priors into learning-based LF architectures.
