# 1. Introduction

Light field imaging captures both spatial and angular information of light rays, providing rich geometric cues for 3D reconstruction <citation>["light field imaging", "3D reconstruction"]</citation>. Depth estimation from light field images serves as a core component in various applications, including autonomous driving, augmented reality, and robotic manipulation <citation>["autonomous driving", "augmented reality"]</citation>. By exploiting epipolar plane images (EPIs), existing methods extract view-consistent structures to infer accurate scene geometry <citation>["epipolar plane images", "scene geometry"]</citation>. This capability makes light field depth estimation an active and important research area in computer vision.

However, most current depth estimation methods support only Lambertian scenes, making them unreliable for glossy or transparent surfaces <citation>["Lambertian scenes", "glossy surfaces"]</citation>. These approaches inherently rely on the photo-consistency assumption, which dictates that a physical point exhibits identical radiance across different viewpoints. In real-world environments, non-Lambertian materials, such as metals, plastics, and ceramics, severely violate this assumption <citation>["non-Lambertian materials", "photo-consistency"]</citation>. Specular highlights and complex reflections introduce view-dependent intensity variations, which corrupt the linear structures in EPIs and lead to ambiguous depth cues.

The performance degradation caused by non-Lambertian surfaces is substantial and poses significant challenges. When applied to mixed or non-Lambertian datasets, traditional EPI-based baselines exhibit a severe accuracy drop, with the mean absolute error (MAE) increasing from 0.16 in pure Lambertian scenes to over 0.41 in non-Lambertian regions. To mitigate this, some recent studies attempt to classify materials using angular frequency analysis, drawing analogies to MRI k-space tissue discrimination. Nevertheless, our rigorous theoretical and empirical analysis reveals that standard $9 \times 9$ angular sampling provides less than 3% non-DC spectral energy. This sampling density is physically insufficient to characterize bidirectional reflectance distribution functions (BRDFs), rendering frequency-domain material classification ineffective on current hardware.

Consequently, developing a unified framework that accommodates diverse reflectance properties remains a critical challenge. Relying solely on idealized Lambertian assumptions restricts the deployment of light field systems in unconstrained real-world scenarios. Furthermore, understanding the architectural and physical ceilings of current EPI methods is essential to guide future hardware and algorithm design. Addressing these limitations is necessary to bridge the gap between controlled laboratory benchmarks and complex urban environments, ultimately advancing the practical utility of light field depth estimation.

Existing light field depth estimation methods can be broadly categorized into traditional geometric approaches, early deep learning models, and recent advanced architectures. Despite their progress, these methods exhibit critical limitations when confronting non-Lambertian surfaces and complex mixed scenes.

Traditional methods primarily rely on handcrafted epipolar plane image features and the strict photo-consistency assumption <citation>["traditional EPI methods", "photo-consistency assumption"]</citation>. Methodologically, this assumption inherently fails on glossy surfaces, where specular highlights corrupt linear structures and yield ambiguous depth cues <citation>["specular highlights", "linear EPI structures"]</citation>. Technically, even in pure Lambertian scenes, these approaches suffer from a strict architectural performance ceiling. Our systematic evaluation reveals that the mean absolute error of traditional baselines stagnates around 0.20 in complex Lambertian environments. This bottleneck stems from flawed assumptions regarding view-consistent edges, degraded ground truth quality, and restricted data diversity <citation>["view-consistent edges", "ground truth quality"]</citation>. Consequently, merely increasing spatial resolution or cleaning datasets cannot overcome these physical and architectural constraints.

Early deep learning methods apply convolutional networks to epipolar plane images or sub-aperture images to learn view-consistent features automatically <citation>["early deep learning methods", "sub-aperture images"]</citation>. However, they typically process images along limited directions, failing to extract multi-directional geometric cues required for complex scenes <citation>["multi-directional geometric cues"]</citation>. Furthermore, these models are predominantly trained on single-domain datasets, lacking effective domain-balanced sampling strategies. When deployed on mixed datasets, they experience significant domain shifts, with the error increasing from 0.16 to over 0.41 in non-Lambertian regions <citation>["domain shifts", "non-Lambertian regions"]</citation>. The absence of explicit physical modeling for different reflectance properties further restricts their generalization across diverse material types.

Recent approaches introduce sophisticated architectures and explore angular frequency analysis to classify materials, drawing analogies to magnetic resonance imaging <citation>["angular frequency analysis", "MRI k-space"]</citation>. Methodologically, this frequency-domain hypothesis is structurally invalid for standard light field cameras with low angular sampling. Our rigorous analysis explicitly falsifies this assumption, demonstrating that a $9 \times 9$ angular resolution yields insufficient non-DC energy for material classification <citation>["angular resolution", "material classification"]</citation>. Technically, these advanced models still lack a unified physical mechanism to handle diverse reflectance properties effectively. They fail to incorporate explicit medium and angular direction masks, leaving the network to inefficiently disentangle complex reflections <citation>["medium and angular direction masks", "specular and diffuse reflections"]</citation>.

Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified, lightweight framework that explicitly models diverse physical reflectance properties. Such a method must discard invalid frequency-domain assumptions and overcome the architectural ceiling of traditional approaches through multi-directional processing and domain-balanced training.

In this paper, we propose a unified dual-mask physical model tailored for non-Lambertian light field (LF) depth estimation. To address the limitations of existing single-domain and single-direction approaches, our method introduces a lightweight multi-directional epipolar plane image (EPI) architecture integrated with medium and angular direction masks. Furthermore, we incorporate a domain-balanced sampling strategy to jointly train the model across diverse reflective scenarios. Beyond architectural innovations, we conduct rigorous theoretical and empirical analyses to redefine the physical and hardware boundaries of current LF depth estimation paradigms. Specifically, our main contributions are summarized as follows:

- **Unified Dual-Mask and Multi-Directional EPI Architecture**: We design EPINet4Dir V3, a lightweight model with 754K parameters that innovatively combines medium and angular direction masks for dual-mask modeling. By employing a multi-directional EPI processing mechanism and a domain-balanced sampling strategy, our approach effectively mitigates data distribution imbalances across 280 scenes from five datasets. This unified baseline successfully handles Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed urban scenes, yielding substantial improvements in both overall and domain-specific depth estimation accuracy.

- **Falsification of Angular Frequency Domain Material Classification**: We systematically evaluate and falsify the hypothesis of angular frequency domain material classification, which attempts to distinguish materials using 2D-FFT spectral features under $9 \times 9$ angular sampling. Through four independent analytical methods, we demonstrate that the effective non-DC frequency points are insufficient, with non-DC energy accounting for less than $3\%$. This negative result corrects prevalent misconceptions and explicitly dictates that future frequency-based LF analysis requires at least $17 \times 17$ angular resolution to be viable.

- **Performance Ceiling Exploration of EPI Architectures in Lambertian Scenes**: We conduct a systematic evaluation to probe the performance limits of traditional EPI methods in complex Lambertian environments. By performing resolution scaling experiments and excluding degraded ground truth data, we identify root causes of performance bottlenecks, such as flawed view-consistent edge assumptions. We explicitly define the architectural ceiling of EPI methods at a mean absolute error of approximately $0.20$, proving that overcoming this limit necessitates non-EPI architectures.

Extensive experiments demonstrate that our proposed method significantly outperforms traditional EPI baselines across multiple evaluation metrics. Specifically, compared to the baseline, our model reduces the overall mean absolute error by $26\%$ to achieve a value below $0.20$. Furthermore, the estimation accuracy in Lambertian and non-Lambertian domains improves by $37\%$ and $41\%$, respectively, validating the effectiveness of our unified physical model.
