# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Traditional Light Field Depth Estimation Methods

Many depth estimation methods for light-field cameras have been proposed, **primarily** leveraging the geometric constraints embedded in epipolar plane images (EPIs), which **emerge as** a fundamental representation for 3D reconstruction <citation>["epipolar plane images", "geometric constraints", "light field depth estimation"]</citation>. Wanner et al. <citation>["Wanner", "structure tensor", "globally consistent depth labeling"]</citation> **propose** a globally consistent depth labeling framework that **derive** local depth hypotheses from the structure tensor of EPIs and subsequently refine them using Markov Random Field (MRF) optimization. This approach **successfully** **demonstrate** that the orientation of linear structures in EPIs is **explicitly** inversely proportional to scene depth, **accurately** estimating depth and achieving **remarkably** high performance on standard Lambertian datasets. **However**, these EPI-based methods **mostly** rely on the photo-consistency assumption and work poorly on non-Lambertian surfaces. **Specifically**, specular highlights severely **violate** this assumption, disrupting the continuous linear trajectories in EPIs and leading to erroneous orientation estimations. Furthermore, as our theoretical analysis reveals, traditional EPI architectures inherently **encounter** a performance ceiling (e.g., Mean Absolute Error $\approx 0.20$) in complex Lambertian scenes, indicating that merely refining EPI line extraction is insufficient for breaking through physical and architectural limits.

**While** EPI-based methods focus predominantly on angular line structures, other approaches extend the geometric cues by combining defocus and correspondence metrics to improve depth resolution. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "defocus and correspondence", "light-field cameras"]</citation> **propose** a unified depth estimation algorithm that **leverage** both defocus blur and multi-view correspondence, **significantly** enhancing depth accuracy in textureless regions where EPI gradients are **typically** ambiguous. By formulating depth inference as an energy minimization problem, they effectively **address** the scale ambiguity inherent in single-cue methods, and **validate** their effectiveness **extensively** on synthetic datasets. **Nonetheless**, the optimization process in such correspondence-defocus frameworks **exhibit** extremely high computational complexity. The dense evaluation of focus metrics across all angular views makes these methods computationally prohibitive, and they **often** struggle to scale to large-scale, high-resolution modern light-field datasets, lacking practical engineering value. **Moreover**, these multi-cue fusion strategies **usually** assume a uniform reflectance model, making them highly sensitive to the view-dependent intensity variations caused by mixed urban or glossy scenes.

To **address** the violations of the Lambertian assumption, early attempts have explored physical priors and heuristic filtering to handle non-Lambertian reflections. For instance, Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "clustering method", "specular pixels"]</citation> **propose** a clustering method that eliminates specular pixels by separating them from the diffuse components before depth estimation. **On the one hand**, this heuristic **successfully** recovers depth for simple glossy objects. **On the other hand**, they attempt a binary classification of pixels into purely diffuse or purely specular categories. This rigid dichotomy **generally** fails to handle glossy surfaces with complex, mixed bidirectional reflectance distribution functions (BRDFs), where specular and diffuse lobes heavily overlap in the angular domain. Similarly, other physics-based methods <citation>["physics-based", "specular removal", "geometric optics"]</citation> rely on strict geometric optics assumptions to model specularities, which **dramatically** degrades when confronted with real-world mixed materials, and require researchers to **fine-tune** them extensively for different material priors. These isolated treatments highlight a critical gap: existing methods lack a unified processing framework to efficiently handle both Lambertian and non-Lambertian multi-domain mixed data within a single model.

**In short**, traditional light-field depth estimation methods suffer from three fundamental limitations. First, they highly depend on hand-crafted features and strict physical assumptions, resulting in extremely poor robustness and severe depth artifacts when applied to complex non-Lambertian and mixed (e.g., urban) scenes. Second, their optimization processes entail prohibitively high computational complexity, preventing extension to modern high-resolution datasets and lacking practical engineering value. Third, they lack a unified processing framework capable of simultaneously and efficiently processing Lambertian and non-Lambertian multi-domain mixed data within a single model, failing to cope with the complex reflection characteristics of the real world. **Therefore**, **in contrast** to these fragmented and computationally heavy approaches, our work introduces a unified dual-mask physical model. Rather than relying on flawed binary clustering or computationally expensive global optimization, we establish a lightweight, unified baseline leveraging multi-directional EPIs and domain-balanced training. Furthermore, we **explicitly** falsify the community's assumption regarding frequency-domain material classification at low angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$), establishing a strict resolution boundary (requiring $\ge 17 \times 17$) and probing the architectural ceiling of pure EPI methods, thereby providing clear theoretical and engineering guidance for future light-field analysis.

## 2.2 Deep Learning-based General Light Field Depth Estimation

Deep learning techniques have dramatically advanced light field depth estimation, which has emerged as a core component in 3D reconstruction. Early convolutional neural network (CNN) based methods primarily focus on fusing spatial and angular information. For instance, Shin et al. <citation>["Shin", "EPINET", "epipolar plane images", "convolutional neural networks"]</citation> propose EPINET, a pioneering architecture that explicitly combines a central sub-aperture image with multiple EPIs. The core idea is to leverage the central view for rich spatial textures while utilizing EPIs to derive geometric line structures. By feeding these multi-view features into a multi-stream CNN, EPINET successfully demonstrates remarkable accuracy on standard Lambertian datasets, significantly outperforming traditional hand-crafted descriptors. However, this approach mostly relies on local convolutional operations, which inherently limits the receptive field and struggles to capture long-range view consistency in scenes with large disparities.

While Shin et al. focus on local EPI structures, subsequent works extend the modeling capacity by introducing attention mechanisms and exploring alternative light field representations. Wang et al. <citation>["Wang", "parallax attention", "sub-aperture", "macropixel"]</citation> propose a parallax attention framework that processes both macropixel and sub-aperture images to address occlusion and weak-texture challenges. Specifically, they design an attention module to establish long-distance dependencies across different viewpoints, allowing the network to dynamically weight reliable correspondences and suppress occluded regions. This strategy accurately recovers depth in complex Lambertian scenes with severe occlusions, and the effectiveness of such attention mechanisms has been extensively validated in subsequent studies. Nonetheless, the attention mechanism is typically applied to raw sub-aperture images, which incurs substantial computational overhead and often fails to fully exploit the intrinsic epipolar geometry embedded in EPIs.

To further overcome the locality constraints of CNNs and fully leverage the global context of light fields, recent studies introduce Transformer architectures. Liang et al. <citation>["Liang", "Transformer", "light field", "epipolar geometry"]</citation> propose an Epipolar Transformer framework that applies self-attention mechanisms along the angular and spatial dimensions of EPIs. By treating EPI patches as sequential tokens, this method explicitly models the global linear trajectories and view-dependent variations across the entire light field. The Transformer-based architecture successfully achieves state-of-the-art performance, particularly in scenarios requiring global reasoning and fine-grained structural preservation. Generally, these advanced models fine-tune the feature extraction process within the EPI domain, implicitly assuming that enhancing resolution or network capacity can indefinitely improve depth estimation. Despite these architectural advancements, they inherently encounter the same physical bottlenecks when dealing with complex real-world reflections.

Despite the significant progress achieved by these deep learning-based general models, they exhibit several critical limitations that hinder their applicability in real-world scenarios. First, the vast majority of these architectures implicitly assume a pure Lambertian reflectance model. When encountering non-Lambertian materials (e.g., specular or scattering surfaces), the fundamental photo-consistency assumption is severely violated, causing the view-consistency features to collapse and leading to a dramatic degradation in depth estimation performance. Although a few attempts have been made to separate materials via frequency-domain analysis, they fail to recognize the hardware resolution boundaries; as our theoretical analysis reveals, a standard $9 \times 9$ angular resolution is fundamentally insufficient for reliable frequency-based material classification. Second, these methods lack an effective mechanism to handle the imbalanced data distribution across multiple domains (Lambertian, Non-Lambertian, and Mixed). Training directly on mixed datasets typically causes the model to bias towards the dominant domain, neglecting the scarce non-Lambertian samples. Finally, and most importantly, the community lacks a systematic evaluation of the performance ceiling of EPI-based architectures in complex Lambertian scenes. Existing works fail to clarify that merely improving network complexity or data cleaning cannot overcome the physical and architectural limits of pure EPI modeling (e.g., an inherent Mean Absolute Error $\approx 0.20$). In contrast to these approaches that blindly scale up EPI-based networks, our work explicitly identifies this ceiling, proposes a unified dual-mask architecture with domain-balanced training to robustly handle mixed reflectance properties, and highlights the necessity of incorporating non-EPI cues or hybrid routing for future architectural evolution.

## 2.3 Deep Learning-based Physics-Driven and Non-Lambertian Light Field Analysis

While the aforementioned approaches primarily exploit geometric photo-consistency, addressing non-Lambertian surfaces necessitates the incorporation of physical reflectance priors and advanced signal analysis. To mitigate the corruption of epipolar structures by specular highlights, several methods have turned to angular frequency-domain analysis. For instance, Huang et al. <citation>["Huang", "angular frequency", "material classification", "specular"]</citation> proposed a frequency-driven framework that classifies surface materials by analyzing the angular frequency spectrum of light fields. The core idea relies on the observation that specular reflections exhibit distinct high-frequency angular responses compared to the low-frequency profiles of diffuse Lambertian regions. By explicitly separating these frequency components, their method successfully guides the depth regression network to down-weight unreliable specular pixels, yielding remarkable improvements on glossy surfaces. However, this approach fundamentally assumes that the light field possesses a sufficiently high angular resolution to capture discriminative non-DC frequency nodes, which is rarely satisfied in practical scenarios.

While frequency-domain techniques implicitly distinguish materials via spectral responses, explicit physics-driven networks attempt to directly model the underlying rendering equations for reflection separation. Li et al. <citation>["Li", "physics-driven", "reflection separation", "rendering equation"]</citation> introduced a heavy multi-branch architecture that explicitly decomposes light fields into diffuse and specular radiance components. Specifically, they integrate physical priors of the bidirectional reflectance distribution function (BRDF) into the network design, forcing the intermediate features to align with physically meaningful diffuse and specular maps. This explicit decomposition significantly enhances depth accuracy on complex reflective surfaces, such as metals and ceramics. Nonetheless, the reliance on elaborate physical modeling results in a massive parameter footprint and high computational overhead. Moreover, such physics-driven networks typically process single-directional epipolar plane images (EPIs) and fail to effectively integrate multi-directional EPI structures with dual-mask mechanisms (i.e., medium masks and angular direction masks) for joint spatial-angular modeling.

Beyond single-domain physical modeling, constructing a unified model capable of handling both Lambertian and non-Lambertian scenes requires robust multi-domain training strategies. To bridge the domain gap between synthetic Lambertian data and real-world non-Lambertian captures, Zhang et al. <citation>["Zhang", "domain adaptation", "mixed-domain", "generalization"]</citation> explored a joint training framework equipped with domain adversarial adaptation. Their method aligns the feature distributions across different domains, enabling the network to generalize better to unseen reflective materials. Although this multi-domain approach improves cross-domain robustness, it typically employs uniform or naive sampling strategies during training. Consequently, the model's performance on the data-scarce non-Lambertian domain remains severely constrained, failing to establish a truly unified and balanced baseline for mixed-domain light field depth estimation.

In short, while existing physics-driven and non-Lambertian light field analysis methods have made significant strides, they collectively suffer from three critical limitations that hinder their practical deployment and theoretical soundness. First, current frequency-domain methods lack rigorous theoretical falsification and boundary definitions regarding angular resolution. They often erroneously assume high angular sampling, whereas in common low-resolution setups (e.g., $9 \times 9$), the severe scarcity of non-DC frequency nodes fundamentally invalidates the frequency-based material classification hypothesis. Second, there is a distinct lack of lightweight, unified baseline models; existing physics-driven networks are excessively parameterized and fail to synergize multi-directional EPIs with dual-mask joint modeling. Third, in multi-domain joint training, the absence of effective domain-balanced sampling strategies severely limits performance gains in the non-Lambertian domain. In contrast to these approaches, our work explicitly addresses these gaps. We rigorously prove the failure of frequency-domain material classification at $9 \times 9$ resolution, establishing a theoretical boundary that necessitates at least $17 \times 17$ angular sampling for future research. Furthermore, we propose a lightweight, unified dual-mask architecture integrated with multi-directional EPIs and a novel domain-balanced sampling strategy, successfully establishing a highly efficient and robust baseline for mixed-domain light field depth estimation.

Although existing methods leverage geometric photo-consistency and angular frequency-domain priors to handle non-Lambertian surfaces, they often rely on unverified assumptions regarding low-resolution frequency analysis and remain constrained by the inherent performance ceilings of traditional Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) architectures. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by integrating multi-directional EPIs with a domain-balanced training strategy for robust mixed-domain estimation, while rigorously redefining the hardware resolution boundaries for frequency-domain material classification and the architectural ceilings of EPI-based paradigms.
