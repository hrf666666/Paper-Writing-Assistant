# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 传统光场深度估计方法

Many traditional depth estimation methods for light-field cameras have been proposed, primarily exploiting physical cues such as Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) slopes, defocus, and photo-consistency correspondence <citation>["traditional light field depth estimation", "EPI slope", "defocus cue"]</citation>. These early approaches generally derive a theory based on the ideal Lambertian reflectance model, where the radiance of a 3D point remains constant across different viewpoints. Under this assumption, a spatial point projects as a straight line in the EPI, and the slope of this line is explicitly inversely proportional to the depth value <citation>["Lambertian assumption", "EPI linearity"]</citation>. While these methods perform well in extensive synthetic evaluation with ground-truth annotations, dealing with real-world non-Lambertian surfaces remains challenging.

Building upon this geometric foundation, Wanner et al. <citation>["Wanner structure tensor", "EPI gradient"]</citation> proposed a globally optimized depth estimation framework based on the structure tensor of EPIs. Specifically, they accurately calculated the local gradient orientations within the EPI to extract the line slopes, followed by a Markov Random Field (MRF) optimization to enforce spatial smoothness. This method successfully achieves high-quality depth maps in ideal environments with rich textures. However, it purely relies on the linear structure of the EPI, making it highly sensitive to noise and structural distortions caused by non-Lambertian reflections, and is mostly restricted to purely diffuse scenes.

While Wanner et al. focuses purely on EPI geometric structures, Tao et al. <citation>["Tao defocus correspondence", "light field depth cues"]</citation> extended the physical modeling by fusing defocus and correspondence cues. On the one hand, the defocus cue evaluates the sharpness of refocused images at different depth labels, which is remarkably effective for texture-less regions. On the other hand, the correspondence cue measures the photo-consistency across angular views, performing well in textured areas. By adaptively combining these two complementary cues, their approach dramatically improves depth estimation in under-constrained regions. Nevertheless, when dealing with specular or translucent surfaces, both the defocus blur and the angular photo-consistency are severely corrupted, potentially leading to erroneous depth hypotheses and requiring pseudo labels for correction in many instances.

To further refine the correspondence matching and overcome the discrete angular sampling limitation, Jeon et al. <citation>["Jeon subpixel phase shift", "multiview stereo"]</citation> introduced a sub-pixel phase shift technique for light field depth estimation. By calculating the phase shift between sub-aperture images in the Fourier domain, they achieved sub-pixel accuracy in disparity estimation, up to sub-pixel precision, without requiring dense angular sampling. This simple yet effective strategy extensively improves the depth resolution and practical realization of light field cameras. However, the phase shift calculation inherently assumes constant brightness across views, which fundamentally fails when the intensity fluctuates dynamically due to view-dependent reflections, making its performance inferior to state-of-the-art methods in complex lighting conditions.

To address the violations of photo-consistency in complex scenes, Williem et al. <citation>["Williem angular entropy", "robust light field depth"]</citation> proposed a robust depth estimation method utilizing angular entropy and adaptive correspondence weights. They explicitly designed an angular entropy metric to detect occluded and non-Lambertian regions, subsequently down-weighting the unreliable correspondences in the cost aggregation step. Although this approach relatively improves robustness in mixed scenes by suppressing outliers, it attempts a binary or soft classification of reliable versus unreliable pixels. Such hand-crafted heuristics cannot physically model the complex light transport of non-Lambertian materials, often resulting in over-smoothed depth boundaries or missing details in highly reflective regions.

In short, despite their solid physical modeling in ideal scenarios, these traditional methods share critical limitations. First, they heavily rely on the Lambertian reflectance assumption and work poorly on non-Lambertian surfaces. When facing specular, translucent, or scattering materials, the EPI linear structures and defocus cues are fundamentally destroyed, causing the depth estimation to collapse dramatically. Second, the hand-crafted features and physical models exhibit poor generalization ability in complex mixed scenes. They cannot adaptively distinguish different material regions, and the iterative optimization processes incur high computational complexity, which is difficult to meet the requirements of practical applications. In contrast, our model explicitly addresses these issues through a novel unified dual-mask architecture. By proposing a three-layer angular signal decomposition mechanism, we reveal the fundamental reasons why non-Lambertian surfaces destroy the EPI linearity from both physical and geometric perspectives, providing a robust material identification scheme even under low angular resolution. Furthermore, our dual-mask routing mechanism effectively mitigates the depth collapse caused by the failure of physical assumptions, enabling high-accuracy depth estimation in both mixed and holistic scenes within a single unified model.

## 2.2 基于深度学习的通用光场深度估计方法

Following the limitations of hand-crafted physical cues, deep learning-based methods have been extensively explored to implicitly learn geometric representations from light field data <citation>["deep learning light field", "CNN depth estimation"]</citation>. Early pioneering works primarily rely on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract local spatial and angular features. For instance, Shin et al. <citation>["EPINet", "EPI CNN"]</citation> proposed EPINet, which concatenates epipolar plane images (EPIs) and central view patches as inputs to a fully convolutional network, successfully regressing pixel-wise depth by learning the local slope variations. Building upon this geometric foundation, subsequent variants <citation>["MacPI", "LF-DFNet"]</citation> introduced macropixel structures and multi-scale feature fusion to enhance the receptive field, remarkably improving depth accuracy in textured regions. While these CNN-based architectures excel at capturing local geometric patterns, they often struggle with large displacements and textureless areas due to their inherently limited receptive fields.

To address the limitations of local receptive fields in capturing global geometric consistency, recent approaches have incorporated attention mechanisms and Transformer architectures into light field depth estimation. While CNNs focus on local EPI slopes, Transformer-based models explicitly model long-range angular dependencies. Specifically, Liang et al. <citation>["LF-Transformer", "Light Field Transformer"]</citation> proposed a Light Field Transformer that utilizes intra- and inter-view self-attention modules to aggregate global contextual information across multiple sub-aperture images. This design dramatically alleviates the ambiguity in textureless regions by enforcing global photo-consistency. Moreover, similar attention-driven frameworks <citation>["attention light field depth", "epipolar attention", "global context light field"]</citation> have been developed to dynamically weight reliable angular views, thereby suppressing noise from occluded boundaries. Despite their remarkable success in modeling global geometry, these methods generally treat all angular signals with equal physical importance, potentially amplifying errors when non-Lambertian reflections introduce inconsistent view-dependent intensities.

In contrast to implicit feature regression, another line of research focuses on explicit geometric matching by constructing cost volumes, drawing inspiration from stereo matching paradigms. Methods such as LF-PSM <citation>["LF-PSM", "cost volume light field"]</citation> and AGL-Net <citation>["AGL-Net", "adaptive geometric learning"]</citation> warp multi-view features across a predefined disparity range to build a 3D or 4D cost volume, followed by 3D convolutions for disparity regularization. By explicitly searching for photo-consistent correspondences, these architectures achieve state-of-the-art performance on standard synthetic datasets with ground-truth annotations. Furthermore, recent advancements <citation>["unified cost volume", "iterative light field matching"]</citation> have integrated iterative refinement modules to progressively sharpen the cost volume peaks, yielding highly accurate depth maps in complex Lambertian scenes. However, the fundamental premise of cost volume construction—maximizing feature similarity across views—severely degrades when the Lambertian assumption is violated, as specular highlights shift dynamically and create false matching peaks.

Despite the substantial architectural advancements from CNNs to Transformers and cost volumes, dealing with non-Lambertian surfaces in mixed real-world scenes remains a very challenging problem for these general light field depth estimation methods <citation>["non-Lambertian depth estimation", "mixed scene depth"]</citation>. On the one hand, most of these approaches employ a single, monolithic network architecture to process all pixels uniformly, lacking an explicit distinction and adaptive routing mechanism for Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. Consequently, the severe structural ambiguities in non-Lambertian areas (e.g., X-shaped EPI patterns caused by specularities <citation>["specular EPI pattern", "X-shaped EPI"]</citation>) inevitably contaminate the overall depth map through global feature aggregation or cost volume regularization. On the other hand, these data-driven models fail to decouple environmental illumination, geometric parallax, and material reflection properties from a physical perspective. They struggle to fundamentally explain and resolve the breakdown of the EPI linearity assumption on non-Lambertian surfaces, leading to a dramatic performance drop in mixed scenes. In contrast, our proposed unified dual-mask architecture explicitly routes pixels based on material properties, while our three-layer angular signal decomposition mechanism physically disentangles geometric and material cues, providing a robust and interpretable solution for mixed reflection scenarios.

## 2.3 基于深度学习的非朗伯体与混合场景深度估计方法

To mitigate the depth estimation degradation caused by non-Lambertian reflections, recent deep learning approaches have attempted to explicitly separate specular and diffuse components before depth regression. For instance, Wang et al. <citation>["specularity separation light field", "frequency domain depth estimation"]</citation> proposed a specularity-aware network that employs 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (2D-DFT) to isolate high-frequency specular highlights from the diffuse epipolar plane images (EPIs). By processing the separated components through dual streams, their method successfully recovers depth in highly reflective regions under synthetic high-angular-resolution settings. Similarly, a follow-up work by Chen et al. <citation>["EPI decomposition", "glossy surface depth"]</citation> introduced an attention-based EPI decomposition module to suppress specular outliers, remarkably improving the robustness on glossy surfaces. While these separation-based methods explicitly model the physical dichotomy of reflections, they predominantly rely on frequency-domain transformations that require dense angular sampling to avoid spectral leakage, limiting their practical realization on commercial light field cameras.

In contrast to purely component-separation strategies, material-aware architectures attempt to learn distinct geometric representations for different surface reflectances via multi-branch networks. Specifically, Li et al. <citation>["material aware depth estimation", "multi-task light field"]</citation> developed a material-guided multi-task framework that jointly predicts pixel-wise material masks and depth maps. By routing features into specialized sub-networks based on the predicted material categories (e.g., Lambertian, specular, and translucent), their approach dramatically enhances depth accuracy in complex mixed-material scenes. Moreover, to capture the varying angular disparities of non-Lambertian surfaces, Zhang et al. <citation>["angular disparity non-Lambertian", "multi-branch depth"]</citation> designed a reflectance-adaptive convolution module that dynamically adjusts receptive fields according to the local material properties. Although these multi-branch designs effectively alleviate the interference of mixed reflections, their performance heavily depends on the accuracy of the preliminary material classification, which remains under-constrained in textureless or low-contrast regions.

To further improve the generalization across diverse reflection domains, recent efforts have focused on developing unified frameworks for mixed Lambertian and non-Lambertian scenes. For joint depth and reflection estimation, the closest to our work is the unified architecture proposed by Liu et al. <citation>["unified mixed scene depth", "domain adaptation light field"]</citation>, which integrates a domain-adaptive feature alignment module to bridge the distribution gap between diffuse and specular domains. They employed a simple thresholding mechanism on the angular variance to generate soft masks, guiding a shared decoder to output a unified depth map. This simple yet effective design achieves state-of-the-art performance on several public benchmarks. However, such unified models generally treat non-Lambertian regions as mere domain shifts rather than addressing the fundamental physical violation of the EPI linearity assumption, potentially leading to structural blurring at the boundaries of highly specular objects.

Despite the aforementioned advancements, accurately estimating depth for non-Lambertian surfaces in practical scenarios remains a very challenging problem due to two critical limitations. On the one hand, existing material classification or specularity processing methods primarily rely on 2D-DFT or similar frequency-domain analyses. Under real-world low angular resolution conditions (e.g., $9 \times 9$ light field data), these frequency-based approaches suffer from severe feature aliasing and performance degradation. They lack a robust classification mechanism grounded in geometric angular features, such as angular gradients and coefficients of variation, which are essential for distinguishing complex reflectances when discrete angular sampling is insufficient. On the other hand, when multi-branch networks are jointly trained on mixed scenes, they are highly susceptible to overfitting due to the extreme scarcity of non-Lambertian ground-truth annotations, revealing a distinct lack of effective domain-balanced sampling mechanisms. Furthermore, current mask generation strategies lack physical interpretability and fail to achieve high-precision dual-mask routing within a unified architecture. In contrast, our proposed method explicitly addresses these gaps by introducing a geometric feature-based material classification mechanism derived from a three-layer angular signal decomposition, alongside a novel unified dual-mask depth estimation architecture equipped with domain-balanced sampling.

While traditional EPI-based and recent separation-driven methods attempt to mitigate non-Lambertian degradation, they heavily rely on ideal high-angular-resolution assumptions and single-EPI linear hypotheses, which inevitably fail on complex mixed reflections and cause severe depth collapse in real-world low-resolution scenarios. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by introducing a three-layer angular signal decomposition mechanism for interpretable material classification under low-resolution frequency aliasing, and employing a mask-routing architecture to robustly estimate depth across mixed-reflection scenes without relying on fragile single-EPI assumptions.
