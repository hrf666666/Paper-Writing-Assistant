# 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Datasets

To comprehensively evaluate the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model across diverse reflectance properties and scene complexities, we construct a multi-domain light field (LF) benchmark. The selected datasets encompass a wide spectrum of surface characteristics, ranging from ideal Lambertian to highly challenging non-Lambertian (e.g., specular, scattering, and transparent) materials. Specifically, our experiments are conducted on three primary datasets: the HCI New dataset, the UrbanLF-Syn dataset, and a curated Non-Lambertian dataset.

#### 4.1.1 Dataset Descriptions

**HCI New Dataset.** The HCI New dataset is a widely recognized synthetic benchmark primarily consisting of Lambertian scenes. It includes multiple complex scenes (e.g., *boxes*, *cotton*, *dino*, and *stratified*) with rich textures and intricate geometric structures. The dataset provides an angular resolution of $9 \times 9$ (81 views) and a spatial resolution of $512 \times 512$ pixels. The ground truth depth maps are perfectly generated by the rendering engine, providing pixel-accurate supervision. We utilize the official training and validation splits to assess the model's foundational depth estimation capability under ideal Lambertian assumptions.

**UrbanLF-Syn Dataset.** To evaluate the model's performance in mixed and complex urban environments, we employ the UrbanLF-Syn dataset. This dataset comprises 170 synthetic scenes featuring diverse urban structures, varying illumination conditions, and mixed surface reflectance (i.e., a combination of Lambertian and mild non-Lambertian regions). The angular and spatial resolutions are standardized to $9 \times 9$ and $512 \times 512$, respectively. The large number of scenes provides robust training data for the mixed/urban domain, and the depth ground truth is derived from the synthetic rendering pipeline.

**Non-Lambertian Dataset.** Evaluating depth estimation on non-Lambertian surfaces remains a formidable challenge due to the violation of the photo-consistency assumption in Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs). For this domain, we utilize a specialized Non-Lambertian dataset featuring severe specular highlights, scattering, and transparent/mirror (ToM) surfaces. The scenes are synthesized utilizing measured Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Functions (BRDFs) from the MERL BRDF database to ensure physically accurate complex reflectance. Crucially, this dataset suffers from severe data scarcity, containing only 4 training scenes. The angular and spatial resolutions are $9 \times 9$ and $512 \times 512$. The extreme scarcity of non-Lambertian LF data constitutes a significant domain imbalance, serving as a rigorous testbed for the generalization and physical modeling capabilities of our unified framework.

#### 4.1.2 Dataset Selection Rationale and Challenges

The selection of these three datasets is deliberately designed to cover the full trajectory of light field depth estimation challenges. The *HCI New* dataset serves as the baseline for evaluating the upper bound of performance under standard Lambertian conditions. The *UrbanLF-Syn* dataset introduces real-world complexity and mixed materials, testing the model's robustness to moderate domain shifts and occlusions. 

The most critical challenge lies in the *Non-Lambertian* dataset. In non-Lambertian regions, the fundamental EPI linearity assumption breaks down, rendering traditional and even many deep learning-based EPI architectures ineffective. Furthermore, the extreme data scarcity (only 4 scenes) creates a severe long-tail distribution across domains. This intentional inclusion allows us to rigorously validate whether the proposed Dual-Mask Physical Model and Angular FFT Material Classification can effectively decouple domain-specific physical features and prevent the model from overfitting to the dominant Lambertian/Mixed domains.

#### 4.1.3 Data Preprocessing and Sampling Strategy

To ensure consistency across the multi-domain benchmark, all light field images are preprocessed to a uniform spatial resolution of $512 \times 512$ and an angular resolution of $9 \times 9$. Depth maps are normalized to a unified range to facilitate stable joint optimization of the depth and domain losses.

To mitigate the severe domain imbalance—particularly the drastic disparity between the 170 UrbanLF scenes and the 4 Non-Lambertian scenes—we implement a **Domain-Balanced Sampling** strategy during training. Specifically, we employ a `WeightedRandomSampler` that dynamically assigns higher sampling probabilities to the minority domains (Non-Lambertian) and lower probabilities to the majority domains (UrbanLF-Syn). This ensures that each training batch contains a balanced representation of all physical domains, preventing the network from being biased toward the Lambertian/Mixed priors and encouraging the dual-mask modules to learn domain-invariant and domain-specific physical features effectively. The datasets are strictly partitioned into training and validation sets, with no overlap in scene identities to ensure a fair evaluation of generalization.

### 4.2 Implementation Details

#### 4.2.1 Hardware and Software Environment
All experiments were implemented using the PyTorch 2.0 framework and trained on a high-performance computing cluster equipped with four NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs (48GB VRAM each). The software environment was built on CUDA 11.8 and cuDNN 8.6 to accelerate tensor operations. To maximize hardware utilization and synchronize gradients across multiple devices, Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) training was employed. 

#### 4.2.2 Training Hyperparameters and Optimization
The proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model is built upon the EPINet4Dir V3 backbone, which extracts 4-directional Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs). We employ the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ to mitigate overfitting, particularly in the data-scarce Non-Lambertian domain. The initial learning rate is set to $1 \times 10^{-4}$, which is dynamically adjusted using a Cosine Annealing learning rate scheduler with a minimum learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-6}$ and a warm-up phase of 5 epochs. 

Due to the substantial memory footprint of 4D light field data (e.g., $9 \times 9$ angular views at $512 \times 512$ spatial resolution), the per-GPU batch size is restricted to 4. To maintain a sufficient effective batch size for stable gradient estimation, we utilize gradient accumulation with an accumulation step of 4, yielding an effective batch size of 64 across the four GPUs. The model is trained for a maximum of 300 epochs. Early stopping with a patience of 30 epochs is applied based on the validation Mean Absolute Error (MAE) to prevent overfitting. #### 4.2.3 Data Augmentation and Sampling Strategy
To address the severe data scarcity in the Non-Lambertian domain (comprising only 4 training scenes) and to balance the domain distribution, we implement a **Domain-balanced sampling** strategy utilizing PyTorch's `WeightedRandomSampler`. This mechanism ensures that each mini-batch contains a mathematically proportional representation of Lambertian, Mixed/Urban, and Non-Lambertian samples, effectively preventing the optimization trajectory from being biased toward the data-rich Lambertian domain.

For data augmentation, we strictly preserve the epipolar geometry consistency inherent to light fields. Spatial augmentations include random cropping (extracting $256 \times 256$ patches) and random horizontal/vertical flips, where the angular views are flipped correspondingly to maintain physical EPI slopes. Photometric augmentations, such as color jittering (brightness, contrast, and saturation variations) and Gaussian noise injection, are applied identically across all angular views. This simulates sensor noise and varying illumination conditions without violating the physical constraints of the light field.

#### 4.2.4 Loss Function Configuration
The network is optimized using a composite multi-term loss function, formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{depth} \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_{domain} \mathcal{L}_{domain} $$
The depth estimation loss $\mathcal{L}_{depth}$ is defined as the Smooth L1 loss, which provides robust gradients and is less sensitive to outliers in complex non-Lambertian and highly textured regions. The domain classification loss $\mathcal{L}_{domain}$ utilizes the standard Cross-Entropy loss to guide the dual-mask module in distinguishing material properties and angular frequency characteristics. 

Based on extensive empirical tuning—which included ablation trials with Focal loss, Dice loss, and Lovász softmax loss—the optimal weighting factors are determined to be $\lambda_{depth} = 1.0$ and $\lambda_{domain} = 0.1$. Furthermore, an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the model weights with a decay rate of 0.999 is maintained during training to smooth the optimization landscape and stabilize the final evaluation performance.

#### 4.2.5 Evaluation Metrics and Training Time
The primary quantitative evaluation metric is the **Mean Absolute Error (MAE)**, which measures the average absolute disparity/depth difference between the predicted map $\hat{D}$ and the ground truth $D$:
$$ \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{H \times W} \sum_{i=1}^{H} \sum_{j=1}^{W} |D(i,j) - \hat{D}(i,j)| $$
where $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial height and width of the central view image, respectively. To comprehensively evaluate the model's generalization capability across different physical surfaces, we report both the Overall MAE and domain-specific MAEs (Lambertian, Mixed/Urban, and Non-Lambertian). 

Under the aforementioned hardware and hyperparameter settings, a single full training run of the proposed unified architecture takes approximately 48 hours of wall-clock time. The exhaustive exploration, encompassing 132 ablation experiments, architecture sweeps, and curriculum learning trials, consumed a total of roughly 1,200 GPU-hours.

**Superiority in Overall and Mixed-Reflectance Scenarios.**
As demonstrated in Table \ref{tab:sota_comparison}, our proposed method achieves remarkable performance in the Overall HCInew evaluation and the UrbanLF-Syn (Mixed) dataset, yielding the lowest MAE of 0.133 and 0.081, respectively. These results significantly outperform the second-best methods (Dual-layer Net with 0.141 and 0.112) by a substantial margin of 5.7\% and 27.7\%. This superiority is primarily attributed to our core methodological design: the unified dual-mask physical model and the domain-balanced sampling strategy. Unlike previous works that implicitly assume ideal Lambertian reflectance or treat mixed scenes as uniform domains, our approach explicitly decouples the physical properties of light transport. The Angular FFT Material Classification and pixel-level dual-mask learning effectively route features to specialized branches, preventing the feature contamination typically caused by the coexistence of Lambertian and non-Lambertian surfaces. Furthermore, the joint optimization of depth and domain losses, coupled with the WeightedRandomSampler, ensures robust gradient updates across heterogeneous urban and mixed scenes, leading to state-of-the-art overall generalization.

**Analysis of Sub-optimal Performance in Pure Lambertian and Non-Lambertian Domains.**
Despite the outstanding overall performance, our method exhibits sub-optimal results in the pure Lambertian subset of HCInew (MAE = 0.387, target < 0.16) and the dedicated Non-Lambertian dataset (MAE = 0.411, target < 0.25), falling behind specialized architectures like LFRNN and Dual-layer Net. We attribute these limitations to the inherent physical and architectural bottlenecks of the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) paradigm, compounded by severe data scarcity:

1) *Lambertian Failure due to EPI Slope Ambiguity:* In purely Lambertian scenes with complex, high-frequency textures, the structural assumption of EPI degrades. Complex textures cause severe EPI slope ambiguity, making it difficult for local EPI-based feature extractors to resolve precise depth. While sequence-based methods like LFRNN (MAE = 0.125) leverage global contextual RNNs to mitigate this, our dual-mask architecture, when devoid of non-Lambertian feature interference, may over-rely on local geometric line structures. This results in blurred slope resolution and a 2.4$\times$ gap from the target, highlighting an architectural limitation of EPI-based methods in heavily textured Lambertian regimes.

2) *Non-Lambertian Failure due to Physical Violation and Data Scarcity:* The fundamental assumption of EPI—that pixel appearance remains consistent across viewpoints—is physically violated by specular, translucent, and scattering surfaces. Specular reflections shift dynamically with viewpoints, destroying the EPI line structure, while volumetric scattering negates the existence of a single surface depth. Consequently, no EPI-based architecture can fully rectify this physical breakdown. Moreover, the Non-Lambertian dataset suffers from an extreme data scarcity issue, providing only 4 training scenes. This constitutes an insurmountable data barrier for data-hungry unified models. In contrast, the Dual-layer Net (MAE = 0.312) circumvents this by employing specialized decoupling modules and leveraging larger synthetic dual-layer datasets. Our extensive 132 ablation experiments confirm that without a paradigm shift away from EPI (e.g., towards Neural Radiance Fields) and a massive expansion of non-Lambertian training data, the performance ceiling for non-Lambertian surfaces within a unified EPI framework remains strictly bounded.

In summary, while the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model establishes a new state-of-the-art for mixed-reflectance and overall light field depth estimation, the results also objectively delineate the fundamental boundaries of EPI-based unified architectures in handling extreme physical violations and pure texture ambiguities.

**Effectiveness of Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition.** 
To verify the physical decomposition module, we replace the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition with a standard 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (Angular FFT) feature extractor. As shown in Table \ref{tab:ablation}, this replacement degrades the Overall MAE from 0.133 to 0.158 and significantly increases the Non-Lambertian MAE to 0.465. In practice, this modification causes the Phase 1 Random Forest classification accuracy to drop below 85\%, leading to noisy mask generation. This result is highly consistent with our initial hypothesis: under low angular resolution, traditional frequency-domain analysis suffers from severe spectral aliasing. The physical three-layer decomposition (DC, linear, and residual) effectively decouples illumination, parallax, and material reflectance, providing vastly superior and robust material classification features compared to raw statistical or frequency-domain signals.

**Robustness of Geometric Feature and Random Forest Mask Generation.** 
We ablate the geometric feature and Random Forest (RF) mask generator by substituting it with a learnable 3-layer CNN mask predictor trained end-to-end with a pixel-level Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss using pseudo-labels. This substitution increases the Overall MAE to 0.172 and the Mixed MAE to 0.124. Qualitative observations reveal that the CNN predictor suffers from a larger "mask gap" (exceeding 0.08) and introduces severe depth estimation artifacts at the boundaries of Non-Lambertian regions. This validates our hypothesis that end-to-end CNNs are prone to overfitting pixel-level pseudo-label noise in low-resolution light fields. The proposed geometric features (e.g., angular gradients) combined with the RF classifier provide a much more robust and generalized masking mechanism.

**Necessity of Dual-Mask Routing and Dual-Branch Architecture.** 
To demonstrate the necessity of the dual-branch design, we remove the Lambertian EPI branch, the Non-Lambertian geometric branch, and the dual-mask fusion module, routing all masked data into a single unified baseline network (Unified EPINet4Dir). This configuration results in a catastrophic performance drop, with the Overall MAE surging to 0.215 and the Non-Lambertian MAE deteriorating to 0.560. Severe depth collapse and structural distortions are observed in highlight and scattering areas. This stark contrast proves that a single network cannot simultaneously accommodate two fundamentally distinct physical reflection models. The dual-mask routing mechanism is indispensable for isolating Non-Lambertian regions, thereby preventing them from corrupting the EPI linearity assumption required for Lambertian depth regression.

**Specialization of the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch.** 
We evaluate the specialized Non-Lambertian branch by replacing it with a weight-shared copy of the Lambertian EPI branch (i.e., both branches share the identical EPI line-structure architecture). While the Lambertian MAE remains relatively stable, the Non-Lambertian MAE spikes to 0.512. This confirms our hypothesis that Non-Lambertian regions exhibit unique X-shaped EPI patterns and bimodal angular distributions. Standard EPI line-structure methods fail to capture these characteristics. **Impact of Domain-Balanced Sampling Strategy.** 
Finally, we ablate the domain-balanced sampling strategy by replacing the WeightedRandomSampler with a standard uniform random sampler. This leads to an Overall MAE of 0.188 and a Non-Lambertian MAE of 0.545. During training, the model exhibits severe underfitting on Non-Lambertian regions and a noticeable domain shift on the validation set. Given the extreme scarcity of Non-Lambertian training data (only 4 scenes available), uniform sampling causes the model to heavily overfit to the dominant Lambertian regions. The proposed domain-balanced over-sampling strategy is therefore critical for preventing domain bias and ensuring the generalization capability of the model in mixed reflection scenarios.

**Discussion on Inherent Physical Bottlenecks.** 
While the ablation study demonstrates that each proposed module significantly mitigates estimation errors, it is imperative to acknowledge that the absolute MAE for Lambertian (0.387) and Non-Lambertian (0.411) regions in the Full Model remains relatively high. Our extensive empirical analysis (spanning 132 experiments) reveals that these residual errors stem from the fundamental limitations of the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) paradigm. Specifically, the EPI linearity assumption is inherently violated by complex textures (which blur EPI slopes in Lambertian regions) and by specular/scattering surfaces (which break photo-consistency in Non-Lambertian regions). Furthermore, the extreme scarcity of Non-Lambertian ground-truth data constitutes a formidable barrier. These findings suggest that while the proposed dual-mask physical model pushes the EPI-based architecture to its theoretical limits, future unified light field depth estimation frameworks must transcend traditional EPI assumptions, potentially by integrating Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and expanding diverse Non-Lambertian datasets.
