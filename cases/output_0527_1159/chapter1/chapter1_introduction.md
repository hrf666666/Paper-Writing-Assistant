# 1. Introduction

Light field (LF) imaging captures both spatial and angular information of light rays, enabling advanced 3D vision applications such as autonomous driving, virtual reality, and robotic manipulation <citation>["light field imaging", "3D vision applications"]</citation>. Depth estimation serves as a foundational task in LF processing, providing essential geometric cues for downstream scene understanding and navigation <citation>["depth estimation", "light field"]</citation>. Conventional LF depth estimation algorithms predominantly rely on the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) structure, exploiting the photo-consistency and linear epipolar constraints inherent in Lambertian surfaces <citation>["EPI", "Lambertian assumption"]</citation>. Under the strict Lambertian assumption, these methods achieve high accuracy by calculating the slopes of epipolar lines across different angular views, forming the basis of most current geometric reconstruction pipelines.

However, obtaining the accurate shape of glossy, transparent, or scattering objects remains challenging, since standard Lambertian cues cannot be easily applied to non-Lambertian surfaces <citation>["non-Lambertian surfaces", "glossy objects"]</citation>. In real-world environments, scenes are inherently composed of diverse materials, including metals, plastics, ceramics, and biological tissues, which exhibit complex bidirectional reflectance distribution functions <citation>["BRDF", "real-world environments"]</citation>. These non-Lambertian materials violate the core photo-consistency assumption. Specifically, specular reflections introduce view-dependent highlights, while scattering media cause light diffusion, both of which severely distort the linear epipolar structures in the angular domain <citation>["specular reflections", "epipolar structures"]</citation>. Consequently, the angular consistency, which is the core working principle of most LF depth algorithms, breaks down in these regions.

The severity of this performance degradation is evident in quantitative evaluations of state-of-the-art learning-based models. For instance, a representative EPI-based baseline achieves a promising Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of $0.081$ in Lambertian-dominant mixed urban scenes. Unfortunately, when evaluated on strictly non-Lambertian subsets, the error deteriorates significantly to $0.411$, indicating a complete failure of the depth recovery process <citation>["performance degradation", "Mean Absolute Error"]</citation>. Visual analysis further reveals that in non-Lambertian regions, the network predictions degenerate into mere replications of the input textures rather than recovering the underlying geometric structures. This substantial error amplification demonstrates that simply scaling up network parameters or increasing training data cannot resolve the intrinsic physical contradictions caused by non-Lambertian reflections.

Addressing non-Lambertian depth estimation represents a hard challenge due to the complex light-matter interactions at the pixel level. Existing approaches typically treat specularities and scattering as outliers, employing heuristic masking or reflection removal techniques prior to depth estimation <citation>["reflection removal", "outlier masking"]</citation>. On the one hand, these preprocessing steps often discard valuable angular information and introduce visual artifacts. On the other hand, they fail to provide a unified physical representation for mixed scenes where Lambertian and non-Lambertian materials coexist at the sub-pixel level. From a physical optics perspective, distinguishing diffuse, specular, and scattering reflections requires analyzing the wavevector variations and angular frequency distributions <citation>["wavevector variations", "angular frequency"]</citation>. These physical properties cannot be adequately captured by conventional spatial texture features or simple epipolar slope calculations.

Therefore, developing a unified depth estimation framework that explicitly models spatially varying reflectance properties is urgently needed to advance LF technology to unconstrained real-world applications <citation>["spatially varying BRDFs", "real-world applications"]</citation>. A robust solution must decouple the light-matter interaction types from the geometric depth cues, leveraging the rich angular sampling of LF cameras to perform pixel-level material perception. Establishing such a physically grounded model not only resolves the persistent failure modes in non-Lambertian regions but also enhances the generalization capability across diverse scenarios. This theoretical advancement is essential for realizing reliable and comprehensive 3D scene reconstruction in complex optical environments.

The severity of this performance degradation highlights the critical limitations of current algorithms when applied to real-world scenarios. Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily rely on geometric constraints derived from Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) <citation>["EPI", "epipolar geometry"]</citation>. These approaches calculate epipolar line slopes to infer depth, achieving high accuracy on purely diffuse surfaces by effectively exploiting angular photo-consistency. However, they inherently assume a strict Lambertian reflectance model <citation>["Lambertian assumption", "diffuse surfaces"]</citation>, causing complete performance degradation in non-Lambertian regions. Specifically, view-dependent specular highlights and scattering effects severely distort linear EPI structures, thereby violating the core photo-consistency assumption <citation>["photo-consistency", "angular consistency"]</citation>. Furthermore, these traditional geometric techniques lack explicit physical modeling of complex light-matter interactions. They typically employ heuristic outlier rejection to handle reflections, which cannot accurately decouple complex mixed reflectance components. Consequently, their practical applicability remains strictly limited to controlled environments that contain predominantly matte objects.

To overcome the limitations of handcrafted features, early deep learning-based methods utilize convolutional neural networks to extract depth from multi-view inputs <citation>["early deep learning LF", "EPINet"]</citation>. These data-driven models demonstrate improved robustness by implicitly learning complex angular correlations across different viewpoints. Unfortunately, they suffer from severe overfitting when applied to non-Lambertian scenes due to the scarcity of annotated training data with diverse material properties <citation>["data scarcity", "non-Lambertian dataset"]</citation>. Moreover, these early architectures typically apply a uniform depth estimation strategy across the entire spatial domain. They fail to differentiate between diffuse, specular, and scattering regions, leading to visible artifacts in areas with complex bidirectional reflectance distribution functions <citation>["BRDF", "reflectance models"]</citation>. This uniform processing paradigm prevents the effective utilization of material-specific geometric cues for accurate 3D shape recovery.

Recent advanced networks attempt to address these challenges by incorporating attention mechanisms, multi-branch structures, and preliminary material awareness modules <citation>["advanced LF networks", "attention LF"]</citation>. Despite these architectural improvements, significant methodological and technical bottlenecks remain when handling complex real-world reflections. First, existing material perception modules predominantly rely on purely data-driven classifiers, such as standard convolutional layers, to identify reflectance types. Under limited angular resolutions, these learning-based classifiers struggle to capture subtle physical distinctions between high-frequency specular and mid-frequency scattering reflections. Second, estimating complex reflectance parameters currently requires massive network capacities and extensive annotated datasets to fit empirical models. These heavy models not only incur high computational costs but also yield parameter estimations that lack strict physical consistency with optical laws <citation>["physical optics", "Maxwell equations"]</citation>.

In summary, current light field depth estimation paradigms struggle with inadequate physical representation, data-hungry parameter estimation, and inflexible depth inference strategies. Both traditional and learning-based methods fail to explicitly decouple light-matter interaction types from wave vector variations in the angular domain. They also lack a unified mechanism to dynamically adapt depth calculation strategies based on pixel-level material properties. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified framework that integrates explicit physical modeling with adaptive depth estimation to robustly handle mixed-reflectance scenes.

In this paper, we propose a unified dual-mask physical model for light field depth estimation to address the severe degradation caused by non-Lambertian reflections. Instead of relying solely on geometric epipolar constraints, our approach formulates depth inference as a physically driven frequency and spatial joint parsing problem. Specifically, we introduce a dual-mask mechanism comprising a medium mask for surface roughness quantification and an angular direction mask for deflection vector field recording. These masks decouple light-matter interaction types from wavevector variations. By integrating this physical representation with a component-aware fusion network, our framework adaptively adjusts depth estimation strategies according to local material properties. This design effectively circumvents the strict Lambertian assumption, enabling robust depth recovery across complex mixed scenes.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- **Contribution 1**: We propose an angular frequency dual-mask physical representation model inspired by magnetic resonance imaging k-space frequency analysis. By applying a two-dimensional discrete Fourier transform to the $9 \times 9$ angular sampling matrix, we extract angular spectrum energy distributions. This establishes a physical mapping where diffuse reflection corresponds to low frequencies, specular reflection to high frequencies, and scattering to mid frequencies. This representation provides robust features to distinguish Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions, overcoming the limitations of traditional spatial texture dependencies.

- **Contribution 2**: We develop a lightweight inverse wavevector parsing mechanism for bidirectional reflectance distribution function parameter estimation. We construct a constrained least squares model with 81 equations to solve the quantitative weights of diffuse, specular, and scattering components. Furthermore, we calculate deflection vectors and verify their consistency with Maxwell equations. This approach achieves physically meaningful parameter estimation without requiring massive neural networks or extensive annotated data, thereby alleviating model overfitting in data-scarce non-Lambertian scenarios.

- **Contribution 3**: We design a component-aware adaptive fusion depth estimation architecture that abandons the global Lambertian assumption. The network implements differentiated depth strategies based on local material properties. It employs epipolar methods for diffuse regions, introduces photometric stereo for specular areas, and applies scattering corrections for scattering regions. Additionally, we introduce a confidence-weighted fusion mechanism and incorporate pixel-level material weights into the loss function. This effectively resolves the failure of epipolar methods in non-Lambertian areas, significantly enhancing generalization in mixed scenes.

Extensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets, including HCI and UrbanLF, validate the effectiveness of our proposed framework. The quantitative results demonstrate that our method achieves competitive performance, substantially reducing the mean absolute error in both non-Lambertian and complex mixed scenes. Specifically, the physical frequency analysis and adaptive fusion strategies enable precise depth recovery on glossy and scattering surfaces. By explicitly modeling physical light-matter interactions, our approach yields accurate depth maps and exhibits strong robustness compared to existing geometry-based and purely data-driven baselines.
