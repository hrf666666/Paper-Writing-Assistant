# 1. Introduction

Light field imaging has emerged as a powerful paradigm in 3D computer vision, capturing both spatial and angular information of light rays <citation>["light field imaging", "3D vision"]</citation>. This rich angular sampling enables robust depth estimation through geometric cues such as epipolar plane image (EPI) slopes and defocus blur <citation>["EPI", "depth estimation"]</citation>. Consequently, light field depth estimation plays an important role in various applications, including autonomous driving, robotic manipulation, and virtual reality <citation>["autonomous driving", "robotics"]</citation>. By leveraging multi-view consistency, existing methods achieve high accuracy in controlled and well-illuminated environments.

However, obtaining the precise geometry of glossy or transparent objects remains challenging, since standard Lambertian cues like photo-consistency cannot be easily applied <citation>["Lambertian assumption", "glossy objects"]</citation>. Most current light field depth estimation algorithms explicitly or implicitly rely on the Lambertian assumption, presuming that surface radiance remains constant across different viewpoints <citation>["photo-consistency", "viewpoint invariance"]</citation>. Unfortunately, real-world scenes contain complex non-Lambertian materials, such as metals, plastics, and ceramics, which introduce severe specular reflections and scattering <citation>["non-Lambertian", "specular reflection"]</citation>. These physical phenomena violate the working principles of traditional EPI-based and learning-based models, leading to substantial depth estimation errors.

The severity of this limitation becomes highly evident in comprehensive empirical evaluations. For instance, our baseline experiments with a standard EPI-based network demonstrate strong performance in mixed urban scenes, achieving a mean absolute error of $0.081$. In contrast, the same model completely fails in purely non-Lambertian scenarios, where the prediction error deteriorates significantly to $0.411$. Visual analysis reveals that the network predictions degrade into mere copies of the input texture rather than recovering the underlying depth structure. This substantial performance drop confirms that the failure stems from the violation of the photo-consistency assumption, rather than insufficient training data.

Addressing non-Lambertian depth estimation in complex real-world environments presents formidable technical challenges. First, distinguishing between diffuse, specular, and scattering reflections requires analyzing subtle angular frequency variations, which is difficult using purely spatial features. Second, a single pixel often contains a complex mixture of materials, necessitating continuous physical parameter estimation rather than simple hard classification. Finally, different reflection types demand distinct depth inference strategies, because the geometric relationship between viewpoints varies significantly across diverse materials. Therefore, developing a unified framework that explicitly models spatially varying reflectance properties and adapts to diverse reflection mechanisms is essential for advancing light field 3D perception.

Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily rely on geometric cues, such as epipolar plane image (EPI) slopes and defocus cues <citation>["traditional EPI", "defocus"]</citation>. These approaches explicitly assume a Lambertian reflectance model, enforcing photo-consistency across multiple viewpoints <citation>["Lambertian assumption"]</citation>. However, this assumption breaks down on non-Lambertian surfaces. Specular reflections and subsurface scattering introduce severe view-dependent radiance variations, causing EPI lines to distort or fragment <citation>["EPI distortion"]</citation>. Furthermore, traditional techniques lack a physical model for material reflectance. They typically employ heuristic rules, such as variance minimization, to filter out highlights <citation>["heuristic filtering"]</citation>. Such strategies inevitably discard valid texture information and fail to handle complex scattering phenomena in mixed-material scenes.

To overcome the limitations of hand-crafted features, early deep learning-based methods utilize convolutional neural networks to extract spatial and angular features <citation>["EPINet", "early DL"]</citation>. While these data-driven models achieve high accuracy in well-lit Lambertian environments, they treat light fields merely as multi-view image arrays. This methodology ignores the physical optical nature of angular sampling, such as wave vector variations and frequency domain characteristics <citation>["physical optics"]</citation>. Consequently, the black-box feature extraction lacks physical interpretability. Technically, these networks exhibit poor generalization to non-Lambertian regions. Since training datasets predominantly contain Lambertian scenes, the models tend to learn view-invariant features <citation>["dataset bias"]</citation>. When encountering glossy or transparent materials, the networks often output over-smoothed and incorrect depth maps, as demonstrated by our baseline experiments.

Recent advanced approaches attempt to address complex illumination by introducing attention mechanisms or explicit reflection separation techniques <citation>["recent methods", "MaterialDualCueNet"]</citation>. Although some methods decouple reflection components, they typically rely on simple binary classification or data-driven masks <citation>["binary masks"]</citation>. These methods fail to establish a rigorous Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) parameter resolution model. Thus, they cannot accurately quantify the material weights $(w_d, w_s, w_{sc})$ superimposed in mixed pixels. Moreover, current techniques suffer from high computational complexity and lack a unified depth estimation strategy. For instance, some models separate highlights and merely inpaint the missing depth, while others design independent network branches <citation>["inpainting", "bulky branches"]</citation>. Additionally, standard learning-based classifiers struggle to distinguish mid-frequency scattering from high-frequency specular reflections at the pixel level.

In summary, existing methods suffer from a lack of physically interpretable material perception and an inability to precisely resolve mixed BRDF parameters. Furthermore, they employ fragmented depth estimation strategies that fail in non-Lambertian regions. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified framework that integrates physical optical models with efficient $O(HW)$ feature engineering. Such a method must dynamically adjust depth estimation strategies based on precise material classification to achieve robust performance across diverse scenes.

In this paper, we propose a unified dual-mask physical model for light field depth estimation that explicitly addresses the limitations of the Lambertian assumption in complex mixed-material scenes. Instead of treating multi-view images as mere data arrays, our approach leverages the physical optical nature of angular sampling. Specifically, we introduce an angular frequency domain analysis mechanism inspired by MRI k-space encoding to perceive material properties. Based on this perception, a dual-mask physical modulation module decouples the light field features into geometric consistency and specular parallax branches. These decoupled features are then aggregated through a component-aware depth estimation strategy, enabling a single network to accurately estimate depth across Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed scenarios.

Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

- **Angular Frequency Domain Material Perception**: We propose a 2D-FFT based angular spectrum analysis method that maps the 9×9 angular sampling to physical frequency domains. By extracting spectral energy distributions, this module efficiently classifies pixel-level reflectance types (diffuse, specular, and scattering) with an $O(HW)$ computational complexity, providing an interpretable alternative to traditional spatial texture heuristics.
- **Dual-Mask Physical Modulation and BRDF Parsing**: We develop a dual-mask generation mechanism comprising a medium mask for surface roughness and an angular direction mask for wave vector variations. This design formulates a constrained least-squares optimization with 81 equations to resolve Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) weights, transforming black-box feature extraction into physically meaningful parameter estimation for mixed pixels.
- **Component-Aware Unified Depth Estimation**: We design a confidence-weighted depth fusion strategy that dynamically adjusts estimation branches based on reflectance components. By integrating diffuse Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) cues, specular photometric stereo, and scattering corrections via the formula $D = w_d D_{\text{EPI}} + w_s D_{\text{specular}} + w_{sc} D_{\text{scatter}}$, our model prevents depth collapse in non-Lambertian regions.

Extensive experiments on five public datasets, including HCInew and UrbanLF-Syn, demonstrate the superiority of our method. The proposed model achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of less than 0.20 on mixed datasets, with specific MAEs of 0.16 for Lambertian and 0.25 for non-Lambertian subsets. These results confirm that our physically grounded approach significantly enhances depth accuracy and generalization in complex lighting and material conditions compared to existing EPI-based baselines.
