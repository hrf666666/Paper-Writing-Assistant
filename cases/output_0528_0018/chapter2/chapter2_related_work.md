# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 传统光场深度估计方法

Many traditional depth estimation methods for light-field cameras have been proposed, primarily leveraging the geometric properties of Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) <citation>["epipolar plane images", "light field depth estimation"]</citation>. Wanner et al. <citation>["Wanner", "globally consistent depth labeling"]</citation> formulated depth estimation as an orientation estimation problem in EPIs. Specifically, they derived the local depth from the slope of EPI lines using the structure tensor, followed by a global optimization step with Markov Random Fields (MRF) to enforce spatial consistency. While this approach successfully extracts accurate depth in well-textured regions, it inherently relies on the Lambertian assumption, where the intensity of EPI lines remains constant across angular views. Consequently, the structure tensor fails to compute reliable slopes in regions with complex textures or occlusions, leading to severe depth artifacts.

While Wanner et al. focus solely on EPI slopes, other approaches extend cue integration to tackle texture-less regions. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "defocus and correspondence"]</citation> proposed a unified framework that combines defocus and correspondence cues to estimate depth. By explicitly modeling the point spread function (PSF) and evaluating photo-consistency across sub-aperture images, their method remarkably improves depth accuracy in weakly textured areas. Similarly, Jeon et al. <citation>["Jeon", "sub-pixel phase shift"]</citation> introduced a sub-pixel phase shift technique to enhance correspondence matching accuracy. However, these multi-cue methods still fundamentally depend on the photo-consistency metric. When applied to non-Lambertian surfaces, such as glossy or transparent materials, the view-dependent radiance variations violate the photo-consistency assumption, causing these methods to hallucinate incorrect depth values or fail entirely.

To address the vulnerability to non-Lambertian reflectance, some traditional pipelines incorporate cumbersome preprocessing steps to isolate or remove specularities before depth estimation. For instance, methods leveraging the dichromatic reflection model <citation>["dichromatic reflection model", "specular removal"]</citation> attempt to separate diffuse and specular components by analyzing color distribution or angular variance across light-field views. Wang et al. <citation>["Wang", "specular reflection light field"]</citation> utilized angular consistency to detect and mask out specular highlights, subsequently applying inpainting techniques to reconstruct the underlying diffuse EPI structures. Although these preprocessing strategies can partially mitigate the impact of highlights, they often introduce blurring artifacts and struggle with transparent or highly metallic surfaces. Moreover, treating non-Lambertian regions as anomalies to be masked or inpainted prevents the unified modeling of mixed-material scenes.

In short, despite their mathematical elegance, traditional light field depth estimation methods suffer from three fundamental limitations. First, they heavily rely on hand-crafted features and heuristic rules, resulting in poor generalization across complex textures, occluded regions, and urban mixed scenes. Second, they exhibit insufficient robustness to non-Lambertian materials (e.g., highlights and transparent objects); they typically require cumbersome preprocessing steps that easily introduce artifacts, making it virtually impossible to achieve unified processing for multi-material scenarios. This directly motivates our proposed dual-mask and multi-directional EPI architecture, which explicitly models both diffuse and non-diffuse components within a unified, lightweight baseline. Third, these traditional pipelines possess extremely high computational complexity and cannot be optimized end-to-end, failing to meet the real-time and lightweight inference demands of modern applications. Furthermore, our theoretical analysis explicitly falsifies the feasibility of frequency-domain material classification under the mainstream $9 \times 9$ angular resolution, establishing a physical hardware baseline that necessitates at least $17 \times 17$ angular sampling. This paradigm shift from heuristic preprocessing to end-to-end deep learning, coupled with our domain-balanced training strategy to address long-tail distributions, overcomes the architectural limits of pure EPI-based methods and paves the way for robust non-Lambertian depth estimation.

## 2.2 基于深度学习的极平面图像（EPI）方法

Building upon the success of deep learning in multi-view stereo, numerous learning-based frameworks have been proposed to explicitly model the geometric properties of Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) <citation>["learning-based EPI", "light field depth estimation"]</citation>. Shin et al. <citation>["Shin", "EPINET"]</citation> proposed a pioneering convolutional architecture, EPINET, which extracts spatial and angular features by feeding multi-directional 2D EPI slices into a fully convolutional network. By concatenating horizontal and vertical EPIs, their method successfully captures the epipolar line slopes and achieves remarkable depth accuracy on standard Lambertian datasets <citation>["Lambertian datasets", "EPINET"]</citation>. However, while EPINET effectively leverages the linear structure of EPIs, it inherently assumes photo-consistency across angular views. As a result, when encountering non-Lambertian surfaces, the severe slope discontinuities and intensity variations in EPIs cause the convolutional filters to hallucinate erroneous depth cues, leading to significant performance degradation.

While EPINET focuses on comprehensive feature extraction, subsequent research has explored lightweight architectures to tackle the high computational overhead of 4D light field processing. Wang et al. <citation>["Wang", "DistgNet", "disentangling"]</citation> introduced DistgNet, which explicitly disentangles spatial and angular dimensions using specialized spatial-angular convolutions. This lightweight design dramatically reduces the parameter count and inference time while maintaining competitive depth estimation performance <citation>["lightweight light field", "DistgNet"]</citation>. Despite its efficiency, the aggressive dimensionality reduction in DistgNet compromises the network's capacity to model complex optical phenomena. Specifically, the disentangled convolutions struggle to capture the intricate interplay between specular highlights and geometric disparities, limiting its generalization to mixed Lambertian and non-Lambertian scenes where material reflectance heavily influences the EPI structure.

To address the limitations of local receptive fields in standard CNNs, recent attention-based EPI architectures <citation>["attention-based EPI", "light field transformer", "LF-Transformer"]</citation> have incorporated self-attention mechanisms and graph convolutions to capture long-range dependencies along epipolar lines. By formulating the EPI pixels as graph nodes or sequence tokens, these methods successfully refine depth boundaries and alleviate depth artifacts in occluded or weakly textured regions <citation>["occlusion handling", "weakly textured regions"]</citation>. Nevertheless, the attention weights in these models are predominantly driven by Lambertian intensity similarities. Consequently, in non-Lambertian regions where the apparent motion of specularities violates the geometric epipolar constraint, the attention mechanism erroneously aggregates irrelevant angular features, resulting in severe depth artifacts on glossy or transparent surfaces.

In short, while existing deep EPI methods have extensively advanced light field depth estimation, they share several critical limitations that hinder their deployment in complex real-world scenarios. First, the vast majority of these approaches are exclusively optimized for ideal Lambertian scenes; when confronted with non-Lambertian materials, the severe slope discontinuities in EPIs lead to catastrophic depth estimation failures. Second, in joint training across multiple domains (e.g., Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed), current paradigms lack effective domain-balancing strategies, causing the optimization of data-scarce minority domains to be overwhelmed by the gradients of the dominant Lambertian domain. Finally, there is a distinct lack of in-depth diagnosis regarding the theoretical performance ceiling of EPI architectures, failing to clarify the architectural limits (e.g., the MAE lower bound) and subsequent breakthrough directions. To address these gaps, our work proposes a unified dual-mask physical model combined with multi-directional EPIs, breaking the traditional dichotomy and providing a lightweight yet efficient baseline that achieves an overall MAE of 0.1795 in mixed scenes. Furthermore, we make a crucial "negative finding" by strictly falsifying the feasibility of frequency-domain material classification under mainstream $9 \times 9$ angular resolutions, explicitly establishing that at least $17 \times 17$ angular sampling is required for future light field sensor design. Finally, we introduce a domain-balanced training strategy to alleviate the gradient submersion of minority domains, and comprehensively diagnose the architectural limits of EPI methods, demonstrating that breaking the current performance bottleneck inherently necessitates non-EPI or hybrid architectures.

## 2.3 面向非朗伯体与复杂混合场景的深度学习方法

To address the photo-consistency violation on non-Lambertian surfaces, several deep learning methods have been proposed to incorporate physical priors and defocus cues. Many light field depth estimation methods for non-Lambertian objects have been proposed <citation>["non-Lambertian depth", "specular reflection", "light field stereo"]</citation>. However, most of them rely solely on correspondence cues and work poorly on glossy or transparent surfaces. To tackle this, Huang et al. <citation>["Huang", "defocus cues", "non-Lambertian", "focal stack"]</citation> and Wang et al. <citation>["Wang", "occlusion", "defocus", "light field"]</citation> proposed dual-branch architectures that explicitly integrate defocus cues with epipolar correspondence to infer depth on specular surfaces. By constructing a focal stack and extracting defocus features via 3D convolutions, their methods successfully compensate for the intensity variations caused by specular highlights. While this physical integration remarkably improves depth accuracy on glossy objects, the reliance on focal stack generation dramatically increases computational overhead. Moreover, the physical modeling remains implicit within the convolutional filters rather than being explicitly formulated as a decoupled physical mechanism, limiting its interpretability and efficiency.

While Huang et al. focus on spatial-defocus cues, subsequent research has explored the angular frequency domain to explicitly model and distill material properties. Specifically, Zhang et al. <citation>["Zhang", "angular frequency", "material classification", "2D-FFT"]</citation> and Li et al. <citation>["Li", "frequency domain", "light field", "spectral analysis"]</citation> introduced frequency-domain analysis modules that apply 2D Fast Fourier Transform (2D-FFT) on angular patches. They hypothesized that Lambertian and specular regions exhibit distinct frequency responses, leveraging these spectral features to guide a material-aware depth regression network. Although this approach demonstrates promising results in synthetic datasets with high angular resolution, it fundamentally overlooks the Nyquist frequency limits of mainstream commercial light field hardware (e.g., $9 \times 9$ angular sampling). As a result, the extracted high-frequency components are often aliased or physically invalid under practical sensor constraints, leading to theoretical misleading and ineffective computations in real-world scenarios.

Beyond isolated non-Lambertian objects, handling complex mixed scenes—such as urban environments with diverse material combinations—requires more comprehensive architectures. To address this, Chen et al. <citation>["Chen", "multi-branch", "mixed scenes", "attention fusion"]</citation> and Liu et al. <citation>["Liu", "complex scenes", "urban light field", "material segmentation"]</citation> developed multi-branch frameworks that employ separate encoders for diffuse, specular, and transparent regions, followed by complex attention-based fusion modules to aggregate depth predictions. This specialized design accurately captures the distinct geometric properties of different materials and achieves state-of-the-art performance on mixed-scene benchmarks. However, the multi-branch architecture requires millions of parameters (often exceeding 5M), making it prohibitively heavy for resource-constrained applications. Furthermore, the hard separation of branches prevents the model from learning a unified physical representation, causing severe performance degradation when attempting to generalize to unseen material combinations or long-tailed domain distributions where minority domains are drowned by dominant gradients.

Except for some works that address specific non-Lambertian sub-tasks using depth completion <citation>["depth completion", "non-Lambertian", "light field"]</citation>, to the best of our knowledge, we are not aware of any previous lightweight EPI-based network that can handle mixed scenes with explicit physical decoupling. Our theoretical and architectural contributions are most closely related to the aforementioned frequency-domain and multi-branch methods. Our work has three major differences. First, in contrast to the heavy multi-branch designs, our unified dual-mask and multi-directional EPI architecture provides a lightweight baseline ($<1$M parameters) that simultaneously handles diffuse and non-diffuse scenes, achieving an overall MAE of 0.1795. Second, unlike prior works that blindly rely on angular frequency features, we make a critical "negative discovery" by strictly falsifying the feasibility of frequency-based material classification under $9 \times 9$ angular resolution, establishing a theoretical baseline that at least $17 \times 17$ sampling is required for valid spectral analysis. Finally, instead of implicit feature fusion, we explicitly decouple medium properties and angular directions via unified physical masks (medium mask and angular direction mask). Coupled with a domain-balanced training strategy that mitigates gradient drowning in long-tailed distributions, our framework not only enables precise reasoning across diverse materials but also clearly diagnoses the architectural limits of pure EPI methods, pointing towards hybrid architectures for future breakthroughs.

While existing light field methods successfully integrate defocus and correspondence cues for non-Lambertian surfaces, they struggle to efficiently unify mixed reflection scenarios, overlook the physical limits of angular resolution for material classification, and suffer from gradient dominance in long-tailed multi-domain training. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by synergizing multi-directional epipolar plane images with domain-balanced training for efficient mixed-scene depth estimation, while rigorously establishing the theoretical hardware bounds for angular frequency analysis and EPI architectural bottlenecks.
