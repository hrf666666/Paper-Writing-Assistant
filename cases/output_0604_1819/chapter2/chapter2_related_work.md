# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Traditional Light Field Depth Estimation Methods

Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily exploit geometric and optical cues under ideal physical assumptions, generally categorizing into Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) analysis, defocus cue utilization, and Multi-View Stereo (MVS) techniques. These approaches model the light field as a collection of rays interacting with Lambertian surfaces, where the radiance remains invariant across different viewing angles.

EPI-based methods establish the foundation of light field depth estimation by analyzing the linear structures formed by light rays in the spatio-angular domain. Wanner and Heidrich <citation>["Wanner", "Heidrich", "variational light field analysis"]</citation> formulated depth estimation as a variational problem, employing the structure tensor of EPIs to extract the slopes of linear trajectories, which directly correspond to scene disparities. This approach produces accurate depth maps in well-textured Lambertian regions by leveraging the strong geometric linearity of EPIs. However, the reliability of the structure tensor degrades significantly when the linear assumption is violated by complex surface reflectance.

To compensate for the limitations of pure EPI methods in textureless regions, researchers have integrated defocus cues with correspondence cues. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "defocus", "correspondence", "light-field cameras"]</citation> proposed a depth estimation framework that combines defocus and correspondence metrics. The defocus cue measures the variance of pixel intensities across different synthetic apertures, providing robust depth information in areas lacking distinct textures, while the correspondence cue relies on the photo-consistency across viewpoints. By fusing these two cues, the method improves depth accuracy in homogeneous regions. Nevertheless, both cues implicitly assume Lambertian reflectance; the correspondence cue fails under specular reflections, and the defocus cue becomes ambiguous when highlights shift across apertures.

To achieve sub-pixel depth precision, MVS techniques have been adapted for light field imaging. Jeon et al. <citation>["Jeon", "MVS", "phase shift", "sub-pixel depth"]</citation> introduced a local phase shift and translation method to align multi-view images, computing matching costs with sub-pixel accuracy. By shifting the phase of the Fourier transform for each view, this method effectively reduces the quantization error inherent in discrete light field sampling, demonstrating substantial improvements in depth resolution. Despite these advancements, the photo-consistency constraint central to MVS remains intrinsically tied to the Lambertian assumption, rendering the matching cost unreliable in the presence of non-Lambertian materials.

Despite their theoretical elegance, these traditional methods share inherent limitations when applied to real-world environments. Primarily, they strictly depend on the Lambertian assumption. When scenes contain non-Lambertian materials such as specular highlights or reflections, the linear structure of EPIs is distorted, and the photo-consistency in MVS is broken, leading to severe depth errors or complete failure. Beyond geometric assumptions, these methods lack the capability to perceive and classify surface materials and reflectance properties. Unable to adaptively adjust in regions where physical assumptions fail, they indiscriminately compute depth, inevitably producing artifacts. This deficiency highlights the necessity for a mechanism that can identify material properties—preferably using geometric features rather than frequency-domain features, which often fail due to the limited angular resolution (e.g., $9 \times 9$) of discrete light field sampling—to route depth estimation processes effectively. From a systemic perspective, these traditional approaches suffer from substantial computational overhead and lack robustness in complex scenes with mixed materials, such as urban environments. They fail to provide reliable prior guidance for modern complex scenarios, underscoring the need for a unified architecture that can isolate conflicting physical assumptions and handle domain shifts through balanced training strategies, especially given the severe scarcity of real-world non-Lambertian light field data.

## 2.2 Deep Learning-based General Light Field Depth Estimation

Deep learning techniques have advanced light field depth estimation by automatically extracting hierarchical features from spatio-angular data. Early convolutional neural network (CNN) based approaches established the paradigm of jointly modeling spatial and epipolar plane image (EPI) features. Shin et al. <citation>["Shin", "EPINET", "epipolar"]</citation> proposed a foundational architecture that concatenates the central view image with multiple angular EPI slices, feeding them into a multi-stream CNN to regress disparity maps. This method demonstrates strong performance on synthetic Lambertian datasets by effectively combining local texture cues with angular geometric structures. However, the simple feature concatenation strategy treats all surface types uniformly, lacking the capacity to differentiate regions where the EPI linearity assumption holds from those where it fails.

To enhance feature representation, subsequent research focused on disentangling and interactively fusing spatial and angular information. Wang et al. <citation>["Wang", "DistgDisp", "disentangled"]</citation> introduced a disentangled feature extraction mechanism that employs specialized 3D and 2D convolutions to process angular and spatial dimensions separately before fusing them via attention modules. This architectural refinement improves depth accuracy at object boundaries and textureless regions by reducing feature interference between different dimensions. Similarly, Liang et al. <citation>["Liang", "LF-DFN", "light field"]</citation> developed a deep fusion network that iteratively refines disparity estimates through cascaded spatial-angular feature interactions. While these advanced CNN architectures improve representational capacity, they inherently assume that all pixels adhere to a unified geometric projection model. Consequently, when applied to non-Lambertian surfaces, the disentangled features still encode distorted EPI slopes caused by specular shifts or volumetric scattering, leading to erroneous disparity predictions.

More recently, Transformer-based architectures have been adopted to capture long-range dependencies within the 4D light field data. Liang et al. <citation>["Liang", "LF-Transformer", "self-attention"]</citation> designed a light field Transformer that utilizes self-attention mechanisms to model global spatio-angular correlations, overcoming the limited receptive fields of conventional CNNs. This approach yields considerable improvements in complex scenes by aggregating contextual information across distant viewpoints. Despite the enhanced global modeling capability, the self-attention mechanism inadvertently propagates feature corruption. In non-Lambertian regions, anomalous pixels generated by specular highlights or translucent scattering act as intense noise, which is then diffused globally across the attention maps, degrading the overall depth estimation quality.

Despite the architectural evolution from basic CNNs to advanced Transformers, these general deep learning methods share critical limitations when confronted with non-Lambertian surfaces. First, model training heavily relies on pure Lambertian datasets, such as HCInew. When encountering non-Lambertian regions, the absence of material priors and isolation mechanisms causes EPI feature extraction to be severely contaminated by specular highlights or scattering effects. Second, a single unified network architecture struggles to simultaneously fit the distinct physical imaging laws of Lambertian surfaces, which depend on EPI linearity, and non-Lambertian surfaces, which follow complex reflection models. This architectural rigidity leads to a significant performance degradation in mixed scenes. Third, existing methods overlook the feature distribution discrepancies between different data domains (Lambertian versus non-Lambertian). Direct end-to-end training easily triggers domain shift, failing to effectively resolve physical assumption conflicts.

To address these deficiencies, our approach introduces a three-layer angular signal decomposition and geometry-based material classification mechanism, revealing the physical and geometric reasons why non-Lambertian surfaces break the EPI linearity assumption. By utilizing geometric features instead of frequency-domain features, this mechanism overcomes the failure of frequency analysis caused by the insufficient resolution of discrete light field sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$), providing an efficient and reliable routing basis for material perception and depth estimation, thereby avoiding blind depth computation in regions where physical assumptions fail. Building upon this classification, we design a unified depth estimation architecture based on dual-mask routing and domain-balanced mixed training. The dual-mask mechanism isolates regions with conflicting physical assumptions at the architectural level, while the domain-balanced mixed training strategy maximizes the utilization of existing synthetic data. This architecture significantly improves depth estimation accuracy in both overall and complex urban mixed scenes, demonstrating the feasibility of enhancing generalization through architectural design and training strategy optimization under data-constrained conditions.

## 2.3 Deep Learning-based Non-Lambertian and Mixed-Scene Depth Estimation

To address the violation of the Lambertian assumption, several methods incorporate physical reflectance models or frequency-domain analysis to identify and process non-Lambertian regions. For instance, frequency-based approaches <citation>["frequency", "specular", "EPI"]</citation> apply Fourier transforms or high-pass filters to Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) to isolate specular highlights, which manifest as distinct high-frequency components compared to the linear structures of diffuse surfaces. By separating these frequency bands, such methods attempt to reconstruct the underlying diffuse EPI slopes or directly regress depth for specular pixels. While effective in controlled environments, these frequency-domain techniques impose stringent requirements on angular resolution. On consumer-grade light field cameras with discrete and sparse angular sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$ or lower), the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem is often violated, causing severe frequency aliasing. Consequently, high-frequency features become unreliable, and these methods lack robust alternatives based on geometric and statistical cues to maintain performance under sparse sampling conditions.

Moving beyond frequency analysis, recent deep learning frameworks employ multi-branch architectures to explicitly disentangle diffuse and specular reflections in the feature space. A representative dual-stream network <citation>["dual-stream", "multi-branch", "reflection"]</citation> utilizes parallel convolutional branches to extract features tailored for Lambertian and non-Lambertian surfaces, respectively. An attention-based fusion module subsequently aggregates these features to produce the final disparity map. This structural separation improves depth accuracy at material boundaries by reducing the interference between conflicting physical assumptions. However, existing multi-branch designs typically lack an explicit physical mask routing mechanism. Without hard routing at the architectural level, features from physically conflicting regions (e.g., the transition zones between diffuse and specular materials) still interact during the fusion stage, leading to depth blurring. These architectures also struggle to achieve dynamic balancing of cross-domain exposures and features within a unified framework, limiting their adaptability to complex mixed scenes.

The efficacy of the aforementioned deep learning models is heavily constrained by the severe scarcity of annotated non-Lambertian light field data in real-world scenarios. To mitigate this data imbalance, current research relies predominantly on synthetic datasets and employs specific training strategies. For example, mixed-scene training paradigms <citation>["synthetic data", "imbalance", "mixed-scene"]</citation> combine Lambertian and non-Lambertian synthetic samples, utilizing pixel-level loss weighting or focal loss to penalize the misclassification of the minority non-Lambertian class more heavily. Some approaches also incorporate curriculum learning or specialized sampling strategies to balance the gradient contributions from different surface types. Despite these efforts, conventional sampling and weighting strategies fail to effectively alleviate the overfitting caused by extreme class imbalance, where non-Lambertian pixels constitute a minimal fraction of the total image. The inherent domain shift between synthetic renderings and real-world complex reflections further degrades the generalization capability of these models when deployed in practical environments.

Collectively, existing approaches for non-Lambertian and mixed-scene depth estimation exhibit three primary limitations. First, current frequency-domain analysis methods impose stringent requirements on light field angular resolution; on consumer-grade light field cameras with discrete and sparse sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$), frequency features frequently fail, lacking robust alternative schemes based on geometric and statistical features. Second, deep learning models targeting non-Lambertian surfaces require extensive annotated data, whereas real-world non-Lambertian light field data is extremely scarce (e.g., containing an extremely limited number of training scenes), and existing sampling strategies cannot effectively alleviate the overfitting caused by extreme imbalance. Third, multi-branch architectures typically lack an explicit physical mask routing mechanism, failing to thoroughly isolate regions with conflicting physical assumptions at the architectural level, and struggling to achieve dynamic balancing of cross-domain exposures and features within a unified framework. Addressing these gaps requires a unified architecture that integrates geometry-based material classification with explicit physical routing and domain-balanced training.

While existing frequency-domain and physics-based methods attempt to handle non-Lambertian reflectance, they predominantly suffer from severe frequency aliasing under sparse angular sampling and lack robust mechanisms to overcome the extreme data scarcity and domain shift in complex scenes. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by replacing aliasing-prone frequency features with a geometry-aware material classification mechanism via tri-layer angular signal decomposition, and by employing a dual-mask routing architecture with domain-balanced mixed training to isolate physical assumption conflicts and maximize data utilization.
