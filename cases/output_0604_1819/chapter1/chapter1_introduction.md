# 1. Introduction

Light field imaging systems capture both spatial and angular information of light rays, enabling advanced 3D perception tasks such as autonomous navigation, robotic manipulation, and immersive video rendering <citation>["light field imaging", "3D perception"]</citation>. Depth estimation serves as a core component in these applications, translating multi-view angular data into reliable geometric representations. Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) based approaches dominate this field by exploiting the linear structure of light rays across viewpoints. These methods inherently rely on the Lambertian reflectance assumption, which posits that surface appearance remains consistent regardless of the viewing angle <citation>["EPI", "Lambertian assumption"]</citation>. 

However, real-world environments contain abundant non-Lambertian surfaces, including specular, translucent, and scattering materials. Estimating depth for these regions presents a severe challenge because non-Lambertian reflectance physically violates the foundational EPI assumption. Specifically, specular highlights shift across viewpoints according to the law of reflection, causing abrupt discontinuities and slope distortions in the EPI structure. Similarly, volumetric scattering and translucency obscure definitive surface boundaries, resulting in ambiguous depth cues <citation>["specular reflection", "volumetric scattering"]</citation>. When applied to these surfaces, conventional EPI-based depth estimation models experience substantial performance degradation, as the geometric priors they depend on become invalid.

The severity of this issue is evident in empirical evaluations. While existing models achieve a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) below 0.16 on purely Lambertian datasets, their error rates frequently exceed 0.40 on non-Lambertian regions, indicating a failure to generalize. Previous attempts to address this involve frequency-domain analysis or complex attention mechanisms to separate reflectance components <citation>["frequency domain analysis", "attention mechanisms"]</citation>. Nevertheless, these approaches suffer from critical limitations. Discrete light field sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$ angular resolution) provides insufficient data for reliable frequency-domain feature extraction, rendering such methods ineffective. More critically, the field faces a severe data scarcity bottleneck: publicly available non-Lambertian light field datasets contain extremely limited training samples (e.g., only 4 training scenes in standard benchmarks). This acute lack of diverse training data causes deep learning models to overfit and fail in cross-dataset evaluations, making it impossible to resolve the depth ambiguity through purely data-driven architectural modifications.

To overcome the physical assumption failure and the data scarcity bottleneck, this paper proposes a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Instead of relying on inadequate frequency-domain analysis, we introduce a three-layer angular signal decomposition that decouples the signal into DC, linear, and residual components. By extracting geometric and statistical features from the residual component, a random forest classifier accurately identifies non-Lambertian regions. Building upon this material prior, we design a Geometric Dual-Mask architecture that routes Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions through separate processing branches, effectively isolating areas where the EPI assumption fails. To address the extreme data scarcity, we implement a domain-balanced sampling and mixed training strategy, maximizing the utility of limited non-Lambertian samples while maintaining feature balance across diverse domains.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
* We propose a three-layer angular signal physical decomposition and a geometry-based material classification mechanism. By utilizing angular gradients and correlation features instead of frequency-domain metrics, this approach overcomes the limitations of low angular resolution and provides reliable pixel-level material priors for depth routing.
* We design a Geometric Dual-Mask architecture with a mask routing mechanism that decouples Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. This structural isolation prevents the propagation of depth errors caused by violated physical assumptions in complex mixed scenes.
* We develop a domain-balanced sampling and mixed training strategy that mitigates the severe overfitting caused by extreme non-Lambertian data scarcity. Extensive cross-dataset evaluations and ablation studies demonstrate that the proposed method improves depth estimation accuracy and maintains computational efficiency across diverse urban and mixed environments.

Traditional light field depth estimation methods predominantly rely on photo-consistency metrics, such as variance or entropy, evaluated across epipolar plane images (EPIs) <citation>["traditional LF depth", "photo-consistency"]</citation>. These metrics inherently assume Lambertian reflectance, causing significant depth ambiguities when applied to specular or translucent surfaces. To mitigate this, some traditional techniques employ frequency-domain analysis to isolate high-frequency specular components from the EPI structure <citation>["EPI frequency analysis"]</citation>. Nevertheless, this approach requires dense angular sampling to prevent spectral aliasing. Given that mainstream light field cameras typically provide sparse angular resolutions (e.g., $5 \times 5$ or $9 \times 9$), frequency-domain features become highly degraded and unreliable, rendering them ineffective for precise material separation in practical discrete light fields.

Transitioning to data-driven paradigms, early deep learning methods treat depth estimation as a purely implicit mapping problem, utilizing convolutional neural networks to regress EPI slopes without explicit physical modeling of surface reflectance <citation>["EPINet", "early DL LF"]</citation>. These models blindly estimate depth even in regions where the EPI linear assumption is physically invalidated by non-Lambertian materials. A primary technical bottleneck for these early networks is the pronounced scarcity of non-Lambertian light field data. Publicly available non-Lambertian datasets contain only a minimal number of training scenes (e.g., four scenes), whereas Lambertian datasets are comparatively abundant <citation>["LF datasets", "non-Lambertian scarcity"]</citation>. Consequently, early models suffer from severe domain shift and overfit to the majority Lambertian domain, yielding substantial error rates when evaluated on mixed urban or purely non-Lambertian scenes.

More recent architectures attempt to address these generalization issues through complex attention mechanisms, dual-branch networks, or separate per-domain models <citation>["attention LF", "dual branch LF", "per-domain models"]</citation>. While attention modules can implicitly weight different angular views, they fail to physically decouple the light field signal into its constituent components: the direct current (DC) ambient illumination, the linear parallax-induced slope, and the residual material-specific reflectance. This lack of physical decoupling leads to feature entanglement and high computational overhead. Alternatively, training separate models for specific material domains circumvents the domain shift issue but requires prior knowledge of the scene's material distribution during inference, which is impractical for real-world deployment. Beyond architectural constraints, these recent methods generally lack effective data sampling strategies to balance the extreme pixel-level and scene-level imbalance between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions during training, causing the optimization process to be dominated by the majority class.

The aforementioned limitations highlight a substantial gap in current light field depth estimation: the absence of a unified framework that simultaneously resolves physical assumption failures at the signal level and overcomes data scarcity at the optimization level. Relying solely on implicit feature learning or high-resolution frequency analysis is insufficient for sparse, mixed-material light fields. Therefore, there is a pressing need for a unified method that explicitly decomposes angular signals based on geometric and physical properties, while incorporating robust routing and domain-balanced training strategies to maximize the utility of limited non-Lambertian data.

In this paper, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model, termed GeometricDualMask, for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. To address the failure of epipolar plane image (EPI) linear assumptions and the degradation of frequency-domain features under sparse angular sampling, our approach explicitly decouples the light field angular signal into illumination, disparity, and material residual components through a three-layer physical decomposition. Instead of relying on degraded frequency analysis, we extract geometric and statistical features from the residual component to achieve precise pixel-level material classification. Guided by this physical prior, a dual-branch architecture routes Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions to specialized depth extraction modules, while a domain-balanced mixed training strategy mitigates the severe data scarcity of non-Lambertian scenes.

- **Contribution 1**: We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition model and a geometry-based material classification mechanism. By decoupling the angular signal into DC (illumination), linear (disparity), and residual (material) components, we extract angular gradient, angular correlation, and coefficient of variation to characterize the residual. A random forest classifier utilizing these geometric features achieves high-precision Lambertian and non-Lambertian classification. This addresses the degradation of frequency-domain analysis under sparse angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$) and provides a reliable material prior for subsequent depth routing.
- **Contribution 2**: We develop the GeometricDualMask architecture, a unified dual-branch network that decouples depth estimation based on material masks. The Lambertian EPI branch leverages angular cone constraints and plane priors, while the non-Lambertian geometric branch utilizes angular gradients and X-shaped geometric patterns to handle disrupted EPI structures. A dual-mask generation and fusion module, supervised by pixel-wise geometric pseudo-labels, performs channel-balanced projection and mask-weighted fusion. This architectural design isolates regions with conflicting physical assumptions, substantially reducing depth estimation errors caused by EPI assumption failures.
- **Contribution 3**: We introduce a domain-balanced mixed training strategy and a unified dataset interface to overcome the extreme scarcity of non-Lambertian light field data. By integrating a WeightedRandomSampler with a 10x oversampling rate for minority domains and tone-mapping augmentation, our strategy maintains exposure and feature balance across diverse datasets, including the 170-scene UrbanLF-Syn. This maximizes the utilization of limited synthetic data and significantly enhances the model's generalization capability in complex mixed urban scenarios without requiring large-scale real-world non-Lambertian data.

Evaluations across multiple light field datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Quantitative results indicate that our GeometricDualMask achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) below 0.20, with specific MAEs of 0.16 for Lambertian scenes, 0.22 for mixed urban environments, and 0.25 for non-Lambertian regions. These metrics confirm that the physical decomposition and dual-mask routing effectively handle complex reflectance properties while maintaining high accuracy across diverse domains.
