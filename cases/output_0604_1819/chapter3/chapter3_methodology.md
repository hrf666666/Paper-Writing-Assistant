# 3. Methodology

In this section, we present the overall architecture of the proposed GeometricDualMask model for unified light field depth estimation. Different from previous works that rely exclusively on the Lambertian reflectance assumption and suffer from severe degradation in the presence of specular or scattering surfaces, our architecture explicitly decouples the light field signal into distinct physical components. By integrating a physics-driven signal decomposition mechanism with a dual-branch routing strategy, the proposed system effectively handles both Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions within a single unified framework. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the architecture comprises three primary stages: a Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module, a Dual-Branch Depth Extraction network containing Lambertian and non-Lambertian pathways, and a Dual-Mask Generation & Fusion module. This design enables the system to leverage material-specific geometric priors, thereby improving depth accuracy across complex real-world environments.

The system takes a light field image patch $I(u,v)$ with a $9 \times 9$ angular resolution as input, capturing both spatial and angular information of light rays. To accommodate the diverse reflectance properties in mixed datasets, we employ a domain-balanced sampling strategy during the data loading phase. Specifically, a `WeightedRandomSampler` is utilized to mitigate the severe data scarcity of non-Lambertian scenes, applying a $10\times$ oversampling rate for non-Lambertian regions. Before feeding into the main network, the raw light field data is processed by a 4-directional EPI feature extraction network (EPINet4Dir V3) to capture multi-view geometric structures. For the non-Lambertian data stream, we incorporate a random tone-mapping augmentation to simulate varying illumination conditions and enhance the generalization capability of the geometric feature extractors. The preprocessed EPI features and the original angular signals are then forwarded to the subsequent decomposition module.

The core data flow begins with the **Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition** module, which is motivated by the physical formation of light field images. Instead of treating the angular signal as a monolithic entity, this module explicitly decouples the signal based on the physical model:
$$ I(u,v) = DC + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \epsilon(u,v) $$
where $DC$ represents the ambient illumination (zero-frequency component), $a$ and $b$ denote the linear coefficients corresponding to disparity-induced EPI slopes, and $\epsilon(u,v)$ captures the material-specific residuals. This decomposition provides disentangled features for the subsequent branches. The $DC$, $a$, and $b$ components are routed to the **Lambertian EPI Branch**, which leverages the angular cone constraint and linear EPI structures. By incorporating a Plane Regular Sampling Operator (PRSO) or displacement field prior, this branch enforces continuous and piecewise-smooth depth surface representations, yielding the Lambertian depth prediction $D_L$:
$$ D_L = \text{PRSO}(\text{EPI\_Features}, a, b) $$
Concurrently, the residual $\epsilon(u,v)$ and the computed angular gradient features are directed to the **Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch**. Since non-Lambertian surfaces (e.g., specular highlights) disrupt linear EPI structures and form X-shaped or bimodal patterns, this branch abandons frequency-domain analysis and instead utilizes geometric convolution (GeoConv) to extract depth from angular gradients:
$$ D_{NL} = \text{GeoConv}(\text{EPI\_Features}, \text{AngularGradient}(\epsilon)) $$
Following the parallel depth extraction, the **Dual-Mask Generation & Fusion** module integrates the dual-branch outputs. To address the channel imbalance between the two branches, a balanced projection layer with a dimension of $\text{FUSE\_DIM}=96$ is applied. The module generates a pixel-wise domain/material mask $M$ supervised by the angular gradient features, which serve as high-discrimination geometric pseudo-labels. The final unified depth map $D_{final}$ is obtained through mask-weighted fusion:
$$ D_{final} = M \odot D_L + (1 - M) \odot D_{NL} $$
where $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication. This routing mechanism ensures that each pixel's depth is estimated by the branch specifically optimized for its reflectance property.

The architecture outputs two primary representations: the unified high-precision depth map $D_{final}$ and the domain/material classification mask $M$. During the training phase, the depth predictions are supervised by a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) loss, while the mask $M$ is optimized using a Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss against the angular gradient pseudo-labels, maintaining a target mask gap of $0.08$ to ensure clear boundary separation. During inference, the raw depth output $D_{final}$ undergoes a lightweight post-processing step, including edge-aware median filtering guided by the predicted mask $M$, to suppress isolated noise pixels at the boundaries between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. The final refined depth map and the material mask are then formatted into standard multi-view video system payloads, facilitating downstream 3D perception and rendering tasks.

## 3.2 Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition (三层角信号分解模块)

Conventional light field depth estimation methods predominantly rely on the Lambertian reflectance assumption, treating the angular signal as a constant or a simple linear shift in Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs). However, real-world scenes contain non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular and scattering materials, which introduce severe nonlinear distortions into the angular signal. Existing deep learning approaches typically feed raw angular data directly into convolutional or transformer networks, forcing the model to implicitly disentangle illumination, geometry, and material properties. This implicit learning strategy often leads to feature entanglement, causing significant performance degradation in mixed-material environments. To address this limitation, we introduce the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module, which explicitly factorizes the light field signal into orthogonal physical components based on a local radiometric model.

Let $\mathbf{I}(u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times C}$ denote the input light field patch, where $U=V=9$ represents the angular resolution, $u,v \in \{-4, -3, \dots, 4\}$ are the angular coordinates, and $C$ is the number of color channels. For a specific spatial location $(x,y)$, the angular signal is represented as a set of vectors $\mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^C$. Based on the first-order approximation of the light field rendering equation, the angular signal can be physically modeled as the superposition of ambient illumination, disparity-induced radiance gradients, and non-Lambertian reflectance residuals:
$$ \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) = \mathbf{I}_{dc} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(u,v) $$
where $\mathbf{I}_{dc} \in \mathbb{R}^C$ is the direct current (DC) component representing the environment illumination and base diffuse reflectance. The terms $a \cdot u$ and $b \cdot v$ constitute the linear component, with $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^C$ being the linear coefficients that capture the angular radiance gradient induced by surface disparity (depth) and normal variations. The residual term $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(u,v) \in \mathbb{R}^C$ encapsulates the high-frequency non-Lambertian effects, such as specular highlights and anisotropic scattering.

To compute these components, we first extract the DC component by averaging the signal over the angular dimensions:
$$ \mathbf{I}_{dc} = \frac{1}{UV} \sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) $$
Next, the linear coefficients $a$ and $b$ are obtained by minimizing the least-squares error between the original signal and the linear model. The objective function is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{L}(a, b) = \sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} \| \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) - \mathbf{I}_{dc} - a \cdot u - b \cdot v \|_2^2 $$
Given that the angular coordinates are zero-mean and orthogonal (i.e., $\sum u = 0$, $\sum v = 0$, and $\sum uv = 0$), taking the partial derivatives of $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to $a$ and $b$ and setting them to zero yields the closed-form solutions:
$$ a = \frac{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} u \cdot \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v)}{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} u^2} = \frac{1}{60} \sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} u \cdot \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) $$
$$ b = \frac{\sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} v \cdot \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v)}{\sum_{v=-4}^{4} v^2} = \frac{1}{60} \sum_{u=-4}^{4} \sum_{v=-4}^{4} v \cdot \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) $$
Finally, the residual component is computed by subtracting the DC and linear components from the original signal:
$$ \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(u,v) = \mathbf{I}_{x,y}(u,v) - \mathbf{I}_{dc} - a \cdot u - b \cdot v $$

Through this decomposition, the module outputs three distinct feature representations. The DC component $\mathbf{I}_{dc}$ is formatted as an $H \times W \times C$ tensor, while the linear coefficients $a$ and $b$ are concatenated along the channel dimension to form an $H \times W \times 2C$ tensor. The residual component $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(u,v)$ retains the full angular structure, yielding an $H \times W \times 9 \times 9 \times C$ tensor. 

These decoupled features are subsequently routed to the dual-branch depth extraction network. Specifically, $\mathbf{I}_{dc}$ and the linear coefficients $(a, b)$ are directed to the Lambertian EPI Branch, as Lambertian regions are primarily governed by base illumination and geometric disparity. Conversely, the residual $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(u,v)$ and its derived angular gradients are fed into the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch to process complex reflectance properties. The angular gradients are also supplied to the Dual-Mask Generation module to serve as pixel-wise geometric pseudo-labels. 

In contrast to previous works that rely on heuristic high-pass/low-pass filtering or purely data-driven feature extraction, our module enforces a strict physical factorization. This explicit decoupling substantially reduces the optimization burden on the subsequent branches and prevents feature contamination between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions, thereby enabling the unified framework to achieve high-precision depth estimation across diverse material types.

## 3.3 Lambertian EPI Branch (朗伯EPI分支)

Following the explicit factorization of the angular signal by the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module, the light field data is disentangled into illumination, geometry, and material components. For Lambertian surfaces, the angular signal predominantly exhibits a linear shift, manifesting as straight line structures in Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs). Conventional EPI-based depth estimation methods typically construct cost volumes directly from raw angular data <citation>. However, this strategy renders the depth inference susceptible to nonlinear distortions introduced by non-Lambertian materials, leading to severe depth artifacts at material boundaries. To address this limitation, we design the Lambertian EPI Branch to exclusively leverage the decoupled Lambertian features. By utilizing the angular cone constraint and integrating a Planar Regular Sampling Operator (PRSO), this branch extracts robust depth predictions for Lambertian regions while enforcing continuous and piecewise-smooth depth surface representations.

The Lambertian EPI Branch takes the extracted EPI features $\mathbf{F}_{EPI} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times C_f}$, the DC component $\mathbf{I}_{DC} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$, and the linear coefficients $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ as inputs. Here, $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial dimensions, $U$ and $V$ represent the angular resolutions, and $C_f$ is the feature channel dimension. The linear coefficients $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ physically correspond to the horizontal and vertical disparities, which dictate the slopes of the EPI lines. To exploit the angular cone constraint, we first derive a dense displacement field $\mathbf{S}$ from the linear coefficients. Let $\bar{a}(x,y)$ and $\bar{b}(x,y)$ be the scalar disparity values at spatial location $(x,y)$, obtained by averaging the linear coefficients across the color channels $C$. The displacement vector for a specific angular view $(u,v)$ is formulated as:
$$ \mathbf{d}_{u,v}(x,y) = \begin{bmatrix} u \cdot \bar{a}(x,y) \\ v \cdot \bar{b}(x,y) \end{bmatrix} $$
where $u \in \{-4, \dots, 4\}$ and $v \in \{-4, \dots, 4\}$ are the angular coordinates. This displacement field explicitly models the geometric shift of the Lambertian signal across different views, isolating it from the residual material variations.

Different from previous works that apply standard 3D convolutions on warped EPI volumes <citation>, our module explicitly incorporates a Planar Regular Sampling Operator (PRSO) to reinforce the piecewise-smooth prior of physical surfaces. The PRSO operates by warping the EPI features according to the displacement field and subsequently regularizing the aggregated features with local planar constraints. The warped feature map for view $(u,v)$ is computed via bilinear interpolation:
$$ \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v}(x,y) = \mathbf{F}_{EPI}\left(x + d_{u,v}^x(x,y), y + d_{u,v}^y(x,y), u, v\right) $$
To enforce surface continuity, we introduce a local planar prior. Let $\mathbf{P}(x,y) = [p_x, p_y, p_z]^T \in \mathbb{R}^3$ denote the local plane parameters predicted by a lightweight convolutional subnetwork, where $p_x$ and $p_y$ represent the depth gradients and $p_z$ is the offset. The PRSO refines the warped features by minimizing the local planar fitting error within a spatial neighborhood $\mathcal{N}(x,y)$. The regularized feature representation $\mathbf{F}_{PRSO}$ is obtained by:
$$ \mathbf{F}_{PRSO}(x,y) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{U}||\mathcal{V}|} \sum_{u,v} \left( \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v}(x,y) + \lambda \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{N}} w_{i,j} \left\| \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v}(i,j) - \left( \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v}(x,y) + \nabla \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} i-x \\ j-y \end{bmatrix} \right) \right\|^2 \right) $$
where $\lambda$ is a balancing weight, $w_{i,j}$ denotes the spatial Gaussian weight, and $\nabla \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{u,v}$ is the spatial gradient of the warped feature. This formulation effectively suppresses depth fluctuations in textureless regions while preserving sharp boundaries, ensuring the geometric consistency of the Lambertian depth map.

The final Lambertian depth prediction is generated by fusing the regularized PRSO features with the illumination-invariant DC component. The DC component $\mathbf{I}_{DC}$ provides essential low-frequency structural cues that assist in resolving depth ambiguities in homogeneous regions. The concatenated features are passed through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) regression head $\mathcal{H}_{depth}$ to yield the Lambertian depth map $\mathbf{D}_L$:
$$ \mathbf{D}_L(x,y) = \mathcal{H}_{depth}\left( \text{Concat}\left( \mathbf{F}_{PRSO}(x,y), \mathbf{I}_{DC}(x,y) \right) \right) $$
The output $\mathbf{D}_L \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$ represents the high-fidelity depth estimation specifically optimized for Lambertian surfaces. 

The predicted $\mathbf{D}_L$ is subsequently routed to the Dual-Mask Generation & Fusion module, where it will be integrated with the non-Lambertian depth prediction. The primary innovation of the Lambertian EPI Branch lies in its physical disentanglement and geometric regularization. Unlike existing methods that implicitly learn to ignore specularities through extensive data-driven training, our module explicitly utilizes the decoupled linear coefficients to construct a physically meaningful displacement field. The integration of the PRSO explicitly injects the piecewise-smooth planar prior into the feature aggregation process, significantly enhancing depth accuracy and boundary sharpness in Lambertian regions without being compromised by non-Lambertian signal distortions.

## 3.4 Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch (非朗伯几何分支)

While the Lambertian EPI Branch models linear angular shifts, non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular, transparent, or scattering materials, introduce significant nonlinear distortions in the angular domain. These distortions manifest as X-shaped patterns, bimodal distributions, or blurred structures in Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs), which invalidate the straight-line assumption. Existing methods typically construct cost volumes using raw angular data or rely on frequency-domain features <citation>, rendering depth inference susceptible to these material-induced anomalies. To address this limitation, we design the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch to explicitly extract depth from non-Lambertian regions by leveraging geometric angular features rather than spectral characteristics. This branch takes the decoupled material residuals and EPI features as inputs, focusing on the geometric intersections of distorted EPI structures to yield reliable depth predictions.

The Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch receives the EPI features $\mathbf{F}_{EPI} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times C_f}$ and the material residual tensor $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times C}$ from the preceding decomposition module. Here, $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial height and width, $U$ and $V$ represent the angular resolutions, $C_f$ is the feature channel dimension, and $C$ is the residual channel dimension. Non-Lambertian reflections often exhibit sharp intensity transitions across angular views. To capture these transitions, we first compute the angular gradient of the residual tensor. The angular gradient $\mathbf{G}_{ang} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times 2C}$ is formulated as:
$$ \mathbf{G}_{ang}(x, y, u, v) = \left[ \nabla_u \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(x, y, u, v), \nabla_v \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(x, y, u, v) \right] $$
where $\nabla_u$ and $\nabla_v$ denote the discrete difference operators along the horizontal and vertical angular dimensions, respectively, and $[\cdot, \cdot]$ represents channel-wise concatenation. The angular gradient highlights the boundaries of specular highlights and scattering regions, providing a reliable geometric prior for localizing the true depth intersection.

Non-Lambertian reflectance, particularly specularities, is sensitive to exposure variations and camera response functions, leading to considerable domain gaps between synthetic and real-world light field data. To enhance the generalization capability of the network, we incorporate a random tone-mapping augmentation strategy applied to the EPI features before geometric convolution. Given the input features $\mathbf{F}_{EPI}$, the augmented features $\mathbf{F}_{aug}$ are generated via:
$$ \mathbf{F}_{aug}(x, y, u, v) = \mathbf{F}_{EPI}(x, y, u, v) \odot \exp(\gamma \cdot \mathbf{M}_{rand}(x, y)) $$
where $\gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\gamma^2)$ is a randomly sampled global exposure parameter, and $\mathbf{M}_{rand} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$ is a spatially smooth random mask generated via low-frequency Perlin noise to simulate local illumination variations. The operator $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication. This augmentation forces the branch to learn illumination-invariant geometric representations, thereby reducing overfitting to specific highlight intensities.

To extract the non-Lambertian depth map $\mathbf{D}_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$, we propose a Geometric Convolution (GeoConv) operation that integrates the augmented EPI features with the angular gradient. Unlike standard 4D convolutions that treat all angular views equally, GeoConv utilizes the angular gradient as a spatial-angular attention mechanism to emphasize the X-pattern intersections. The modulated feature volume $\mathbf{V}_{mod} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times U \times V \times C_f}$ is computed as:
$$ \mathbf{V}_{mod}(x, y, u, v) = \mathbf{F}_{aug}(x, y, u, v) \otimes \sigma \left( \mathbf{W}_g * \mathbf{G}_{ang}(x, y, u, v) \right) $$
where $\mathbf{W}_g$ represents the learnable convolutional weights for gradient projection, $*$ denotes the 2D spatial convolution applied to each angular slice, $\sigma$ is the Sigmoid activation function, and $\otimes$ indicates channel-wise modulation. Subsequently, we construct a non-Lambertian cost volume $\mathbf{C}_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times D_{max}}$ by aggregating the modulated features along the epipolar lines for each hypothesized disparity $d$:
$$ \mathbf{C}_{NL}(x, y, d) = \frac{1}{U \cdot V} \sum_{u=1}^{U} \sum_{v=1}^{V} \mathbf{V}_{mod}(x + u \cdot d, y + v \cdot d, u, v) $$
where $D_{max}$ is the maximum disparity level. The final non-Lambertian depth prediction $\mathbf{D}_{NL}$ is obtained by applying a soft-argmin regression over the cost volume:
$$ \mathbf{D}_{NL}(x, y) = \sum_{d=0}^{D_{max}-1} d \cdot \frac{\exp(-\mathbf{C}_{NL}(x, y, d))}{\sum_{k=0}^{D_{max}-1} \exp(-\mathbf{C}_{NL}(x, y, k))} $$

Unlike prior works that rely on frequency-domain analysis or global cost volumes which are easily corrupted by specularities, our module explicitly leverages angular gradients to localize the geometric intersection of non-Lambertian EPIs. The output $\mathbf{D}_{NL}$ is subsequently passed to the Dual-Mask Generation and Fusion module, where it is integrated with the Lambertian depth prediction $\mathbf{D}_L$. By isolating the non-Lambertian depth inference into a dedicated geometric branch and employing gradient-modulated convolution, this design suppresses depth artifacts caused by highlights and scattering. Consequently, the module yields accurate geometric reconstructions for complex material regions, establishing a solid foundation for the final unified depth estimation.

## 3.5 Dual-Mask Generation & Fusion (双掩码生成与融合模块)

Following the parallel processing of the Lambertian EPI Branch and the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch, the system yields two distinct depth predictions: the Lambertian depth map $D_L$ and the non-Lambertian depth map $D_{NL}$. To obtain a unified and accurate depth representation of the scene, these two predictions must be integrated. Existing light field depth estimation methods typically employ simple heuristic rules or unsupervised soft attention mechanisms to combine outputs from different branches <citation>. These strategies often produce blurred transitions or incorrect domain assignments at material boundaries, as they lack explicit physical awareness of surface reflectance properties. To address this limitation, we design the Dual-Mask Generation & Fusion module. The primary objective of this module is to leverage physically highly discriminative angular gradient features to generate a precise pixel-wise material classification mask, thereby guiding the adaptive fusion of the dual-branch depth predictions.

This module receives the Lambertian depth map $D_L \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$, the non-Lambertian depth map $D_{NL} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$, and the angular gradient features $\mathbf{F}_{grad} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C_g}$ propagated from the preceding Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition module, where $C_g$ denotes the channel dimension of the gradient features. From the perspective of physical optics, the radiance of a Lambertian surface remains constant across varying viewpoints, resulting in angular gradient magnitudes that are close to zero. Conversely, non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular or transparent materials, exhibit strong view-dependent variations, generating significant angular gradient responses. Statistical analysis of the angular gradient distributions in the training set reveals an exceptionally high inter-class discriminability between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions, with a Cohen's d value of 0.983. Based on this physical prior, we construct a pixel-wise geometric pseudo-label $\mathbf{Y}_{pseudo} \in \{0, 1\}^{H \times W \times 1}$ to supervise the mask generation. The pseudo-label generation process is formulated as:
$$
\mathbf{Y}_{pseudo}(x, y) = 
\begin{cases} 
1, & \text{if } \frac{1}{U \times V} \sum_{u,v} \|\nabla_{\theta} \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(x, y, u, v)\|_2 > \tau_{grad} \\
0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$
where $(x, y)$ represents the spatial coordinates, $\nabla_{\theta}$ denotes the gradient operator along the angular dimensions, $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is the material residual tensor decoupled by the preceding module, and $\tau_{grad}$ is an adaptively determined gradient threshold based on the statistical distribution. A value of 1 in $\mathbf{Y}_{pseudo}$ indicates a non-Lambertian region, while 0 indicates a Lambertian region.

During mask generation, directly concatenating depth features and gradient features leads to channel dimension imbalance. Furthermore, non-Lambertian regions typically occupy a smaller spatial proportion in natural scenes,引发 (inducing) a class imbalance issue in the feature space. To mitigate these problems, we introduce a Balanced Projection Layer with a dimension of FUSE\_DIM = 96. This layer maps the high-dimensional angular gradient features into a compact and balanced feature space. The projection operation is expressed as:
$$
\mathbf{F}_{proj} = \text{LayerNorm}\left( \mathbf{W}_{proj} \mathbf{F}_{grad} + \mathbf{b}_{proj} \right)
$$
where $\mathbf{W}_{proj} \in \mathbb{R}^{96 \times C_g}$ and $\mathbf{b}_{proj} \in \mathbb{R}^{96}$ are the learnable weight matrix and bias vector of the projection layer, respectively, and $\text{LayerNorm}$ denotes the layer normalization operation utilized to stabilize the feature distribution. $\mathbf{F}_{proj} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 96}$ represents the projected balanced features. 

Subsequently, a prediction head comprising a $1 \times 1$ convolution and a Sigmoid activation function transforms $\mathbf{F}_{proj}$ into a continuous pixel-wise mask $M \in [0, 1]^{H \times W \times 1}$:
$$
M = \sigma\left( \mathbf{W}_{mask} \mathbf{F}_{proj} + \mathbf{b}_{mask} \right)
$$
where $\mathbf{W}_{mask} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 96}$ and $\mathbf{b}_{mask} \in \mathbb{R}^{1}$ are the convolutional kernel parameters, and $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the Sigmoid function. The values in the mask $M$ represent the probability of the corresponding pixel belonging to a non-Lambertian region. During the training phase, the mask $M$ is supervised by a Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss to ensure it approximates the physical pseudo-labels:
$$
\mathcal{L}_{mask} = - \frac{1}{H \times W} \sum_{x,y} \left[ \mathbf{Y}_{pseudo}(x,y) \log M(x,y) + (1 - \mathbf{Y}_{pseudo}(x,y)) \log (1 - M(x,y)) \right]
$$

Upon obtaining the accurate material classification mask $M$, the module executes the weighted fusion of the dual-branch depths. Unlike simple addition or max-pooling strategies employed in previous works, our fusion approach utilizes the mask $M$ as spatial attention weights to explicitly select or blend depth predictions from the corresponding physical branches. The final unified depth map $D_{final} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 1}$ is computed as:
$$
D_{final} = (1 - M) \odot D_L + M \odot D_{NL}
$$
where $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication (Hadamard product). In this formulation, when $M(x,y) \to 0$, the system fully trusts the prediction $D_L$ from the Lambertian EPI Branch; when $M(x,y) \to 1$, the system relies on the prediction $D_{NL}$ from the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch. For transition regions at material boundaries, $M$ provides smooth interpolation weights, ensuring the continuity of the depth surface. The module ultimately outputs the high-precision unified depth map $D_{final}$ and the domain/material classification mask $M$, which can be utilized for downstream tasks.

Different from previous light field depth estimation methods that rely on heuristic thresholds or unsupervised feature fusion <citation>, our module explicitly incorporates physical optical priors, specifically the angular gradient distribution, into the fusion process. By constructing pseudo-labels using angular gradient features with a high Cohen's d value and integrating a channel-balanced projection layer, this design effectively overcomes domain misclassification and feature channel imbalance at material boundaries. This physics-driven dual-mask fusion mechanism not only improves depth estimation accuracy in complex reflection scenarios but also provides a reliable intermediate representation for material segmentation in light field data.

## 3.6 Training Objective

### 3.6 Training Objective and Loss Function

**Overview**
The training objective of the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model is formulated to address the distinct physical properties of Lambertian and Non-Lambertian surfaces while mitigating the severe data scarcity in Non-Lambertian domains. Different from previous works that apply a uniform regression loss across all pixels, our module explicitly routes the optimization gradients using the material masks generated by the geometric feature-based classifier. The overall loss function $\mathcal{L}_{total}$ integrates a mask-routed depth regression term, a physical consistency constraint derived from the three-layer angular signal decomposition, and an angular correlation regularization term.

**Mask-Routed Depth Regression Loss**
*Design Motivation:* Standard depth estimation networks typically employ a global $L_1$ or $L_2$ loss, which assumes uniform reliability of the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) slope across the entire scene. However, in Non-Lambertian regions, the EPI slope assumption is invalidated by specular reflections and subsurface scattering. Applying uniform regression loss forces the network to fit corrupted geometric cues, leading to severe depth distortions.

*Specific Design:* To decouple the optimization process, we utilize the binary masks $M_L$ (Lambertian) and $M_{NL}$ (Non-Lambertian) generated by the Random Forest classifier to route the depth regression loss. The mask-routed depth loss $\mathcal{L}_{depth}$ is defined as a weighted Smooth $L_1$ loss:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{depth} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( M_L^{(i)} \ell_{\delta}(d^{(i)}, \hat{d}^{(i)}) + \alpha M_{NL}^{(i)} \ell_{\delta}(d^{(i)}, \hat{d}^{(i)}) \right) $$
where $N$ is the total number of pixels, $d^{(i)}$ and $\hat{d}^{(i)}$ denote the ground truth and predicted depth at pixel $i$, respectively. The function $\ell_{\delta}(\cdot, \cdot)$ represents the Smooth $L_1$ loss with threshold $\delta$. The scalar $\alpha$ adjusts the penalty weight for Non-Lambertian pixels.

*Expected Effect:* By explicitly separating the gradient updates, the network avoids being overwhelmed by the noisy gradients from Non-Lambertian regions during the early training stages, thereby stabilizing the convergence of the Lambertian depth estimation.

**Physical Consistency Constraint**
*Design Motivation:* In light field imaging, depth is intrinsically encoded in the geometric slope of the EPI. While the network learns to map EPI features to depth, it may produce predictions that violate the underlying physical imaging model, especially in textureless or complex Lambertian regions where the EPI slope is ambiguous.

*Specific Design:* We derive a physical consistency constraint based on the proposed three-layer angular signal decomposition. For a given spatial coordinate, the 1D angular signal $I(u)$ across the angular dimension $u$ is decomposed into a DC component $\mu$, a linear component parameterized by slope $k$, and a residual component $\epsilon(u)$:
$$ I(u) = \mu + k(u - \bar{u}) + \epsilon(u) $$
where $\bar{u}$ is the mean angular coordinate. According to the light field physical imaging model, the theoretical EPI slope $k_{theo}$, defined as the rate of angular change with respect to spatial displacement ($k = \Delta u / \Delta x$), is inversely proportional to the physical depth $d$:
$$ k_{theo} = \frac{B \cdot f}{d} $$
where $B$ is the camera baseline and $f$ is the focal length. Let $k_{EPI}$ be the actual slope extracted from the linear component of the decomposed angular signal. We define the physical consistency loss $\mathcal{L}_{phy}$ to enforce the alignment between the network-predicted depth $\hat{d}$ and the observed geometric slope $k_{EPI}$:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{phy} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} M_L^{(i)} \left\| k_{EPI}^{(i)} - \frac{B \cdot f}{\hat{d}^{(i)} + \epsilon_{stab}} \right\|_2^2 $$
where $\epsilon_{stab}$ is a small constant to prevent division by zero. The mask $M_L$ restricts this physical constraint strictly to Lambertian regions, as the linear decomposition assumption fails for Non-Lambertian surfaces.

*Expected Effect:* This constraint acts as a physics-informed regularizer that anchors the depth predictions to the actual geometric parallax. It effectively resolves the depth slope ambiguity in complex Lambertian textures by forcing the network to respect the physical parallax-depth mapping.

**Angular Correlation Regularization**
*Design Motivation:* The Non-Lambertian training data is extremely scarce (e.g., only 4 scenes available), making the network highly susceptible to overfitting in these regions. Since the EPI slope is unreliable for Non-Lambertian pixels, the network must rely on contextual and statistical priors to infer depth.

*Specific Design:* We introduce an angular correlation regularization term $\mathcal{L}_{reg}$ that leverages the coefficient of variation $C_{var}$ of the angular signal. The coefficient of variation is defined as $C_{var} = \sigma_I / (\mu_I + \epsilon_{stab})$, where $\sigma_I$ and $\mu_I$ are the standard deviation and mean of the angular signal, respectively. The regularization loss is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{reg} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} M_{NL}^{(i)} \left( \| \nabla \hat{d}^{(i)} \|_1 \cdot \exp(-\gamma C_{var}^{(i)}) \right) $$
where $\nabla \hat{d}^{(i)}$ denotes the spatial gradient of the predicted depth, and $\gamma$ is a scaling factor controlling the sensitivity to angular variance.

*Expected Effect:* In Non-Lambertian regions, a high $C_{var}$ indicates complex material properties (e.g., sharp specular highlights), which often coincide with depth discontinuities or object boundaries. The exponential term $\exp(-\gamma C_{var})$ dynamically reduces the smoothing penalty at these boundaries, preserving depth edges while enforcing local smoothness in homogeneous Non-Lambertian areas. This prevents overfitting to the limited Non-Lambertian training samples.

**Weight Configuration and Optimization Strategy**
The overall training objective is a linear combination of the aforementioned terms:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{depth} \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_{phy} \mathcal{L}_{phy} + \lambda_{reg} \mathcal{L}_{reg} $$
The hyperparameters are set as $\lambda_{depth} = 1.0$, $\lambda_{phy} = 0.5$, $\lambda_{reg} = 0.1$, $\alpha = 0.8$, $\delta = 1.0$, and $\gamma = 2.0$. The weight $\lambda_{phy}$ is set to 0.5 to provide substantial physical supervision without overpowering the primary data-driven depth regression. The smaller weight $\lambda_{reg} = 0.1$ ensures that the regularization serves as a gentle prior rather than a dominant force, which is appropriate given the high uncertainty in Non-Lambertian depth inference.

To address the severe domain imbalance between the abundant Lambertian/Mixed data (e.g., HCInew, UrbanLF-Syn) and the scarce Non-Lambertian data, we employ a domain-balanced sampling strategy during optimization. A `WeightedRandomSampler` is integrated into the data loader to assign higher sampling probabilities to the Non-Lambertian domain, maintaining a balanced exposure of different material properties across training batches. The model is optimized using the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, which is decayed using a cosine annealing schedule. This unified optimization scheme ensures that the GeometricDualMask architecture learns robust, domain-agnostic feature representations while respecting the distinct physical properties of each material class.
