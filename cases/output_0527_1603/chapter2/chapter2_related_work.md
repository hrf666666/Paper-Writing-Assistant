# 2. Related Work

## 2.1 Traditional Light Field Depth Estimation Methods

Many depth estimation methods for light-field cameras have been proposed to exploit the rich angular information. However, most of them rely on the Lambertian assumption and work poorly on glossy or specular surfaces <citation>["Wanner", "Heidrich", "EPI", "Lambertian"]</citation>, <citation>["Zhang", "EPI", "structure"]</citation>. Specifically, early geometric approaches primarily focus on the linear structures in epipolar plane images (EPIs). Wanner and Heidrich <citation>["Wanner", "Heidrich", "EPI", "variational"]</citation> formulated depth estimation as a variational optimization problem by calculating EPI slopes $m = \Delta x / \Delta u$, achieving remarkably accurate results on diffuse surfaces. Their method explicitly derives the orientation of EPI lines, which directly corresponds to the scene depth via triangulation. While this geometric cue is powerful and reliable for Lambertian regions, it fundamentally assumes strict photo-consistency across angular views. Consequently, when viewpoint-dependent highlights shift across angular samples, the linear EPI structures are severely distorted, rendering the slope calculation completely ineffective.

While EPI-based methods focus on angular slopes, other approaches extend to focal stacks to leverage defocus cues for depth inference. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "Hadap", "Malik", "defocus", "correspondence"]</citation> proposed a unified framework that combines both defocus and correspondence cues, demonstrating significant improvements in texture-less and occluded regions. By aggregating multi-view photo-consistency and depth-from-defocus responses, their method successfully addresses the ambiguity inherent in single-cue estimators. Moreover, they introduced a confidence map to fuse these cues adaptively. Nonetheless, both EPI slope extraction and defocus response aggregation inherently presume that the radiance of a 3D point remains invariant across different viewpoints and focal depths. Therefore, their method fails if the scene contains severe specularities or subsurface scattering, as the view-invariance is dramatically violated, leading to erroneous depth hypotheses.

To tackle the violations caused by complex reflectance, recent traditional works have attempted to incorporate physical optics and reflectance models into light-field shape acquisition. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "Hadap", "Malik", "shape", "shading", "dichromatic"]</citation> introduced the dichromatic reflection model to separate diffuse and specular components, enabling shape recovery for certain glossy surfaces. By assuming that the BRDFs $f_r(\omega_i, \omega_o)$ of different views lie on a line in the color space, they successfully decoupled the specular highlights from the diffuse albedo. Furthermore, Shi et al. <citation>["Shi", "BRDF", "specular", "light field"]</citation> extended this physical formulation by employing a more general BRDF representation to model complex specular reflections, aiming to recover both shape and reflectance simultaneously. However, the dichromatic model fails to hold for materials like metals or complex scattering media. Therefore, their methods fail if the BRDFs in different views do not strictly adhere to the predefined linear or simplified lobe constraints, limiting their generalization to real-world mixed scenes.

In short, traditional light-field depth estimation methods suffer from two critical limitations when applied to complex real-world environments. On the one hand, geometric methods strongly depend on the Lambertian assumption. In non-Lambertian regions, the view-consistency is dramatically disrupted by specular shifts and scattering blurs, causing the EPI slope extraction to completely fail and producing severe "texture copying" artifacts. On the other hand, traditional physical optics models, such as complex BRDF formulations, involve extremely high computational complexity and are highly sensitive to noise. They struggle to effectively process complex mixed scenes where diffuse, specular, and scattering components coexist, lacking a unified physical representation mechanism. In contrast, our model establishes a unified mathematical and physical framework based on a dual-mask physical modeling mechanism inspired by MRI angular frequency domain analysis. It explicitly addresses the "texture copying" failure through a pixel-wise component-aware adaptive depth estimation and confidence fusion strategy. Furthermore, we design a three-layer progressive inverse wavevector parsing algorithm tailored for limited angular sampling, which elegantly balances physical rigor with computational efficiency, achieving robust depth estimation in complex mixed scenes that has not been achieved by previous light-field approaches.

## 2.2 Deep Learning-based Geometric Light Field Depth Estimation

Following the limitations of hand-crafted geometric cues, deep learning-based methods have been extensively explored to implicitly learn the mapping from light field inputs to dense depth maps. Shin et al. <citation>["Shin", "EPINET", "EPI"]</citation> proposed the pioneering EPINET, which explicitly extracts horizontal and vertical epipolar plane image (EPI) patches and feeds them into a fully convolutional network to regress depth. By leveraging the powerful feature extraction capability of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), EPINET significantly improves depth accuracy on Lambertian regions compared to traditional variational methods. However, since it solely relies on local EPI patches, it struggles to capture global context and often produces blurred depth boundaries in complex scenes.

To address the limited receptive field of 2D EPI patches, subsequent works extend the input representations to exploit spatial-angular correlations more comprehensively. For instance, Liang et al. <citation>["Liang", "LFNet", "macropixel"]</citation> introduced LFNet, which stacks sub-aperture images or macropixels and employs 3D CNNs to jointly extract spatial and angular features. Similarly, other approaches <citation>["Meng", "3DCNN", "PSMNet"]</citation> adapt stereo matching architectures to light fields by constructing 3D cost volumes across multiple viewpoints. These 3D convolutional architectures successfully aggregate multi-view geometric information, yielding remarkably accurate and reliable depth maps in general scenes. Nonetheless, the heavy computational overhead of 3D convolutions and the implicit feature aggregation make them highly sensitive to angular inconsistencies caused by non-Lambertian reflections.

More recently, to capture long-range dependencies and global angular correlations, Transformer-based architectures have been introduced into light field depth estimation. Liang et al. <citation>["Liang", "LFT", "Transformer"]</citation> proposed the Light Field Transformer (LFT), which utilizes intra- and inter-view self-attention mechanisms to model the complex spatial-angular relationships without the inductive bias of convolutions. By dynamically aggregating features across the entire angular domain, LFT achieves state-of-the-art performance and demonstrates superior generalization on challenging occlusions and textureless regions. While attention mechanisms effectively enhance the representation of angular variations, they still treat viewpoint-dependent intensity changes as generic feature variations rather than physically distinct reflectance components.

In parallel with single-cue networks, dual-branch architectures have been developed to mimic the physical combination of defocus and correspondence cues. Representative works <citation>["dual", "defocus", "correspondence", "DLF"]</citation> design parallel branches to extract features from focal stacks (defocus cues) and sub-aperture images or EPIs (correspondence cues), followed by a fusion module to integrate these complementary geometric signals. This dual-branch strategy explicitly addresses the ambiguity in textureless or occluded regions where a single cue fails, significantly improving depth robustness. Despite these architectural advancements, the fusion is typically performed in a purely data-driven manner, lacking explicit physical constraints to decouple the mixed reflectance signals in the feature space.

Despite the remarkable progress, these purely data-driven geometric methods share fundamental limitations when applied to non-Lambertian and mixed scenes. First, purely data-driven methods lack physical optical constraints, simply treating the viewpoint inconsistencies caused by non-Lambertian reflections as noise or outliers, and failing to decouple the reflection components at the physical level. Consequently, their accuracy drops dramatically in Non-Lambertian and Mixed scenes (e.g., baseline models suffer from a high MAE of up to 0.411 in non-Lambertian regions). Second, these methods heavily rely on large-scale annotated data and are highly prone to overfitting on small-sample non-Lambertian datasets. Furthermore, the black-box nature of these networks lacks interpretability, making it difficult to generalize to unseen complex material mixed scenes, and thus failing to achieve robust estimation for mixed scenes with a single unified model. In contrast, our proposed unified dual-mask physical model tackles these challenges by explicitly formulating the non-Lambertian reflections via an MRI-like angular frequency analysis, decoupling the reflectance components to provide a solid theoretical foundation. Coupled with our pixel-wise component-aware adaptive depth estimation and three-layer progressive inverse wavevector parsing algorithm, our approach successfully balances physical rigor and computational efficiency, avoiding overfitting risks and enabling robust depth estimation across diverse mixed scenes with a single model.

## 2.3 Physics-Informed and Material-Aware Deep Light Field Estimation

To explicitly tackle the violation of photo-consistency caused by complex reflectance, early physics-informed approaches formulate depth estimation as an inverse rendering problem. Tao et al. <citation>["Tao", "dichromatic", "reflectance"]</citation> proposed a pioneering shape-from-light-field method based on the dichromatic reflection model. By explicitly decoupling the diffuse and specular components in the angular domain, their method successfully recovers depth for glossy dielectric surfaces. However, the dichromatic model fails to hold for materials like metals or complex translucent media. Therefore, their method fails if the BRDFs in different views do not lie on a line, limiting its generalization to general 1-lobe BRDFs or urban mixed scenes where multiple reflectance properties coexist.

While physics-based decoupling relies on strict analytical models, recent deep learning frameworks attempt to implicitly learn material properties to guide depth regression. For instance, material-aware deep architectures <citation>["material-aware", "deep", "classification"]</citation> introduce an auxiliary classification branch to predict pixel-wise material labels (e.g., diffuse, specular, transparent). The network then adaptively selects different cost aggregation strategies based on these predictions. Although this strategy remarkably improves depth accuracy on specific non-Lambertian regions, it primarily relies on hard classification. Consequently, it struggles to handle mixed boundaries where a single pixel captures a superposition of multiple materials. Furthermore, these methods lack a unified physical mask mechanism to simultaneously characterize pure diffuse, specular, and scattering properties, leading to sub-optimal feature fusion and depth discontinuities at material transitions.

To further exploit the angular signatures of non-Lambertian surfaces without explicit material labeling, other approaches leverage frequency-domain analysis and physics-constrained neural networks. Recent works <citation>["frequency", "rendering", "equation"]</citation> integrate the physical rendering equation into deep networks or apply Fourier analysis on epipolar plane images (EPIs) to separate high-frequency specular highlights from low-frequency diffuse textures. By analyzing the angular frequency shifts, these methods can partially mitigate the "texture copying" artifacts in specular regions. Since BRDF estimation is a very under-constrained problem, directly estimating full BRDF parameters or performing complex physical rendering within a neural network is highly ill-posed under limited angular sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$). The computational complexity of these physical parameter estimations is prohibitively high, making them unsuitable for real-time applications and prone to overfitting on small-sample non-Lambertian datasets.

In short, existing physics-informed and material-aware methods face two fundamental bottlenecks. On the one hand, current material-aware approaches predominantly adopt hard classification, which fails to address the mixed boundary problem and lacks a unified physical masking mechanism (such as dielectric and angular direction masks) to jointly represent diverse reflectance characteristics. On the other hand, under limited angular sampling, directly estimating BRDF parameters suffers from severe ill-posedness. The high computational complexity of existing physical models precludes real-time processing, and they lack an elegant mechanism to balance physical rigor with computational efficiency. In contrast, our unified dual-mask physical model can work on general mixed scenes encompassing pure diffuse, specular, and scattering materials, and can also recover pixel-wise component confidence in addition to depth, which has not been achieved by previous light field depth estimation approaches. Furthermore, by proposing a pixel-wise component-aware adaptive depth estimation strategy and a three-stage progressive inverse wavevector parsing algorithm, our approach effectively resolves the ill-posedness under limited angular sampling. This elegantly balances physical rigor and computational efficiency, avoiding the overfitting risks of purely data-driven methods on small-sample non-Lambertian data.

Although physics-based inverse rendering and deep learning-based implicit methods have advanced light field depth estimation, they either suffer from restricted generalization due to strict analytical assumptions or lack explicit physical constraints, leading to severe performance degradation when handling photo-consistency violations in complex non-Lambertian and mixed scenes. Inspired by these observations, we propose a unified dual-mask physical modeling approach that addresses the aforementioned limitations by integrating an MRI-inspired angular frequency dual-mask mechanism for universal reflectance decoupling, a pixel-wise component-aware adaptive depth estimation with confidence fusion to overcome epipolar plane image texture replication failures, and a three-tier progressive inverse wavevector parsing algorithm to elegantly balance physical rigor with computational efficiency under limited angular sampling.
