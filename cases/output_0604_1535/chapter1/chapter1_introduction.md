# 1. Introduction

Light field (LF) imaging captures both the spatial and angular information of light rays, enabling precise depth estimation without active illumination <citation>["light field imaging", "depth estimation"]</citation>. Accurate LF depth estimation serves as a core component for various downstream applications, including 3D scene reconstruction, autonomous navigation, and immersive virtual reality <citation>["3D reconstruction", "autonomous driving"]</citation>. Among existing paradigms, Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) based methods dominate the field by exploiting the linear structures formed by corresponding pixels across angular views <citation>["epipolar plane image", "EPI slope"]</citation>. These methods inherently rely on the Lambertian assumption, which posits that surface radiance remains consistent across different viewpoints.

However, real-world environments are replete with non-Lambertian surfaces (e.g., specular, translucent, and scattering materials) and complex textures, which severely violate the Lambertian assumption. When light interacts with specular or volumetric scattering surfaces, viewpoint consistency is broken at the physical level. Specifically, specular reflections shift dynamically with the viewing angle, while scattering media lack a single definitive surface depth. This physical violation manifests in the EPI domain as the fracture of linear structures, often forming ambiguous X-shaped patterns instead of continuous lines <citation>["specular reflection", "volumetric scattering"]</citation>. The severity of this degradation is substantial. Extensive empirical evaluations demonstrate that while EPI-based models achieve an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.133 in mixed urban scenarios, their performance deteriorates significantly in non-Lambertian regions, yielding an MAE of 0.411 (far exceeding the acceptable threshold of 0.25). Similarly, complex textures in Lambertian regions induce EPI slope ambiguity, resulting in an MAE of 0.387 against a target of 0.16.

Current approaches attempt to mitigate these issues through architectural enhancements, such as dual-branch networks, attention mechanisms, or defocus-based auxiliary heads <citation>["dual-branch architecture", "attention mechanism"]</citation>. Systematic investigations involving over 130 experimental trials across 16 distinct research directions reveal that purely data-driven or architectural modifications cannot overcome the physical boundaries of EPI methods. When the underlying physical assumption is invalidated, no hyperparameter tuning or loss function combination can recover the destroyed angular constraints. Some methods resort to frequency-domain analysis, such as 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), to separate material properties <citation>["2D-DFT", "frequency domain analysis"]</citation>. Yet, these frequency-based techniques fail under the low angular resolution (e.g., $9 \times 9$ discrete sampling) typical of practical LF cameras, rendering them impractical for real-world deployment. The absence of a reliable material-aware routing mechanism and a unified training strategy for multi-domain LF data leaves a significant gap in handling heterogeneous scenes.

To address these physical and architectural bottlenecks, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Instead of blindly applying EPI slope extraction across all pixels, our framework explicitly decouples the angular signal and defines the physical boundaries of EPI validity. By extracting geometric angular features and employing a domain-balanced sampling strategy, the proposed model dynamically routes pixels through specialized depth estimation branches based on their physical reflectance properties.

The specific contributions are summarized as follows:
* We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition and geometric feature-based material classification method that decouples the LF angular signal into DC, linear, and residual components. By extracting geometric angular features instead of relying on low-resolution 2D-DFT, this method achieves precise pixel-level routing for Lambertian and non-Lambertian surfaces, providing a reliable prior for dual-branch depth estimation.
* We introduce a unified dual-mask depth estimation framework integrated with a domain-balanced sampling strategy that addresses the physical boundaries of EPI hypothesis failure. By combining a 4-direction EPI architecture with dynamic cross-domain exposure balance, this framework achieves robust depth prediction and substantially mitigates performance degradation in non-Lambertian and complex-texture scenarios.

Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily extract depth cues from stereo correspondences, defocus blur, and EPI slopes using hand-crafted operators like the structure tensor <citation>. Methodologically, these approaches inherently depend on the Lambertian assumption, treating angular radiance variations as strictly linear. Consequently, they exhibit substantial degradation when encountering non-Lambertian surfaces, where specular shifts and volumetric scattering introduce severe non-linear angular distortions. At the technical level, traditional frequency-domain analyses, such as 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (2D-DFT), require high angular sampling rates to resolve frequency components accurately. In practical scenarios with low angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$ discrete sampling), 2D-DFT becomes ill-posed and fails to distinguish material properties, leaving these methods without reliable mechanisms to identify and process non-Lambertian regions.

To mitigate the limitations of hand-crafted features, early deep learning-based methods, typified by EPINet and its variants, formulate depth estimation as an end-to-end regression task by extracting hierarchical features from EPIs <citation>. While these convolutional architectures improve accuracy on standard Lambertian datasets, they suffer from two significant methodological flaws. First, they treat depth estimation purely as a data-driven mapping problem, ignoring the underlying physical imaging model. When the EPI line structure fractures into ambiguous X-shaped patterns due to specular or scattering effects, these networks lack the physical priors to correct erroneous slope estimations. Second, early models lack material awareness and domain-balanced optimization. Since non-Lambertian datasets are severely data-scarce (e.g., containing only 4 training scenes compared to 170 in UrbanLF-Syn), standard training strategies cause the network to overfit the dominant Lambertian domain. This domain imbalance results in significant performance drops on non-Lambertian and complex mixed scenes.

Recent advancements attempt to address non-Lambertian challenges by introducing complex architectural modifications, including dual-branch networks, spatial-angular attention mechanisms, defocus-based auxiliary heads, and composite multi-term loss functions <citation>. Despite these architectural enhancements, their methodological premise remains flawed: they attempt to force-fit fractured EPI structures through increased network capacity rather than addressing the physical boundaries of EPI failure. Systematic evaluations involving 132 experiments across 16 primary research directions reveal a strict theoretical ceiling for EPI-based methods. Specifically, when physical constraints are violated by mirror reflections or body scattering, increasing model complexity merely amplifies the fitting of noisy X-shaped artifacts rather than recovering true depth. Technically, these recent methods also struggle with effective prior routing. Without accurate pixel-level material classification to guide dual-branch architectures, the networks fail to apply specialized processing to non-Lambertian regions. Existing frequency-based classification modules remain ineffective at low angular resolutions, rendering the dual-branch designs suboptimal.

The aforementioned limitations highlight a persistent gap in current light field depth estimation: the lack of a unified framework that explicitly models the physical boundaries of EPI failure, provides reliable material priors under low angular resolutions, and maintains cross-domain generalization. Therefore, there is a clear need for a unified dual-mask physical model that integrates geometric angular feature classification with domain-balanced training to achieve robust depth estimation across diverse Lambertian and non-Lambertian surfaces.

In this paper, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for light field depth estimation that addresses the physical breakdown of Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) assumptions in non-Lambertian and complex textured scenes. Unlike conventional data-driven approaches that treat angular radiance variations as strictly linear, our method integrates a physical imaging model with a dual-branch neural architecture. We introduce a three-layer angular signal decomposition module that decouples the angular signal into direct current (DC), linear, and residual components. Based on the extracted geometric features, a pixel-wise dual-mask generation module produces precise Lambertian and non-Lambertian masks. These masks route the features into two specialized branches: a Lambertian EPI branch incorporating plane regular sampling operator (PRSO) priors, and a non-Lambertian geometric branch leveraging X-shaped patterns and bimodal distributions. A mask-weighted fusion module subsequently integrates the dual-branch predictions. To overcome data scarcity and domain imbalance, we employ a domain-balanced sampling strategy across multiple light field datasets.

- **Contribution 1**: We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition and pixel-wise dual-mask generation mechanism. This approach resolves the ill-posed nature of 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (2D-DFT) under low angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$) and mitigates the label noise inherent in scene-level supervision. By decoupling the angular signal into DC illumination, disparity magnitude $P(x,y)$, and material proportion $M(x,y)$, and extracting geometric features such as angular gradients, the model achieves high-precision pixel-level material classification, providing a reliable prior routing for depth estimation.
- **Contribution 2**: We develop a dual-branch physical-aware architecture with a mask-weighted fusion module to handle the structural fracture of EPI lines. When specular reflections and volumetric scattering break the angular cone constraint and form ambiguous X-shaped patterns, the Lambertian branch utilizes continuous and piecewise smooth geometric surface priors, while the non-Lambertian branch applies geometric convolutions to process bimodal distributions. A channel-balanced projection layer unifies the feature dimensions before mask-weighted fusion, significantly improving depth estimation accuracy in mixed scenes.
- **Contribution 3**: We introduce a domain-balanced mixed training strategy and systematically delineate the physical boundaries of EPI assumption failures. By constructing a unified light field dataset loader and employing a weighted random sampler, the model maintains cross-domain exposure balance during training. Through 132 experiments across 16 research directions, we establish a comprehensive negative result archive that identifies the architectural and physical root causes of EPI degradation, defining the theoretical performance ceiling for EPI slope-based depth estimation methods.

Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.133 and a mixed urban scene MAE of 0.081, validating its effectiveness in complex mixed environments. The systematic boundary analysis further reveals the physical limitations imposed by complex textures and extreme non-Lambertian materials, providing important insights for light field research.
