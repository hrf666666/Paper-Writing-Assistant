# 3. Methodology

In this section, we present the overall architecture of the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Different from previous works that treat all surface reflectances uniformly or rely on heuristic post-processing, our framework explicitly integrates a physical reflectance model into a dual-path neural architecture. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the network comprises a Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition & Mask Generation Module, a Lambertian EPI Branch, a Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch, and a Balanced Projection & Mask-Weighted Fusion Module. This design enables the adaptive routing and specialized processing of features based on the underlying material properties, ensuring robust depth recovery across heterogeneous scenes.

The input to our model consists of densely sampled light field data with a $9 \times 9$ angular resolution, represented as a tensor $\mathcal{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 9 \times 9 \times H \times W \times 3}$, where $B$ is the batch size, and $H$ and $W$ denote the spatial height and width, respectively. To capture comprehensive epipolar geometry, we extract 4-directional Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) from the angular grid, serving as the foundational input for the Lambertian EPI Branch. Prior to feeding the data into the network, we apply tone mapping augmentation to simulate diverse illumination conditions and enhance the generalization to varying exposure levels. This preprocessing step ensures that the subsequent physical decomposition remains stable under complex real-world lighting variations. The preprocessed light field tensor is then simultaneously directed to the decomposition module and the Lambertian branch for parallel feature extraction.

The core data flow follows a "decomposition $\rightarrow$ dual-branch parallel extraction $\rightarrow$ mask-guided fusion" topology. Initially, the light field data enters the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition & Mask Generation Module. Motivated by the physical image formation process, this module decomposes the angular signal into three components: the direct current (DC) term representing ambient illumination, the linear term encoding parallax, and the residual term capturing material-specific reflectance. Mathematically, the angular intensity $I(u,v)$ at viewpoint $(u,v)$ is modeled as $I(u,v) = I_{DC} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v)$, where $I_{DC}$ is the ambient light, $a$ and $b$ are linear coefficients corresponding to depth, and $\varepsilon(u,v)$ is the residual reflecting material properties. From this decomposition, we compute geometric angular features, such as the angular gradient $P(x,y) = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and correlation coherence, yielding Geometric Angular Features $\mathcal{F}_{geo} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times H \times W \times C_{geo}}$. These features are fed into a classifier to generate a Pixel-wise Dual-Mask $\mathcal{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$, which partitions the scene into Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. Guided by $\mathcal{M}$, the data flows into two specialized branches. The Lambertian EPI Branch leverages the linear EPI structures and incorporates continuous depth priors to extract Lam Depth Features $\mathcal{F}_{lam} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{lam} \times H \times W}$, enhancing surface smoothness. Conversely, the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch processes $\mathcal{F}_{geo}$ to capture complex X-shaped EPI patterns without relying on the violated linear assumption, producing NL Depth Features $\mathcal{F}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{nl} \times H \times W}$.

To integrate the heterogeneous features from the dual branches, we employ the Balanced Projection & Mask-Weighted Fusion Module. Different from previous works that suffer from severe channel imbalance and feature confusion when directly concatenating Lambertian and non-Lambertian representations, our module explicitly aligns the feature spaces before aggregation. Specifically, we project $\mathcal{F}_{lam}$ and $\mathcal{F}_{nl}$ into a unified balanced dimension ($C_{fuse} = 96$) using $1 \times 1$ convolutions. The projected features are then aggregated via pixel-wise weighting guided by the Pixel-wise Dual-Mask $\mathcal{M}$:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{fuse} = \mathcal{M}_{lam} \odot \text{Proj}_{lam}(\mathcal{F}_{lam}) + \mathcal{M}_{nl} \odot \text{Proj}_{nl}(\mathcal{F}_{nl}) $$
where $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product, $\mathcal{M}_{lam}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{nl}$ are the respective channels of the dual-mask, and $\text{Proj}(\cdot)$ represents the projection operation. The fused depth feature $\mathcal{F}_{fuse} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 96 \times H \times W}$ is subsequently passed through a Depth Decoder, which consists of progressive upsampling and convolutional layers, to regress the Final Depth Map $\mathcal{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$. This mask-weighted fusion mechanism ensures that the final depth prediction strictly adheres to the physical characteristics of each local region, yielding highly accurate and continuous depth maps for downstream 3D video systems.

## 3.2 三层角度信号分解与掩码生成模块

Conventional light field depth estimation methods predominantly rely on the Lambertian assumption, treating all surface reflectances uniformly. This simplification leads to severe depth distortions in regions with specular reflections or complex materials. To address this limitation, we develop the Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition and Mask Generation Module. Unlike previous approaches that implicitly learn material differences or apply heuristic post-processing, this module explicitly integrates a physical reflectance model to decouple the angular signals. The primary objective is to decompose the input light field into distinct physical components, extract geometry-aware angular features, and generate pixel-level dual masks. These masks serve as routing mechanisms for the subsequent dual-path architecture, ensuring that Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions are processed by their respective specialized branches.

The module takes the densely sampled $9 \times 9$ light field data $\mathcal{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 9 \times 9 \times H \times W \times 3}$ as input. For a specific spatial location $(x,y)$, the angular signal across the view grid $(u,v) \in \{-4, \dots, 4\}^2$ is denoted as $I_{x,y}(u,v)$. Based on the physical image formation model, the angular intensity variation can be approximated by a first-order Taylor expansion combined with a residual term. We formulate the three-layer decomposition as:
$$ I_{x,y}(u,v) = I_{dc}(x,y) + a(x,y) \cdot u + b(x,y) \cdot v + \varepsilon_{x,y}(u,v) $$
where $I_{dc}(x,y)$ represents the direct current (DC) component, corresponding to the ambient illumination and base albedo. The linear terms $a(x,y)$ and $b(x,y)$ denote the angular gradients along the $u$ and $v$ axes, which are intrinsically linked to the local disparity and depth. The residual term $\varepsilon_{x,y}(u,v)$ captures the high-frequency angular variations caused by non-Lambertian reflections, such as specular highlights, as well as sensor noise. To solve for these components, we employ a least-squares fitting approach over the $9 \times 9$ angular grid for each spatial pixel, effectively separating the diffuse and specular reflectance properties.

Following the decomposition, we compute geometric angular features to quantify the material properties at each pixel. The magnitude of the linear angular gradient is calculated as:
$$ P(x,y) = \sqrt{a(x,y)^2 + b(x,y)^2} $$
A higher value of $P(x,y)$ indicates a strong linear epipolar structure, characteristic of Lambertian surfaces. Conversely, non-Lambertian regions exhibit significant energy in the residual term. We define the angular gradient feature, denoted as $F_{ang}(x,y)$, by combining the gradient magnitude with the variance of the residual term $\text{Var}(\varepsilon_{x,y})$ and the angular correlation coherence. These metrics are concatenated and projected through a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer to produce the comprehensive geometric angular feature map $\mathcal{F}_{geo} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times H \times W \times C_{geo}}$, where $C_{geo}$ is the channel dimension of the geometric features. This feature map encapsulates the structural deviations from the ideal Lambertian model, providing essential cues for the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch.

To guide the dual-branch feature extraction and fusion, the module generates pixel-level dual masks that classify each spatial location into Lambertian or non-Lambertian categories. We utilize a lightweight spatial classifier operating on the geometric angular feature map. The Lambertian mask $M_{lam}$ is computed as:
$$ M_{lam}(x,y) = \sigma(\alpha \cdot F_{ang}(x,y) + \beta) $$
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the sigmoid activation function, and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are learnable parameters that adaptively threshold the angular gradient features. The non-Lambertian mask is subsequently derived as $M_{nl}(x,y) = 1 - M_{lam}(x,y)$. The final dual-mask output is formatted as $\mathcal{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$. During training, we introduce an auxiliary pseudo-label supervision mechanism. By comparing $\mathcal{M}$ with ground-truth material segmentation or heuristically derived pseudo-labels, we apply a binary cross-entropy loss to regularize the mask generation, ensuring accurate boundary delineation between different reflectance types.

In the overall data flow, the input light field data is fed in parallel into this decomposition module and the Lambertian EPI Branch. The extracted geometric feature map $\mathcal{F}_{geo}$ is directed to the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch to capture material-specific depth cues, while the dual-mask $\mathcal{M}$ is forwarded to the Balanced Projection and Mask-Weighted Fusion Module for final feature integration. By explicitly decoupling the angular signals based on physical priors, this module prevents the contamination of depth features by specular reflections. This explicit physical decomposition yields a more robust feature representation, enabling the subsequent branches to focus on their respective domain-specific geometric structures without interference.

## 3.3 Lambertian EPI 分支

Following the generation of the dual masks, the light field data is routed into specialized processing paths. The Lambertian EPI Branch is designed to handle regions that adhere to the Lambertian reflectance assumption. In these regions, Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) exhibit distinct linear slope structures, where the slope is inversely proportional to the scene depth. Conventional light field depth estimation methods typically apply 3D or 4D convolutions directly to EPIs, implicitly learning the slope patterns. However, this purely data-driven approach often neglects the inherent piecewise smooth physical prior of Lambertian surfaces, leading to depth inconsistencies and staircase artifacts in weakly textured areas. To address this limitation, we develop the Lambertian EPI Branch, which explicitly integrates a continuous piecewise smooth depth prior via a Planar Regularized Sampling Operator (PRSO) to enhance depth consistency and surface smoothness.

The module takes the EPI data $\mathcal{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{epi} \times H \times W}$ and the Lambertian mask $\mathcal{M}_{lam} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ generated from the preceding decomposition module as inputs. Here, $B$ denotes the batch size, $C_{epi}$ represents the channel dimension of the EPI features, and $H$ and $W$ are the spatial height and width, respectively. The processing pipeline consists of an EPI encoder for initial slope feature extraction and a PRSO module for prior integration.

First, the EPI encoder extracts the foundational slope features from the input EPI data. This is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{epi} = \text{EPI\_Encoder}(\mathcal{E}) $$
where $\mathcal{F}_{epi} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{lam} \times H \times W}$ denotes the extracted EPI features, and $C_{lam}$ is the channel dimension of the Lambertian depth features. The EPI\_Encoder comprises a series of 2D convolutional layers optimized to capture the directional gradients along the epipolar lines.

To enforce the continuous piecewise smooth prior, we predict an initial displacement field $\mathcal{D}_{prior} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$, which encodes the local horizontal and vertical parallax shifts. The Lambertian mask $\mathcal{M}_{lam}$ is utilized to spatially constrain this prediction, ensuring that the subsequent regularization operates exclusively on valid Lambertian regions. This is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{D}_{prior} = \text{Disp\_Net}(\mathcal{F}_{epi}) \odot \mathcal{M}_{lam} $$
where $\text{Disp\_Net}$ is a convolutional network predicting the raw displacement vectors, and $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication broadcasted across the channel dimension. 

Different from previous works that rely solely on implicit convolutional learning, our module explicitly incorporates the PRSO to regularize the feature space using $\mathcal{D}_{prior}$. For a specific spatial location $\mathbf{p} = (x,y)$, the displacement vector is denoted as $\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{p}) = (d_x(\mathbf{p}), d_y(\mathbf{p}))$. The PRSO aggregates features within a local neighborhood $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{p})$ by warping the coordinates according to the displacement field and applying a regularization weight. The mathematical formulation of the PRSO is given by:
$$ \text{PRSO}(\mathcal{F}_{epi}, \mathcal{D}_{prior})(\mathbf{p}) = \sum_{\mathbf{q} \in \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{p})} \omega(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}) \mathcal{F}_{epi}(\mathbf{q} + \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{q})) $$
where $\mathbf{q}$ represents a neighboring pixel of $\mathbf{p}$, and $\mathcal{F}_{epi}(\mathbf{q} + \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{q}))$ is evaluated using bilinear interpolation to ensure differentiability. The regularization weight $\omega(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q})$ is computed based on the spatial distance and the displacement consistency between $\mathbf{p}$ and $\mathbf{q}$:
$$ \omega(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}) = \frac{\exp\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{q}\|^2}{2\sigma_s^2} - \frac{\|\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{p}) - \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{q})\|^2}{2\sigma_d^2}\right)}{\sum_{\mathbf{k} \in \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{p})} \exp\left(-\frac{\|\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{k}\|^2}{2\sigma_s^2} - \frac{\|\mathbf{d}(\mathbf{p}) - \mathbf{d}(\mathbf{k})\|^2}{2\sigma_d^2}\right)} $$
where $\sigma_s$ and $\sigma_d$ are scaling parameters controlling the spatial and displacement variances, respectively. This weighting scheme ensures that features are aggregated predominantly from pixels that share similar depth hypotheses, thereby preserving depth discontinuities while smoothing planar regions.

The regularized features are then added to the original EPI features to form the enhanced Lambertian depth feature map:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{lam} = \mathcal{F}_{epi} + \text{PRSO}(\mathcal{F}_{epi}, \mathcal{D}_{prior}) $$
Finally, a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is applied to regress the preliminary Lambertian depth map:
$$ \mathcal{D}_{lam} = \text{MLP}(\mathcal{F}_{lam}) $$
where $\mathcal{D}_{lam} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ represents the estimated depth for the Lambertian regions.

The output feature map $\mathcal{F}_{lam}$ is subsequently forwarded to the Balanced Projection and Mask-Weighted Fusion Module. By explicitly embedding the PRSO into the feature extraction process, the Lambertian EPI Branch effectively mitigates the depth fluctuations commonly observed in textureless Lambertian areas. This explicit physical regularization distinguishes our approach from standard EPI processing pipelines, yielding geometrically consistent and surface-smooth depth estimations for regions governed by Lambertian reflectance.

## 3.4 Non-Lambertian 几何分支

While the Lambertian EPI branch effectively models regions adhering to the linear epipolar constraint, it inherently fails in Non-Lambertian areas where the reflectance properties violate this assumption. In scenes containing specular highlights, transparent objects, or complex anisotropic materials, the EPI structures degenerate from distinct linear slopes into X-shaped crossing patterns or bi-modal and multi-modal distributions. Conventional light field depth estimation methods, which predominantly rely on EPI slope extraction or purely data-driven feature learning, suffer from severe depth ambiguities, inversions, and blurring in these regions. To address this limitation, we design the Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch, which explicitly abandons the compromised linear EPI assumption and instead leverages angular geometric statistics to infer depth and material properties.

The Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch takes the geometric angular feature map $\mathcal{F}_{geo} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{geo} \times H \times W}$ and the Non-Lambertian mask $\mathcal{M}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ generated from the preceding three-layer angular signal decomposition module as inputs. Here, $B$ is the batch size, $C_{geo}$ denotes the channel dimension of the geometric features, and $H$ and $W$ represent the spatial height and width. Unlike the Lambertian branch that processes raw EPI structures, this branch operates on the decomposed geometric features, which encapsulate the residual and angular variation information essential for Non-Lambertian analysis.

To capture the distinct X-shaped and bi-modal patterns characteristic of Non-Lambertian surfaces, we first extract explicit geometric descriptors from $\mathcal{F}_{geo}$. Specifically, we compute the angular gradient $\mathbf{G} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{geo} \times H \times W}$, the correlation coherence $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{geo} \times H \times W}$, and the coefficient of variation $\mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{geo} \times H \times W}$. The angular gradient measures the rate of intensity variation across the angular domain, highlighting the sharp transitions in specular reflections. It is formulated as:
$$ \mathbf{G}_{x,y} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{(u,v) \in \Omega} \left\| \nabla_{u,v} \mathcal{F}_{geo}^{u,v}(x,y) \right\|_2 $$
where $(x,y)$ denotes the spatial coordinates, $(u,v)$ represents the angular coordinates within the local angular patch $\Omega$, $\mathcal{F}_{geo}^{u,v}(x,y)$ is the feature at view $(u,v)$, and $\nabla_{u,v}$ is the gradient operator along the angular dimensions. 

The correlation coherence $\mathbf{C}$ evaluates the structural consistency among different angular views, which is significantly degraded in Non-Lambertian regions due to view-dependent reflectance. It is defined as the average Pearson correlation coefficient between each angular view and the angular mean feature over a local spatial window $\mathcal{W}$:
$$ \mathbf{C}_{x,y} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{(u,v) \in \Omega} \frac{ \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{W}} (\mathcal{F}_{geo}^{u,v}(i,j) - \mu_{u,v}) (\bar{\mathcal{F}}_{geo}(i,j) - \bar{\mu}) }{ \sqrt{\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{W}} (\mathcal{F}_{geo}^{u,v}(i,j) - \mu_{u,v})^2} \sqrt{\sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{W}} (\bar{\mathcal{F}}_{geo}(i,j) - \bar{\mu})^2} } $$
where $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_{geo}(i,j)$ is the mean angular feature at spatial location $(i,j)$, and $\mu_{u,v}$ and $\bar{\mu}$ are the local mean values of $\mathcal{F}_{geo}^{u,v}$ and $\bar{\mathcal{F}}_{geo}$ within the spatial window $\mathcal{W}$ centered at $(x,y)$, respectively. 

Additionally, the coefficient of variation $\mathbf{V}$ quantifies the relative dispersion of the angular features, providing a normalized measure of reflectance variance:
$$ \mathbf{V}_{x,y} = \frac{\sigma_{\Omega}(\mathcal{F}_{geo}(x,y))}{\mu_{\Omega}(\mathcal{F}_{geo}(x,y)) + \epsilon} $$
where $\mu_{\Omega}(\cdot)$ and $\sigma_{\Omega}(\cdot)$ denote the mean and standard deviation computed across the angular domain $\Omega$ for the fixed spatial location $(x,y)$, and $\epsilon$ is a small constant to prevent division by zero. These geometric descriptors ($\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{C}$, and $\mathbf{V}$) collectively provide a comprehensive representation of the Non-Lambertian reflectance properties without relying on linear epipolar constraints.

Subsequently, these descriptors are fed into a Geometry Encoder (Geo_Encoder) to extract high-dimensional material and depth features. To ensure that the network focuses exclusively on the Non-Lambertian regions and suppresses irrelevant background noise from Lambertian areas, we apply feature modulation using the Non-Lambertian mask $\mathcal{M}_{nl}$. The modulated feature extraction process is expressed as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{nl} = \text{Geo\_Encoder}\left( \mathbf{G} \odot \mathcal{M}_{nl}, \mathbf{C} \odot \mathcal{M}_{nl}, \mathbf{V} \odot \mathcal{M}_{nl} \right) $$
where $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication along the spatial dimensions, and $\mathcal{F}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{nl} \times H \times W}$ is the output Non-Lambertian depth and material feature map, with $C_{nl}$ being its channel dimension. The Geo_Encoder is implemented as a cascade of convolutional layers equipped with spatial and channel attention mechanisms, enabling the adaptive aggregation of local geometric contexts.

To facilitate optimization and provide auxiliary supervision during training, an auxiliary depth regression head is attached to the Geo_Encoder. A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) maps the extracted features $\mathcal{F}_{nl}$ to an initial Non-Lambertian depth map $\mathcal{D}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$:
$$ \mathcal{D}_{nl} = \text{MLP}(\mathcal{F}_{nl}) $$
This auxiliary depth prediction encourages the Geo_Encoder to learn depth-discriminative geometric representations. However, the primary output of this branch is the feature map $\mathcal{F}_{nl}$, which is subsequently passed to the balanced projection and mask-weighted fusion module for final integration with the Lambertian features.

Different from previous works that attempt to uniformly process all regions with a single EPI-based network or rely on heuristic specular removal techniques, our Non-Lambertian Geometric Branch explicitly decouples the processing of complex reflectance. By leveraging angular geometric statistics rather than compromised linear EPI slopes, this module effectively captures the intrinsic material and depth cues in Non-Lambertian regions. This design not only prevents the propagation of depth errors caused by violated Lambertian assumptions but also provides complementary geometric features that significantly enhance the overall depth estimation accuracy in challenging real-world scenes.

## 3.5 平衡投影与掩码加权融合模块

Following the extraction of distinct features from the Lambertian EPI branch and the Non-Lambertian Geometric branch, a significant challenge emerges in integrating these heterogeneous representations. The Lambertian features $\mathcal{F}_{lam} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{lam} \times H \times W}$ and Non-Lambertian features $\mathcal{F}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{nl} \times H \times W}$ inherently exhibit severe channel imbalance ($C_{lam} \neq C_{nl}$) and semantic misalignment. Conventional fusion strategies, such as direct concatenation or global attention mechanisms, often lead to feature domination, where the branch with higher channel capacity or stronger activation suppresses the other. This results in depth degradation, particularly at the boundaries between diffuse and specular regions. To address this limitation, we design the Balanced Projection and Mask-Weighted Fusion Module, which aims to harmonize the channel dimensions and enforce physically grounded, pixel-level feature selection.

The module takes $\mathcal{F}_{lam}$ and $\mathcal{F}_{nl}$ as inputs, along with the dual masks $\mathcal{M}_{lam}, \mathcal{M}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ generated by the preceding three-layer angular signal decomposition module. Here, $B$ denotes the batch size, $H$ and $W$ represent the spatial height and width, and $C_{lam}$ and $C_{nl}$ are the respective channel dimensions of the two branches. To eliminate the channel imbalance, we first project both feature maps into a unified balanced dimension $C_{fuse} = 96$. This balanced projection is formulated as:
$$ \hat{\mathcal{F}}_{lam} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_{lam} * \mathcal{F}_{lam} + \mathbf{b}_{lam}) $$
$$ \hat{\mathcal{F}}_{nl} = \sigma(\mathbf{W}_{nl} * \mathcal{F}_{nl} + \mathbf{b}_{nl}) $$
where $*$ denotes the $1 \times 1$ convolution operation, $\mathbf{W}_{lam} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{fuse} \times C_{lam} \times 1 \times 1}$ and $\mathbf{W}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{fuse} \times C_{nl} \times 1 \times 1}$ are the learnable projection weights, $\mathbf{b}_{lam}$ and $\mathbf{b}_{nl}$ are the bias terms, and $\sigma$ represents the GELU activation function. The projected features $\hat{\mathcal{F}}_{lam}, \hat{\mathcal{F}}_{nl} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{fuse} \times H \times W}$ now possess identical channel depths, ensuring equal representational capacity and preventing gradient bias during backpropagation.

Different from previous works that rely on data-driven attention maps or simple additive fusion, our module explicitly leverages the physical dual masks to guide the integration process. The masks $\mathcal{M}_{lam}$ and $\mathcal{M}_{nl}$ act as spatial gates, enforcing that the depth estimation in diffuse regions relies predominantly on EPI slope features, while specular and complex material regions depend on geometric angular statistics. The pixel-level weighted fusion is computed as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{fuse} = \mathcal{M}_{lam} \odot \hat{\mathcal{F}}_{lam} + \mathcal{M}_{nl} \odot \hat{\mathcal{F}}_{nl} $$
where $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication (Hadamard product) with the masks broadcasted along the channel dimension $C_{fuse}$. The resulting fused depth feature $\mathcal{F}_{fuse} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 96 \times H \times W}$ effectively suppresses feature crosstalk, ensuring that erroneous EPI slopes in Non-Lambertian areas are masked out, and unreliable geometric features in Lambertian areas are ignored.

Finally, the fused feature map is passed through a depth decoder $\Psi$ to regress the continuous depth map. The decoder consists of a sequence of convolutional layers with residual connections, formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{D} = \Psi(\mathcal{F}_{fuse}; \mathbf{\Theta}_{dec}) $$
where $\mathbf{\Theta}_{dec}$ represents the parameters of the decoder, and $\mathcal{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ is the final predicted depth map. By explicitly decoupling the feature extraction and physically masking the fusion process, the proposed module yields highly accurate depth boundaries and preserves fine structural details in both Lambertian and Non-Lambertian regions, substantially improving the overall depth estimation performance compared to baseline fusion techniques.

## 3.6 Training Objective

### 3.6 Training Objective and Loss Function

The training objective of the proposed GeometricDualMask network is formulated to jointly optimize depth regression, material-aware mask prediction, and cross-domain feature alignment. Different from previous works that rely on a single homogeneous loss for light field depth estimation, our module explicitly decouples the optimization into physical property prediction and geometric depth regression. The overall loss function $\mathcal{L}_{total}$ is defined as a composite multi-term objective:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_d \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_m \mathcal{L}_{mask} + \lambda_{dom} \mathcal{L}_{domain} $$

where $\mathcal{L}_{depth}$, $\mathcal{L}_{mask}$, and $\mathcal{L}_{domain}$ denote the depth estimation loss, dual-mask prediction loss, and domain alignment loss, respectively. The scalar weights $\lambda_d$, $\lambda_m$, and $\lambda_{dom}$ balance the contributions of each term.

#### 3.6.1 Depth Estimation Loss with Physical Consistency

Standard L1 or L2 losses treat all pixels equally, which leads to blurred depth boundaries and inaccurate estimations in non-Lambertian regions where the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) slope assumption is physically violated. To address this, we employ a robust Smooth L1 loss for the primary depth regression, augmented with an edge-aware physical consistency regularization.

Let $\hat{D}$ and $D_{gt}$ be the predicted and ground-truth depth maps. The primary depth loss is:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{reg} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \text{SmoothL1}(\hat{D}_i, D_{gt,i}) $$

To enforce physical consistency, specifically preserving depth discontinuities at object boundaries and material transitions, we introduce an edge-aware regularization term $\mathcal{L}_{edge}$. This term penalizes depth gradients in flat regions while allowing sharp transitions at luminance edges.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{edge} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left\| \nabla \hat{D}_i \right\|_1 \odot \exp(-\alpha \left\| \nabla I_{center}_i \right\|_1) $$

where $\nabla$ denotes the spatial gradient operator, $I_{center}$ is the center view image, $\odot$ is the element-wise multiplication, and $\alpha$ is a scaling factor controlling the sensitivity to image edges. The total depth loss is $\mathcal{L}_{depth} = \mathcal{L}_{reg} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{edge}$. This formulation restricts depth smoothing across material boundaries, improving the structural integrity of the estimated depth maps in mixed and non-Lambertian scenes.

#### 3.6.2 Dual-Mask Prediction Loss

The dual-mask mechanism generates a parallax magnitude mask $P(x,y)$ and a material ratio mask $M(x,y)$ to route features adaptively. Since non-Lambertian regions (e.g., specular reflections, scattering) occupy a minor portion of the training data (e.g., only 4 scenes in the specific non-Lambertian dataset), standard cross-entropy loss suffers from severe class imbalance, causing the network to ignore minority pixels.

We combine Focal Loss and Lovász-Softmax Loss to optimize the mask predictions. Let $\hat{M}$ be the predicted material mask (Lambertian vs. Non-Lambertian) and $M_{gt}$ be the ground truth derived from the random forest classifier based on the three-layer angular signal decomposition $I(u,v) = DC + a\cdot u + b\cdot v + \epsilon(u,v)$. 

The Focal loss $\mathcal{L}_{focal}$ down-weights easily classified Lambertian pixels:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{focal} = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ M_{gt,i} (1 - \hat{M}_i)^\gamma \log(\hat{M}_i) + (1 - M_{gt,i}) \hat{M}_i^\gamma \log(1 - \hat{M}_i) \right] $$

where $\gamma$ is the focusing parameter. 

To optimize the Intersection over Union (IoU) directly and handle the spatial coherence of the masks, we incorporate the Lovász-Softmax loss $\mathcal{L}_{lovász}$, which is a differentiable surrogate for the Jaccard index:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{lovász} = \frac{1}{|C|} \sum_{c \in C} \overline{\Delta_{J_c}}(\mathbf{m}_c) $$

where $C$ is the set of classes, and $\overline{\Delta_{J_c}}$ is the Lovász extension of the Jaccard loss for class $c$. The total mask loss is $\mathcal{L}_{mask} = \mathcal{L}_{focal} + \eta \mathcal{L}_{lovász}$. This composite loss ensures high-precision pixel-level classification of material properties, enabling the dual-mask module to effectively isolate non-Lambertian regions despite severe data scarcity.

#### 3.6.3 Domain Alignment and Adversarial Loss

Training on heterogeneous datasets (HCInew, UrbanLF-Syn, Non-Lambertian Dataset) introduces domain shifts. A model trained with a simple aggregated loss tends to overfit to the dominant domain (e.g., Lambertian) and degrade on minority domains. 

We introduce a domain classifier attached to the shared feature extractor of the EPINet4Dir V3 backbone, optimized via a Gradient Reversal Layer (GRL) to achieve domain-invariant feature learning. Let $\mathbf{F}$ be the extracted features and $y_{dom}$ be the domain label. 

The domain classification loss $\mathcal{L}_{cls}$ is the standard cross-entropy:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{cls} = -\frac{1}{B} \sum_{j=1}^{B} \sum_{k=1}^{K} y_{dom,j}^{(k)} \log(\hat{y}_{dom,j}^{(k)}) $$

where $B$ is the batch size, $K$ is the number of domains ($K=3$ for Lambertian, Mixed, and Non-Lambertian), and $\hat{y}_{dom}$ is the predicted domain probability. 

To enforce domain alignment, the GRL reverses the gradients during backpropagation. The adversarial loss $\mathcal{L}_{adv}$ applied to the feature extractor is:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{adv} = -\mathcal{L}_{cls} $$

Thus, the overall domain loss is formulated as a minimax game: $\mathcal{L}_{domain} = \mathcal{L}_{cls}$ for the domain classifier, and $\mathcal{L}_{adv}$ for the feature extractor. This adversarial training strategy mitigates feature confusion across different reflection models, enhancing the generalization capability of the unified architecture.

#### 3.6.4 Overall Objective and Optimization Strategy

The hyperparameters are set as $\lambda_d = 1.0$, $\lambda_m = 0.5$, $\lambda_{dom} = 0.1$, $\beta = 0.1$, and $\eta = 1.0$. The depth loss is assigned the highest weight to prioritize geometric accuracy, while the mask and domain losses serve as auxiliary constraints to guide feature routing and alignment. The focusing parameter $\gamma$ in Focal loss is set to 2.0.

The network is optimized using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, which decays slightly per epoch. To maintain exposure and gradient balance across domains, a WeightedRandomSampler is employed during batch construction. This domain-balanced sampling strategy ensures each mini-batch contains a proportional representation of Lambertian, mixed, and non-Lambertian scenes, preventing the optimization from being dominated by the majority Lambertian data.
