# 3. Methodology

In this section, we present the overall architecture of our proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model. The proposed model employs a component-aware adaptive depth estimation framework, aiming to address the challenge of accurate light field depth estimation under complex non-Lambertian reflections. Its core principle involves extracting multi-directional epipolar plane images (EPIs) from the input light field, then parsing the physical reflection properties through a novel dual-mask mechanism to achieve reliable depth inference across diverse material surfaces. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the overall pipeline integrates the EPI Extraction, Directional Feature Extraction, Multi-Directional Feature Fusion, and Depth Regression modules, seamlessly coupled with our proposed physical modeling innovations to facilitate end-to-end optimization.

The input to our network is a 5D light field tensor $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times U \times V \times H \times W}$, where $B$ denotes the batch size, $U$ and $V$ represent the angular resolutions (typically $U=V=9$ for a $9 \times 9$ angular grid), and $H$ and $W$ are the spatial height and width, respectively. Different from previous works that directly feed the raw 4D light field or sub-aperture images into deep networks, our approach explicitly constructs the EPI Extraction module to reorganize the angular-spatial data. Specifically, motivated by the need to reduce computational redundancy while preserving essential geometric cues, this module extracts four 2D EPIs passing through the central viewpoint $(u_0, v_0)$ along the horizontal, vertical, and two diagonal directions. The extracted EPIs, denoted as $\text{EPI}_{hor}$, $\text{EPI}_{ver}$, $\text{EPI}_{diag1}$, and $\text{EPI}_{diag2}$, encapsulate the epipolar geometry and are formulated as:
$$ \text{EPI}_{hor}(x, u) = \mathcal{L}(u, v_0, x, y_0), \quad \text{EPI}_{ver}(y, v) = \mathcal{L}(u_0, v, x_0, y) $$
where $(x_0, y_0)$ is the central spatial coordinate. This preprocessing step remarkably reduces the computational complexity and provides a structured representation for subsequent depth reasoning.

Following the EPI extraction, the data flows into the Directional Feature Extraction module, which comprises four parallel Conv Block modules with shared weights. This weight-sharing strategy is motivated by the isotropic nature of epipolar geometry across different directions, ensuring consistent feature representation while significantly reducing the parameter count. Each Conv Block processes its corresponding EPI to capture directional geometric and textural features, yielding high-dimensional feature maps $\mathbf{F}_{dir} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{feat} \times H \times W}$ for $dir \in \{hor, ver, diag1, diag2\}$. To holistically integrate these multi-directional cues, the Multi-Directional Feature Fusion module concatenates the four feature maps along the channel dimension and applies a $1 \times 1$ convolution to achieve cross-directional feature fusion and dimensionality reduction, producing the fused global feature $\mathbf{F}_{fused} = \text{Conv}_{1 \times 1}(\text{Concat}(\mathbf{F}_{hor}, \mathbf{F}_{ver}, \mathbf{F}_{diag1}, \mathbf{F}_{diag2}))$. Crucially, in contrast to previous works that implicitly assume a global Lambertian reflection model, our architecture explicitly incorporates the MRI-like Angular Frequency Analysis and the Dual-Mask Physical Modeling and Inverse Wavevector Parsing mechanisms. The MRI-like mechanism treats the $9 \times 9$ angular sampling analogously to the k-space in MRI, applying 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) to extract angular spectrum energy distributions for pixel-level material classification. Concurrently, the dual-mask mechanism generates a medium mask to quantify surface roughness and an angular direction mask to record deflection vector fields, guiding the inverse wavevector parsing to estimate Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) parameters. These physical priors are seamlessly injected into the feature fusion process, enabling the network to disentangle complex non-Lambertian reflections from geometric depth cues.

Finally, the enriched global features are fed into the Depth Regression module. Motivated by the necessity to map the fused physical and geometric features into metric depth, this module employs a multi-layer convolutional network to output a single-channel central view depth map $\mathbf{D}_{reg} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$. A Sigmoid activation function is applied at the output layer to restrict the predicted depth values within a valid normalized range, formulated as $\mathbf{D}_{reg} = \sigma(\text{Conv}_{reg}(\mathbf{F}_{fused}))$. To further enhance robustness in non-Lambertian regions, we introduce the Component-Aware Adaptive Depth Estimation Strategy. Instead of relying on a uniform EPI-based depth inference, this strategy dynamically schedules different depth estimation branches based on the continuous material weights derived from the dual masks. Specifically, it applies a component-weighted fusion mechanism: $\mathbf{D} = w_d \mathbf{D}_{reg} + w_s \mathbf{D}_{specular} + w_{sc} \mathbf{D}_{scatter}$, where $w_d$, $w_s$, and $w_{sc}$ denote the pixel-wise confidence weights for diffuse, specular, and scatter components, respectively. During training, the network is optimized end-to-end using the L1 Loss & Domain-balanced Sampling configuration combined with a Cosine Warmup learning rate scheduler. This design sufficiently mitigates the aliasing bimodal distribution caused by specular highlights and ensures stable convergence, thereby advancing the practical realization of dense 3D perception in under-constrained real-world environments.

## 3.2 EPI提取模块 (EPI Extraction)

e EPI Extraction module is explicitly designed to reorganize the high-dimensional angular-spatial data into structured 2D representations. In light field depth estimation, directly processing the raw 5D tensor remains challenging due to the dramatic computational redundancy and the severe memory burden. However, under complex non-Lambertian reflections, the photo-consistency assumption is frequently violated. Unfortunately, this causes geometric cues in specific angular directions to be obscured by specular highlights, making depth inference highly ambiguous. Therefore, the primary objective of this module is to distill the most informative epipolar geometry while minimizing computational overhead, providing a robust foundation for subsequent physical reflection parsing.

Specifically, given the input 5D light field tensor $\mathcal{L} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times U \times V \times H \times W}$, we define the central viewpoint coordinates as $(u_0, v_0)$, where $u_0 = \lfloor U/2 \rfloor$ and $v_0 = \lfloor V/2 \rfloor$. Instead of extracting EPIs from all angular views, which typically leads to an aliasing bimodal distribution in feature learning and excessive computational cost, our module explicitly extracts 2D EPIs along four distinct directions: horizontal, vertical, $45^\circ$ diagonal, and $135^\circ$ diagonal, all intersecting the central viewpoint. The mathematical formulations for these four directional EPIs are rigorously defined as follows:
$$ \mathcal{E}_{hor}(x, u) = \mathcal{L}(u, v_0, x, y) $$
$$ \mathcal{E}_{ver}(y, v) = \mathcal{L}(u_0, v, x, y) $$
$$ \mathcal{E}_{diag1}(s, \delta) = \mathcal{L}(u_0 + \delta, v_0 + \delta, x_0 + s, y_0 + s) $$
$$ \mathcal{E}_{diag2}(s, \delta) = \mathcal{L}(u_0 + \delta, v_0 - \delta, x_0 + s, y_0 - s) $$
where $x \in [0, W-1]$ and $y \in [0, H-1]$ denote the spatial column and row indices, respectively; $u \in [0, U-1]$ and $v \in [0, V-1]$ represent the horizontal and vertical angular indices; $s$ is the spatial index along the diagonal direction; and $\delta$ is the angular offset from the center. The coordinates $(x_0, y_0)$ denote the reference spatial positions for the diagonal EPIs. Note that for $\mathcal{E}_{hor}$ and $\mathcal{E}_{ver}$, the orthogonal spatial coordinates ($y$ and $x$, respectively) act as independent batch indices to form the complete EPI volume. Through these operations, the 3D spatial-angular information is effectively projected onto 2D planes, where the slope of the linear structures in the EPIs is inversely proportional to the ground-truth scene depth.

Consequently, the output of the EPI Extraction module consists of four 2D EPI tensors. By folding the orthogonal spatial dimension into the batch dimension for parallel processing, the effective shapes of the horizontal and vertical EPIs are $(B, C, U, W)$ and $(B, C, V, H)$, respectively, where $C$ denotes the number of color channels. Similarly, the diagonal EPIs yield tensors of shape $(B, C, U, \min(H, W))$. These four directional EPIs are then fed in parallel into the subsequent Directional Feature Extraction module. In contrast to previous state-of-the-art methods that primarily rely on merely horizontal and vertical EPIs or exhaustively process all sub-aperture images, our novel four-direction extraction strategy offers two significant advantages. On the one hand, it remarkably enriches the extensive geometric context by capturing diagonal epipolar lines, which is particularly crucial for reliably estimating the depth of slanted surfaces and complex textures. On the other hand, by restricting the extraction to the central viewpoint across four directions, it sufficiently eliminates angular redundancy, ensuring high computational efficiency without sacrificing essential structural cues.

In short, the EPI Extraction module serves as a critical bridge between the raw 5D light field input and the deep feature learning pipeline. Recently, while some works have attempted to model non-Lambertian effects using heavy 3D convolutions, our approach tackles this open challenge from a data representation perspective. Nonetheless, by explicitly structuring the data into multi-directional 2D EPIs, it not only alleviates the under-constrained nature of non-Lambertian depth estimation by providing complementary geometric clues from diverse orientations but also lays a solid, computationally tractable groundwork for the end-to-end dual-mask physical modeling in the following stages.

## 3.3 方向特征提取模块 (Directional Feature Extraction)

Following the reorganization of high-dimensional angular-spatial data into structured 2D representations by the EPI Extraction module, the raw EPIs primarily contain low-level pixel intensities and local gradient variations. However, under complex non-Lambertian reflections, the photo-consistency assumption is frequently violated, causing the epipolar line slopes—which inherently encode depth information—to be severely distorted or obscured by specular highlights in specific angular directions. Therefore, directly utilizing these raw EPIs for depth inference remains challenging and highly ambiguous. To address this, the Directional Feature Extraction module is explicitly designed to distill high-level semantic and robust geometric features from the multi-directional EPIs. Its primary objective is to capture the diverse epipolar geometric structures across different orientations, providing a reliable and discriminative feature representation for subsequent physical reflection parsing and depth regression.

Its core principle involves processing the 2D EPI tensors from four distinct orientations through a unique shared-weight 2D convolutional sub-network (comprising multiple Conv2d layers, Batch Normalization, and ReLU activations) to achieve the extraction of direction-agnostic generic epipolar geometry priors. Specifically, let the input EPI tensor for a specific direction $dir \in \{hor, ver, diag1, diag2\}$ be denoted as $\mathcal{E}_{dir} \in \mathbb{R}^{B' \times C_{in} \times N \times L_{dir}}$, where $B'$ represents the effective batch size incorporating the spatial dimension orthogonal to the epipolar line (e.g., $B' = B \times H$ for horizontal EPIs), $C_{in}$ is the number of input channels, $N$ is the angular resolution (e.g., $N=9$), and $L_{dir}$ is the spatial resolution along the epipolar line. 

To extract high-dimensional features, we employ a shared-weight 2D convolutional sub-network, denoted as $\Phi_{shared}$, which consists of $K$ sequential convolutional blocks. For the $k$-th layer ($k \in \{1, 2, \dots, K\}$), the feature map is computed as:
$$ \mathcal{H}_{dir}^{(k)} = \text{ReLU}\left(\text{BatchNorm}\left(\text{Conv2D}\left(\mathcal{H}_{dir}^{(k-1)}; \mathbf{W}^{(k)}, \mathbf{b}^{(k)}\right)\right)\right) $$
where $\mathcal{H}_{dir}^{(0)} = \mathcal{E}_{dir}$ is the initial input, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ and $\mathbf{b}^{(k)}$ are the learnable convolutional kernels and bias vectors for the $k$-th layer, respectively. The $\text{Conv2D}$ operation extracts local spatial-angular patterns, while $\text{BatchNorm}$ stabilizes the training process, and $\text{ReLU}$ introduces non-linearity. After $K$ layers, the intermediate feature tensor $\mathcal{H}_{dir}^{(K)} \in \mathbb{R}^{B' \times C_{mid} \times N \times L_{dir}}$ is obtained, where $C_{mid}$ is the number of intermediate channels.

Subsequently, to compress the angular dimension $N$ and map the features back to the original 2D spatial grid, an angular aggregation operation $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ is applied along the angular axis, followed by a spatial reshape operation $\mathcal{R}(\cdot)$. The final high-dimensional directional feature map $\mathcal{F}_{dir}$ is formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{dir} = \mathcal{R}\left( \mathcal{A}\left(\mathcal{H}_{dir}^{(K)}\right) \right) = \Phi_{shared}(\mathcal{E}_{dir}), \quad dir \in \{hor, ver, diag1, diag2\} $$
where $\mathcal{A}(\cdot)$ typically utilizes a 1D convolution with a kernel size of $N \times 1$ or global average pooling to aggregate angular information, and $\mathcal{R}(\cdot)$ reshapes the tensor to the target spatial dimensions. Consequently, the module outputs four directional high-dimensional feature maps, each with the unified shape of $(B, C_{feat}, H, W)$, where $C_{feat}$ is the final feature channel dimension.

Different from previous works that typically process only orthogonal directions (horizontal and vertical) or employ independent network branches for each direction—leading to dramatic parameter redundancy and an inability to share cross-directional geometric priors—our module explicitly introduces two additional diagonal directions ($45^\circ$ and $135^\circ$) and enforces strict weight sharing across all four branches. On the one hand, the inclusion of diagonal EPIs significantly enriches the diversity of epipolar geometry, ensuring that when geometric cues in orthogonal directions are obscured by non-Lambertian specularities, the diagonal branches can still provide sufficiently reliable depth clues. On the other hand, the shared-weight mechanism not only remarkably reduces the model parameter size but also forces the network to learn direction-agnostic generic epipolar geometry priors, thereby significantly enhancing the generalization capability under under-constrained and complex illumination conditions.

In short, the Directional Feature Extraction module serves as a critical bridge in the proposed pipeline. It receives the structured 2D EPIs from the preceding EPI Extraction module and transforms them into robust, high-dimensional geometric representations. These four directional feature maps $\mathcal{F}_{hor}, \mathcal{F}_{ver}, \mathcal{F}_{diag1}$, and $\mathcal{F}_{diag2}$ are then seamlessly forwarded to the subsequent Multi-Directional Feature Fusion module for comprehensive channel-wise integration, ultimately laying a solid foundation for accurate depth regression in challenging non-Lambertian scenes.

## 3.4 多方向特征融合模块 (Multi-Directional Feature Fusion)

Although the Directional Feature Extraction module successfully distills high-level geometric priors from individual orientations, the extracted features remain isolated and direction-specific. Under complex non-Lambertian reflections, specular highlights typically exhibit strong directional anisotropy, meaning that the epipolar geometry might be severely corrupted in certain orientations (e.g., horizontal or vertical) while remaining remarkably intact in others (e.g., diagonal directions). Therefore, relying on a single direction or simply aggregating these features without considering their complementary nature remains challenging and often leads to under-constrained depth ambiguities. To address this open challenge, the Multi-Directional Feature Fusion module is explicitly designed to integrate the direction-specific features into a unified, globally consistent representation, aiming to exploit the cross-directional geometric complementarity and suppress direction-dependent reflection noise.

Its core principle involves concatenating the high-dimensional feature maps from four distinct orientations along the channel dimension, then applying a learnable cross-directional convolution to achieve comprehensive feature fusion and dimensionality reduction. Specifically, let the input high-dimensional feature maps from the preceding module for the four directions be denoted as $\mathcal{F}_{dir} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{feat} \times H \times W}$, where $dir \in \{hor, ver, diag1, diag2\}$, $B$ is the batch size, $C_{feat}$ is the number of feature channels, and $H \times W$ represents the spatial resolution. The first step is to aggregate these multi-directional features via channel-wise concatenation, which can be mathematically formulated as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{concat} = \text{Concat}(\mathcal{F}_{hor}, \mathcal{F}_{ver}, \mathcal{F}_{diag1}, \mathcal{F}_{diag2}) $$
where $\mathcal{F}_{concat} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 4C_{feat} \times H \times W}$ is the concatenated joint feature tensor, and $\text{Concat}(\cdot)$ denotes the concatenation operation along the channel dimension. This operation ensures that the distinct epipolar patterns from each orientation are fully preserved without information loss.

Subsequently, to facilitate cross-directional information interaction and eliminate channel redundancy, a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer is applied to the concatenated tensor. This step projects the high-dimensional joint features into a compact, fused global feature space. The fusion process is defined as:
$$ \mathcal{F}_{fused} = \delta(\mathbf{W}_{fused} * \mathcal{F}_{concat} + \mathbf{b}_{fused}) $$
where $\mathcal{F}_{fused} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{fused} \times H \times W}$ is the output fused global feature map, $C_{fused}$ is the predefined number of output channels (typically $C_{fused} \le 4C_{feat}$ to achieve dimensionality reduction), $\mathbf{W}_{fused} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{fused} \times 4C_{feat} \times 1 \times 1}$ and $\mathbf{b}_{fused} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{fused}}$ are the learnable weights and biases of the $1 \times 1$ convolution, $*$ denotes the convolution operation, and $\delta(\cdot)$ represents the ReLU activation function. 

Different from previous state-of-the-art works that typically aggregate multi-directional features via simple element-wise addition or average pooling, which inevitably dilutes strong directional cues and fails to resolve directional ambiguities, our module explicitly employs a channel-wise concatenation followed by a learnable $1 \times 1$ convolution. This novel design not only preserves the diverse geometric structures from all orientations but also enables the network to implicitly learn a directional attention mechanism. Through the learnable weights $\mathbf{W}_{fused}$, the model can dynamically re-weight the contributions of different directions based on local reflection properties, effectively suppressing the features corrupted by non-Lambertian highlights while emphasizing the reliable geometric cues. 

In short, the Multi-Directional Feature Fusion module serves as a critical bridge in the overall end-to-end pipeline. It receives the isolated directional representations from the Directional Feature Extraction module and outputs a robust, globally integrated feature map $\mathcal{F}_{fused}$. This sufficiently enriched representation is then seamlessly fed into the subsequent Depth Regression module, providing a reliable and unambiguous foundation for accurate depth inference in complex, non-Lambertian scenes.

## 3.5 深度回归模块 (Depth Regression)

Following the Multi-Directional Feature Fusion module, the network yields a globally consistent feature representation $\mathcal{F}_{fused}$ that effectively integrates cross-directional geometric complementarity. However, mapping these high-dimensional abstract features directly into accurate, pixel-wise depth values remains challenging, primarily due to the under-constrained nature of depth regression in the presence of complex non-Lambertian reflections. In such scenarios, specular highlights often induce an aliasing bimodal distribution in the feature space, causing conventional regression heads to produce blurred depth boundaries or severe artifacts. To address this open challenge, the Depth Regression module is explicitly designed to decode the fused global features into a high-resolution, edge-preserving central view depth map.

Its core principle involves progressively refining the local spatial context through a multi-layer convolutional regression network, then projecting the refined features into a single-channel depth prediction via a specialized convolutional layer, and finally constraining the output within a physically valid depth range through a non-linear activation function. Specifically, let the input fused global feature map from the preceding module be denoted as $\mathcal{F}_{fused} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_{fused} \times H \times W}$, where $B$ is the batch size, $C_{fused}$ is the number of fused channels, and $H \times W$ represents the spatial resolution of the central view. The regression process is formulated as a sequence of $N$ convolutional blocks. For the $i$-th block ($i \in \{1, 2, \dots, N-1\}$), the intermediate feature map $\mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(i)}$ is computed as:

$$ \mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(i)} = \phi \left( \text{BN} \left( \text{Conv2D} \left( \mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(i-1)}; \mathbf{W}_{reg}^{(i)}, \mathbf{b}_{reg}^{(i)} \right) \right) \right) $$

where $\mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(0)} = \mathcal{F}_{fused}$, $\text{Conv2D}(\cdot)$ denotes the 2D convolution operation with learnable weights $\mathbf{W}_{reg}^{(i)}$ and biases $\mathbf{b}_{reg}^{(i)}$, $\text{BN}(\cdot)$ stands for Batch Normalization to stabilize the training dynamics and accelerate convergence, and $\phi(\cdot)$ is the ReLU activation function introducing essential non-linearity. This multi-layer configuration allows the network to sufficiently capture fine-grained local spatial contexts that are crucial for dense prediction tasks.

Subsequently, to obtain the raw depth logits, a final $1 \times 1$ convolution layer with a single output channel is applied to the last intermediate feature map $\mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(N-1)}$:

$$ \mathcal{Z} = \text{Conv2D}_{1 \times 1} \left( \mathcal{F}_{reg}^{(N-1)}; \mathbf{w}_{out}, b_{out} \right) $$

where $\mathcal{Z} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ represents the unconstrained depth logits, and $\mathbf{w}_{out} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_{reg} \times 1 \times 1 \times 1}$ and $b_{out} \in \mathbb{R}$ are the corresponding weight and bias parameters for the final projection. 

To ensure the physical validity of the predicted depth and prevent physically meaningless negative values, we employ a Sigmoid activation function $\sigma(\cdot)$ to restrict the output to a normalized range, which is then scaled by a predefined maximum depth (or disparity) threshold $d_{max}$:

$$ \mathcal{D} = d_{max} \cdot \sigma(\mathcal{Z}) $$

where $\mathcal{D} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ is the final single-channel central view depth map. Here, $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$ smoothly maps the logits to the interval $(0, 1)$. This bounding mechanism is particularly vital for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation, as it effectively suppresses extreme outlier predictions caused by severe specular noise and guarantees that the estimated depth reliably falls within the practical realization limits of the scene.

Different from previous works that typically rely on a single linear mapping layer or global average pooling for depth regression, our module explicitly incorporates multi-layer local context refinement prior to the final projection. This architectural distinction is highly significant because depth estimation inherently demands precise spatial alignment. By progressively aggregating local neighborhood information, our module can dramatically preserve sharp depth boundaries and accurately resolve depth ambiguities in texture-less or highly reflective regions, which are typically problematic for state-of-the-art methods.

In short, the Depth Regression module serves as the final decoder in our end-to-end pipeline, seamlessly transforming the abstract, multi-directional geometric priors into a tangible, quantitative depth map. The output $\mathcal{D}$ is then directly fed into the L1 loss function alongside the ground-truth depth, enabling the entire Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model to be optimized jointly. This funnel-shaped data flow—progressing from 5D light field extraction to 4-directional feature representation, and ultimately to 1-channel depth regression—ensures that the network comprehensively leverages all available epipolar cues while maintaining high computational efficiency and robust generalization across complex scenes.

## 3.6 Training Objective

### 3.6 Training Objective and Loss Function

To reliably optimize the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model, we design a comprehensive training objective that integrates component-aware depth supervision, material parameter regression, and physical consistency regularization. The proposed model employs a multi-task physical learning framework, aiming to address the challenge of accurate depth estimation under complex non-Lambertian reflections. Different from previous works that typically rely on a uniform $L_1$ or $L_2$ loss globally, our formulation explicitly incorporates physical priors and material-specific confidence weighting to overcome the degradation of Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) based methods in non-Lambertian regions. The overall training objective $\mathcal{L}_{total}$ is formulated as:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \lambda_{depth} \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_{mat} \mathcal{L}_{mat} + \lambda_{phy} \mathcal{L}_{phy} $$

where $\lambda_{depth}$, $\lambda_{mat}$, and $\lambda_{phy}$ are the balancing hyperparameters that control the contribution of each loss component.

#### 3.6.1 Component-Aware Depth Estimation Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{depth}$)

**Motivation:** Conventional light field depth estimation methods predominantly assume a global Lambertian reflectance, leading to dramatic performance drops in specular or scattering regions where the EPI slope assumption is severely violated. To address this under-constrained problem, our model dynamically schedules different depth estimation branches based on the predicted material weights, rather than treating all pixels uniformly.

**Design:** Specifically, we introduce a pixel-wise confidence weighting mechanism driven by the estimated Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF) parameters. Let $\mathbf{w}(p) = [w_d(p), w_s(p), w_{sc}(p)]^T$ denote the predicted continuous weights for diffuse, specular, and scattering components at pixel $p$. The component-aware depth loss is defined as:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{depth} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \Big( w_d(p) \mathcal{L}_{EPI}(p) + w_s(p) \mathcal{L}_{spec}(p) + w_{sc}(p) \mathcal{L}_{scat}(p) \Big) $$

where $\Omega$ is the set of all valid spatial pixels, and $\mathcal{L}_{branch}(p) = \| D_{branch}(p) - D_{gt}(p) \|_1$ represents the $L_1$ distance between the branch-specific predicted depth and the ground-truth depth $D_{gt}$. Here, $D_{EPI}$, $D_{spec}$, and $D_{scat}$ are the depth maps derived from the EPI slope analysis, photometric stereo/neighborhood propagation, and scattering model branches, respectively. 

**Expected Effect:** This design explicitly forces each depth branch to specialize in its corresponding physical reflection type. By weighting the gradients with the material masks, it effectively prevents cross-domain gradient interference and ensures that the final fused depth $D = w_d D_{EPI} + w_s D_{spec} + w_{sc} D_{scat}$ is remarkably accurate across mixed scenes.

#### 3.6.2 BRDF Parameter Estimation Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{mat}$)

**Motivation:** The dual-mask physical model requires accurate estimation of the medium mask (material weights) to reliably guide the depth scheduling. Inaccurate material classification would severely mislead the subsequent depth fusion, causing aliasing bimodal distribution in the depth predictions.

**Design:** Therefore, we supervise the continuous material weights using an $L_1$ regression loss coupled with an information entropy regularization term. The material loss is given by:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{mat} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left( \| \mathbf{w}(p) - \mathbf{w}_{gt}(p) \|_1 + \gamma \mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w}(p)) \right) $$

where $\mathbf{w}_{gt}(p)$ is the ground-truth material weight vector derived from the dataset annotations, and $\gamma$ is a scaling factor. The entropy term $\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{w}(p)) = -\sum_{k \in \{d,s,sc\}} w_k(p) \log(w_k(p) + \epsilon)$ is introduced to encourage a distinct physical inclination (i.e., approaching a one-hot distribution) for each pixel, thereby avoiding ambiguous mixed weights in homogeneous regions.

**Expected Effect:** By minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{mat}$, the angle-spectrum material classification module (via 2D-DFT) is expected to achieve a high accuracy (target >85%), providing sufficiently reliable scheduling signals for the component-aware depth estimation.

#### 3.6.3 Physical Consistency Regularization ($\mathcal{L}_{phy}$)

**Motivation:** Unfortunately, purely data-driven networks often suffer from texture-copying degradation in non-Lambertian domains, where the model merely replicates input textures instead of inferring true depth structures. To fundamentally resolve this open challenge, we impose strict physical constraints based on the Radiance Tensor Field (RTF) rank properties and reverse wavevector consistency.

**Design:** The physical regularization term is composed of two sub-components: $\mathcal{L}_{phy} = \mathcal{L}_{rtf} + \mathcal{L}_{wave}$.

1) **RTF Rank Constraint ($\mathcal{L}_{rtf}$):** According to physical optics, the RTF $\mathcal{T}(p)$ constructed from the $9 \times 9$ angular samples exhibits a rank of 1 for Lambertian scenes and a rank $\ge 2$ for non-Lambertian scenes. We approximate this rank property via nuclear norm minimization and second singular value maximization:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{rtf} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left[ w_d(p) \|\mathcal{T}(p)\|_* - \beta (w_s(p) + w_{sc}(p)) \log(\sigma_2(\mathcal{T}(p)) + \epsilon) \right] $$

where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm (sum of singular values), and $\sigma_2(\cdot)$ is the second largest singular value. This explicitly forces the diffuse regions to maintain a rank-1 structure while preserving the high-rank characteristics of specular/scattering regions.

2) **Reverse Wavevector Consistency ($\mathcal{L}_{wave}$):** The angular direction mask outputs a deflection vector field $\mathbf{v}(p)$. Based on Maxwell's equations and geometric optics, this vector must be consistent with the surface normal $\mathbf{n}(p)$ (derived from the depth gradient $\nabla D$) and the viewing directions. We define:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{wave} = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{p \in \Omega} \left\| \mathbf{v}(p) - \mathcal{F}_{optics}(\mathbf{n}(p), \mathbf{\omega}_{in}, \mathbf{\omega}_{out}) \right\|_2^2 $$

where $\mathcal{F}_{optics}$ represents the theoretical deflection function governed by the law of reflection and refraction, and $\mathbf{\omega}_{in}, \mathbf{\omega}_{out}$ are the incident and outgoing light direction vectors.

**Expected Effect:** These physical constraints act as strong regularizers, ensuring that the predicted depth and material masks strictly adhere to optical laws. This significantly improves the generalization capability on complex non-Lambertian and mixed urban scenes, preventing the network from learning spurious texture correlations.

#### 3.6.4 Optimization Strategy and Hyperparameter Settings

**Design:** The overall objective is optimized end-to-end using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$, following a cosine annealing schedule with a linear warmup of 3 steps. To stabilize training and prevent gradient explosion, we apply gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0. The batch size is set to 8, combined with a gradient accumulation step of 4 to simulate a larger effective batch size under memory constraints. Automatic Mixed Precision (AMP) is utilized to accelerate the extensive synthetic evaluation and practical realization. Random horizontal flipping is consistently applied across all 81 views for data augmentation.

**Weight Settings:** The loss weights are empirically set as $\lambda_{depth} = 1.0$, $\lambda_{mat} = 0.5$, and $\lambda_{phy} = 0.1$. The primary depth loss is assigned the highest weight to guarantee the main task's convergence, while the material and physical losses serve as auxiliary and regularizing terms, respectively. The entropy scaling factor $\gamma$ and singular value scaling factor $\beta$ are set to 0.1 and 0.05. These settings are carefully chosen to balance the quantitative and qualitative performance across the Lambertian, non-Lambertian, and mixed subsets, ensuring that the model does not overfit to the dominant Lambertian data.
