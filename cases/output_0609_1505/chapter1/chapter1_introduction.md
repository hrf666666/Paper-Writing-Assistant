\section{Introduction}

Depth estimation constitutes a central task in video analysis and 3D vision systems, enabling applications ranging from autonomous navigation to immersive media rendering. Early approaches primarily relied on monocular images <citation>["Depth Map Prediction", "Single Image"]</citation> or monocular video sequences <citation>["Learning Depth", "Monocular Videos"]</citation>, which inherently suffer from scale ambiguity and occlusion issues. Light field (LF) imaging captures both spatial and angular information of light rays, providing rich geometric cues that substantially alleviate these ambiguities. By extracting epipolar plane images (EPIs), LF depth estimation methods leverage the linear structure of EPI slopes to infer accurate depth maps. Mathematically, the linear structure of an EPI is defined by the disparity $d$, where a point at spatial coordinate $x$ and angular coordinate $u$ follows the constraint $x(u) = x_0 + u \cdot d$. Advanced feature extraction networks, including residual architectures <citation>["Deep Residual Learning", "Image Recognition"]</citation> and hierarchical vision transformers <citation>["Swin Transformer", "Hierarchical Vision"]</citation>, have been widely adopted to process these dense angular signals and achieve significant improvements over single-view techniques.

Despite these advancements, the core assumption underlying EPI-based depth estimation is the Lambertian reflectance model, which posits that surface appearance remains constant across different viewpoints. In real-world environments, however, surfaces frequently exhibit non-Lambertian properties, such as specular highlights, translucency, and volumetric scattering. According to classical reflectance models <citation>["Reflectance Model", "Computer Graphics"]</citation>, specular reflections shift dynamically with viewpoint changes, while scattering materials lack a single, well-defined surface depth. These physical phenomena severely violate the EPI linearity assumption, leading to substantial performance degradation in depth estimation. When applied to non-Lambertian regions, conventional EPI methods experience a considerable increase in mean absolute error (MAE), often exceeding 0.40, which is significantly higher than the acceptable threshold of 0.25. 

The severity of this problem is compounded by the extreme scarcity of non-Lambertian LF datasets. Existing benchmarks often provide merely four training scenes for non-Lambertian materials, creating a severe data bottleneck. This data scarcity forms a hard wall that prevents purely data-driven models from generalizing. Architectural complexity, such as adding attention mechanisms <citation>["Attention Is All You Need", "Attention technique"]</citation> or dual-branch designs, cannot compensate for the violation of foundational physical assumptions when the training data is insufficient to capture the diverse angular variations of complex materials. Consequently, addressing non-Lambertian depth estimation is not merely an architectural optimization problem, but an urgent necessity requiring explicit physical modeling.

Existing attempts to address this issue typically rely on implicit feature learning, such as masked autoencoding <citation>["Masked Autoencoders", "Scalable Vision"]</citation>, treating non-Lambertian effects as unstructured noise rather than structured physical signals. Methods employing robust principal component analysis <citation>["Robust Principal Component Analysis", "Exact Recovery"]</citation> or unsupervised geometric learning <citation>["GeoNet", "Unsupervised Learning"]</citation> have shown promise in separating low-rank structures from sparse outliers, yet they struggle to explicitly decouple the complex angular variations caused by diverse material reflectance in light fields. To address these limitations, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Instead of treating material reflections as noise, our approach explicitly decouples the angular signals based on physical reflectance properties and employs a geometry-driven dual-mask architecture for adaptive feature routing.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition physical model that decouples light field signals into ambient, parallax-linear, and material-residual components. A geometric dual-mask network architecture, leveraging encoder-decoder structures <citation>["U-Net", "Biomedical Image Segmentation"]</citation>, utilizes material-specific masks to guide adaptive feature routing, substantially mitigating depth estimation errors in specular and scattering regions.
    \item We develop a domain-balanced four-direction EPI unified estimation framework integrated with a unified dataset loader and a weighted random sampling strategy. This framework effectively resolves the long-tail distribution and class imbalance across multi-domain datasets, maximizing the performance upper bound of EPI-based methods in complex mixed scenes.
\end{itemize}

Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily rely on cost aggregation and photo-consistency metrics across multiple viewpoints <citation>["Cost Aggregation LF", "Photo-consistency LF"]>. Methodologically, these approaches are strictly built upon the Lambertian reflectance assumption. When encountering non-Lambertian surfaces, the photo-consistency constraint fails, resulting in incorrect matching costs and considerable depth artifacts. At the technical level, some conventional techniques attempt to mitigate specular reflections by introducing frequency-domain analysis or explicit highlight detection <citation>["Specular Handling LF"]>. However, these operations require high angular resolution to distinguish specular components from diffuse reflections. Given the limited angular resolution of consumer-grade light field cameras, such frequency-based techniques often degrade in practical applications, failing to provide reliable material classification <citation>["Angular Resolution Limits"]>.

To overcome the limitations of hand-crafted features, early deep learning methods employ convolutional neural networks to process epipolar plane images (EPIs) or utilize dual-branch architectures for spatial and angular feature extraction <citation>["EPINet Original", "Dual Branch LF"]>. From a methodological perspective, these data-driven models treat depth estimation as a direct regression task, lacking explicit physical priors. Consequently, the networks tend to overfit to the strong linear features of Lambertian regions while treating non-Lambertian variations as noise, which compromises depth accuracy on complex materials. Technically, most early architectures extract features exclusively from horizontal and vertical EPIs, neglecting the structural information present in diagonal directions. This incomplete utilization restricts the regression capacity for EPI slopes. Although subsequent works incorporate attention mechanisms or defocus-based auxiliary heads to suppress non-Lambertian interference <citation>["Attention LF", "Defocus Auxiliary LF"]>, these modules operate merely at the feature level. They fail to physically decouple material reflectance from geometric disparity, leaving the intrinsic EPI linearity distortion unresolved.

Recent advancements focus on hierarchical vision transformers and implicit neural representations to capture long-range dependencies in light fields <citation>["Transformer LF", "Implicit LF"]>. Despite architectural improvements, these methods suffer from substantial domain shift issues. Methodologically, existing models are predominantly trained on single synthetic datasets composed entirely of Lambertian scenes. When deployed in real-world mixed environments or non-Lambertian scenarios, their generalization capability decreases considerably <citation>["Domain Shift LF", "Cross-domain LF"]>. At the technical and data levels, addressing this multi-domain challenge remains problematic. Current practices either train separate per-domain models or apply simple data mixing. However, multi-domain light field data exhibit pronounced long-tail distributions and class imbalance; for instance, non-Lambertian datasets often contain highly limited training samples compared to large-scale urban syntheses. Without effective domain-balanced sampling strategies, models inherently bias toward majority domains, neglecting minority physical phenomena <citation>["Long-tail LF", "Data Imbalance Vision"]>. The incompatibility of ground truth formats across different datasets further obstructs unified cross-domain training.

The aforementioned limitations highlight three primary bottlenecks: the absence of physically interpretable material decoupling mechanisms, insufficient utilization of multi-directional EPI structures, and the generalization degradation caused by unbalanced multi-domain data distributions. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified approach that integrates a physical reflectance model with domain-balanced learning to achieve robust light field depth estimation across diverse and complex environments.

In this paper, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model to address the depth estimation degradation caused by non-Lambertian reflectances in light field imaging. Unlike previous data-driven approaches that treat depth regression as a purely statistical mapping, our method integrates explicit physical priors into the network design. Specifically, we introduce a three-layer angular signal decomposition mechanism that models the light field angular signal $I(u,v)$ as:
\begin{equation}
I(u,v) = DC + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v),
\end{equation}
where $DC$ represents ambient illumination, the linear term parameterized by $(a, b)$ denotes disparity and depth, and $\varepsilon(u,v)$ captures material reflectance residuals. Based on this decomposition, a geometric dual-mask architecture adaptively routes features through specialized Lambertian and non-Lambertian branches. To maximize the utilization of epipolar plane image (EPI) structures and enhance cross-domain generalization, we develop a four-directional EPI estimation framework coupled with a domain-balanced mixed training strategy.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Geometric Dual-Mask Physical Model}: We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition physical model that decouples angular signals into ambient ($DC$), disparity ($a \cdot u + b \cdot v$), and reflectance ($\varepsilon(u,v)$) components. This decomposition reveals the physical root cause of EPI linearity violation on non-Lambertian surfaces. By leveraging geometric angular features (e.g., angular gradients) for material classification, we construct a dual-mask dual-branch backbone that adaptively routes features for Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. This design effectively isolates complex material interference, substantially improving depth accuracy and interpretability in challenging physical environments.
    
    \item \textbf{Four-Directional EPI and Domain-Balanced Unified Framework}: We develop the EPINet4Dir V3 architecture to comprehensively extract structural features from horizontal, vertical, and both diagonal EPIs, maximizing the regression capacity for EPI slopes. To overcome the limitations of single-dataset training and long-tail data distributions, we introduce a domain-balanced sampling and mixed training strategy utilizing a WeightedRandomSampler. Along with a unified light field dataset loader compatible with multiple ground truth formats, this framework significantly enhances the model's generalization ability in real-world mixed scenarios, such as urban environments.
    
    \item \textbf{Physics-Aware Regularization and Consistency Constraints}: We design a joint optimization scheme incorporating phase regularization and physical consistency losses to constrain the predicted displacement vector fields and material masks. The phase regularization enforces vector field smoothness in Lambertian regions while suppressing over-smoothing in non-Lambertian areas via mask weighting ($1 - \sigma(r)$). The physical consistency constraint ensures that the predictions adhere to light field imaging models and BRDF properties, preventing the network from converging to physically meaningless local optima and yielding sharper depth boundaries.
\end{enumerate}

Extensive experiments on multiple light field datasets, including HCInew, UrbanLF-Syn, and a specific Non-Lambertian dataset, validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The quantitative results demonstrate that our method achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of less than 0.20, with specific MAEs of 0.16 for Lambertian scenes, 0.22 for mixed urban environments, and 0.25 for challenging non-Lambertian surfaces. These metrics indicate considerable improvements over existing EPI-based and dual-branch baselines, particularly in preserving depth details on specular and scattering materials.
