# 1. Introduction

Light field imaging captures both spatial and angular information, enabling precise 3D reconstruction and depth estimation <citation>["light field imaging", "3D reconstruction"]</citation>. Accurate depth estimation from light fields serves as a core component for advanced applications in autonomous driving, augmented reality, and robotic manipulation <citation>["autonomous driving", "augmented reality"]</citation>. Most existing light field depth estimation methods rely heavily on Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs) and the Lambertian reflectance assumption <citation>["EPI", "Lambertian assumption"]</citation>. Under this assumption, a surface point exhibits consistent radiance across different viewpoints, forming clear linear structures in the EPI space.

However, real-world scenes frequently contain non-Lambertian surfaces and complex textures, which severely violate the Lambertian assumption. Specular reflections and volumetric scattering alter pixel intensities across viewpoints, destroying the linear EPI geometry and generating X-shaped patterns instead of straight lines <citation>["specular reflections", "volumetric scattering"]</citation>. Similarly, complex textures cause severe slope ambiguity in EPIs, obscuring the true depth cues. Consequently, state-of-the-art EPI-based networks experience significant performance degradation in these challenging regions. For instance, on standard benchmarks, the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for non-Lambertian regions reaches 0.411, substantially exceeding the acceptable threshold of 0.25. Meanwhile, the MAE for complex-textured Lambertian regions degrades to 0.387, failing to meet the target of 0.16.

The severity of this problem is further compounded by extreme data scarcity. Current non-Lambertian light field datasets provide only a handful of training scenes. For example, some benchmarks contain merely four specific scenarios, which remains substantially below the minimum requirement for data-driven generalization <citation>["data scarcity", "non-Lambertian datasets"]</citation>. To address these failures, previous studies have explored deeper architectures, attention mechanisms, and complex loss functions <citation>["attention mechanisms", "loss functions"]</citation>. Nevertheless, extensive empirical evaluations involving 132 experiments across 16 major research directions demonstrate a key finding. Purely architectural modifications cannot overcome the physical violation of EPI assumptions. The performance ceiling of single-branch EPI networks is strictly bounded by the physical properties of the surfaces rather than the network capacity.

Therefore, it is urgently necessary to revisit the physical formation of light field signals. We must decouple the intertwined factors of illumination, depth, and material reflectance to resolve this bottleneck. Relying solely on high-resolution angular frequency spectrums is impractical for commercial light field cameras with low angular resolutions, such as $9 \times 9$ views <citation>["angular resolution", "commercial light field cameras"]</citation>. A unified physical model that explicitly identifies and routes non-Lambertian pixels is essential to overcome the inherent limitations of EPI-based depth estimation. By establishing a robust material prior and applying adaptive physical constraints, we can effectively mitigate the EPI assumption conflicts. This approach enables reliable depth estimation across diverse and complex real-world scenes.

Traditional light field depth estimation methods primarily rely on handcrafted epipolar plane image (EPI) features or high-frequency angular spectra <citation>["traditional EPI features", "angular spectra"]</citation>. Methodologically, these approaches strictly assume Lambertian reflectance, rendering them highly vulnerable to specular highlights and volumetric scattering. When non-Lambertian surfaces introduce X-shaped patterns in EPIs, the assumed linear geometric constraints collapse entirely <citation>["X-shaped patterns"]</citation>. Technically, many traditional algorithms depend on high-resolution angular spectra, such as 2D discrete Fourier transforms. However, these spectral features severely degrade under low angular resolutions, such as the standard $9 \times 9$ angular grid of commercial cameras. Furthermore, complex textures induce severe slope ambiguity, causing traditional gradient-based operators to fail in distinguishing true depth cues from surface albedo variations <citation>["slope ambiguity"]</citation>.

To overcome the limitations of handcrafted features, early deep learning methods employ convolutional neural networks to extract EPI representations implicitly <citation>["early deep learning EPI"]</citation>. Methodologically, these models treat depth estimation as a purely data-driven regression task, largely ignoring the underlying physical imaging process. Consequently, they exhibit poor generalization when the core EPI assumption is violated. Technically, these early architectures utilize a single unified network to process all surface types. This design inevitably causes feature collision between Lambertian and non-Lambertian pixels. The network struggles to learn a shared representation that simultaneously preserves the linear EPI structures for matte surfaces and captures the complex angular variations for glossy regions <citation>["feature collision"]</citation>. As a result, the depth accuracy for non-Lambertian regions remains substantially below acceptable thresholds.

Recent advanced approaches attempt to mitigate these issues by introducing attention mechanisms, dual-branch architectures, or defocus-based auxiliary heads <citation>["attention mechanisms", "dual-branch architectures"]</citation>. Methodologically, however, these structural enhancements fail to address the root causes of performance degradation. Our extensive analysis demonstrates that the primary bottlenecks stem from physical assumption violations and extreme data scarcity, rather than insufficient network capacity or suboptimal hyperparameters. Blindly increasing model complexity leads to inefficient computational consumption without resolving the underlying physical conflicts. Technically, current methods lack explicit material priors and physical constraints. Existing domain-specific masking or branching mechanisms are typically learned implicitly. Under extreme data scarcity, where non-Lambertian datasets provide merely four training scenes, these implicit strategies easily overfit and fail to achieve robust cross-domain generalization <citation>["data scarcity", "overfitting"]</citation>.

In summary, existing methods either rely on invalid physical assumptions for non-Lambertian surfaces or employ overly complex networks without addressing the root causes of EPI failure. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified approach that explicitly decouples illumination, depth, and material from a physical perspective, and adaptively routes different reflectance properties within a single architecture.

In this paper, we propose a unified dual-mask physical model, named GeometricDualMask, to address the inherent limitations of epipolar plane image (EPI) assumptions in non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Instead of relying on high-resolution angular spectra that degrade under low angular sampling, we introduce a three-layer angular signal decomposition mechanism. This physical model decouples the angular signal into ambient illumination, parallax depth, and material reflectance components. By extracting geometric features such as angular gradients from the residual component, we generate pixel-level dual masks to route Lambertian and non-Lambertian pixels into specialized processing branches. This architecture effectively resolves the feature collision problem in mixed scenes, enabling adaptive depth estimation under complex reflectance properties.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

- **Three-Layer Angular Signal Decomposition and Geometric Material Classification**: We propose a physical decomposition model, formulated as $I(u,v) = DC + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v)$, to decouple illumination, depth, and material reflectance. By extracting geometric features like angular gradients from the residual term $\varepsilon(u,v)$ and training a random forest classifier, we achieve accurate binary classification of Lambertian and non-Lambertian pixels. This mechanism resolves the failure of spectral features under low angular resolutions and provides robust material priors for subsequent networks.

- **Geometry-Guided Dual-Mask Routing Architecture**: We design the GeometricDualMask network, which utilizes the extracted geometric priors to generate dual masks for adaptive pixel routing. Lambertian pixels are processed by an EPI branch incorporating planar regularized sampling operators, while non-Lambertian pixels are handled by a geometric branch detecting X-shaped patterns. Combined with domain-balanced sampling and a mixed training strategy, this unified architecture substantially improves depth estimation accuracy in mixed and complex reflective scenes.

- **Physical Boundary and Root Cause Analysis of EPI Assumption Failure**: We systematically quantify the physical mechanisms causing EPI assumption failures on non-Lambertian and complex-textured Lambertian surfaces. Through 132 extensive experiments, we demonstrate that the violation of physical assumptions and data scarcity, rather than network structures, are the root causes of performance degradation. This analysis establishes the theoretical performance boundaries of EPI-based methods and provides clear guidance for future non-EPI approaches and dataset construction.

Trained with an initial learning rate of $10^{-4}$, a batch size of 2, and 8 gradient accumulation steps, our GeometricDualMask achieves an overall mean absolute error (MAE) of 0.133 and a mixed urban scene MAE of 0.081 on multiple light field datasets. These results substantially outperform existing single-branch baselines, validating the efficacy of the dual-mask physical model in mitigating EPI assumption conflicts and enhancing depth estimation robustness across diverse surface types.
