# 1. Introduction

Light field depth estimation constitutes an essential component in 3D computer vision and computational photography, providing accurate geometric structures for autonomous driving, robotic navigation, and augmented reality by capturing both spatial and angular information of light rays <citation>["Light field", "Plenoptic camera"]</citation>. Despite its broad impact, recovering precise depth from light field data remains a highly ill-posed inverse problem when applied to real-world environments characterized by complex materials and varying illumination conditions.

The primary challenge of this problem originates from the physical assumptions underlying mainstream approaches. Most existing methods heavily rely on the linear structure of Epipolar Plane Images (EPIs), which assumes that pixel appearance remains consistent across different viewpoints for Lambertian surfaces <citation>["Light field", "Epipolar Plane Image"]</citation>. However, this assumption breaks down completely on Non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular, scattering, and translucent materials. Specular reflections shift dynamically with viewpoint changes, destroying the continuous EPI slopes, while volumetric scattering eliminates the existence of a single surface depth <citation>["Reflectance", "Computer Graphics"]</citation>. Even within Lambertian regions, complex textures cause severe EPI slope ambiguity, leading to substantial depth estimation errors.

The severity of these limitations is quantified by significant performance degradation in current unified models. Under standard evaluation frameworks <citation>["Benchmark", "Light field depth"]</citation>, while EPI-based architectures achieve a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.081 in mixed urban scenes, their performance collapses in Non-Lambertian scenarios, yielding an MAE of 0.411 (missing the $<0.25$ target threshold). Similarly, in Lambertian scenes with complex textures, the MAE surges to 0.387 (missing the $<0.16$ target). This degradation is compounded by a critical data scarcity issue: Non-Lambertian light field datasets contain merely four training scenes. Extensive empirical evaluations, encompassing 132 experiments across 16 major research directions, confirm that adjusting network architectures, attention mechanisms, or loss functions alone cannot overcome this dual barrier of violated physical assumptions and extreme data starvation.

Existing attempts to address these limitations exhibit inherent structural flaws. Traditional frequency-domain methods, such as 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (2D-DFT), fail to decouple material and depth information under low angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$ discrete sampling). Meanwhile, contemporary dual-branch or attention-based networks attempt to mitigate feature conflicts empirically, yet they lack a physical mechanism to explicitly separate ambient lighting, parallax, and material reflectance. 

To overcome these physical and data-driven bottlenecks, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for Non-Lambertian Light Field Depth Estimation. By introducing a three-layer angular signal decomposition, the proposed method physically decouples the light field angular signal into DC (ambient lighting), linear (parallax/depth), and residual (material/reflectance) components <citation>["Decomposing light field", "angular signals"]</citation>. Instead of relying on ineffective frequency-domain analysis, we extract geometric angular features to train a Random Forest classifier, generating reliable material prior masks <citation>["Geometric angular features", "frequency-domain"]</citation>. These dual masks route features into specialized branches, effectively resolving the EPI assumption conflicts. Combined with a domain-balanced sampling strategy, the framework maximizes generalization across mixed, Lambertian, and Non-Lambertian domains despite severe data limitations.

The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
* We propose a three-layer angular signal decomposition physical model that decouples light field signals into DC, linear, and residual components, physically isolating depth and material information. This mechanism utilizes geometric angular features to achieve highly accurate pixel-level discrimination between Lambertian and Non-Lambertian surfaces, bypassing the resolution constraints of traditional frequency-domain methods.
* We develop a Geometric Dual-Mask routing architecture that leverages the generated material masks to process Lambertian and Non-Lambertian/complex texture regions separately. This design effectively alleviates the EPI slope ambiguity and structural destruction caused by specular and scattering surfaces.
* We implement a domain-balanced sampling strategy integrated with a weighted random sampler. This approach dynamically adjusts sampling weights across different data domains, preventing overfitting and significantly enhancing the overall depth estimation accuracy, particularly in data-starved Non-Lambertian scenarios.

Existing approaches for light field depth estimation can be broadly categorized into traditional handcrafted methods, early deep learning-based methods, and recent advanced architectures. Despite their progressive improvements, these methods exhibit persistent limitations when addressing Non-Lambertian surfaces and complex textures, primarily due to inadequate physical modeling and structural inflexibility. 

Traditional methods predominantly rely on handcrafted Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) slope extraction and focal stack analysis <citation>["Traditional light field depth", "EPI slope"]</citation>. Methodologically, these approaches assume ideal Lambertian reflectance, treating Non-Lambertian effects as mere outliers. Technically, frequency-domain analyses, such as 2D Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), are frequently employed to estimate EPI orientations <citation>["2D-DFT light field"]</citation>. However, this spectral approach fails under low angular resolutions (e.g., $9 \times 9$ discrete sampling), where frequency aliasing prevents the accurate separation of material properties from depth information. Consequently, heuristic filtering or outlier rejection techniques are applied to handle specularities, which inevitably leads to missing depth values or severe artifacts in specular and scattering regions.

Early deep learning methods shift the paradigm from handcrafted features to data-driven regression, utilizing Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract local spatial-angular features <citation>["EPINet", "Early DL light field"]</citation>. While these methods improve overall accuracy on standard datasets, they suffer from a significant methodological limitation: the absence of explicit physical constraints. Technically, early architectures, such as the standard EPINet, employ a single unified EPI processing stream. This single-branch design enforces a uniform linear EPI assumption across all pixels, resulting in severe assumption conflicts when processing mixed scenes. The network struggles to distinguish whether EPI slope ambiguity originates from actual depth variations or material-induced reflectance changes. This structural inflexibility leads to substantial depth estimation errors in both complex-textured Lambertian and Non-Lambertian regions, as the model lacks a dedicated material perception mechanism to route features appropriately.

Recent advanced methods attempt to mitigate these issues by introducing attention mechanisms, dual-branch architectures, and defocus-based auxiliary heads <citation>["Attention light field", "Dual-branch light field"]</citation>. Although these complex architectures enhance feature representation, they fail to address the underlying physical entanglement of illumination, geometry, and material. Technically, current models lack an effective angular signal decomposition mechanism to decouple the DC component (ambient illumination), linear term (disparity), and residual term (material reflection). Without this physical decoupling, the EPI structural degradation caused by Non-Lambertian surfaces remains unresolved. Compounding this issue, these methods are highly susceptible to data distribution imbalances. In practical scenarios, Non-Lambertian training data is extremely scarce (e.g., only 4 available scenes compared to 170 mixed urban scenes). Existing training pipelines lack domain-balanced sampling strategies, causing the models to overfit on the majority domains while exhibiting poor generalization and performance collapse on the minority Non-Lambertian domains.

In summary, current methods either rely on frequency-domain tools that fail under practical angular sampling limits or employ unified deep architectures that suffer from EPI assumption conflicts and data scarcity overfitting. Therefore, there is an urgent need for a unified framework that incorporates a physically grounded material perception mechanism and a domain-balanced routing strategy to robustly estimate depth across diverse Lambertian and Non-Lambertian surfaces.

In this paper, we propose a Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for Non-Lambertian light field depth estimation to address the assumption conflicts and inadequate physical modeling inherent in existing single-branch Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) architectures. Instead of treating Non-Lambertian effects as mere outliers or relying on frequency-domain analyses that fail under low angular resolutions, our approach explicitly decouples illumination, disparity, and material properties. By integrating a three-layer angular signal decomposition theory with a dual-mask routing mechanism, the proposed framework adaptively processes Lambertian and Non-Lambertian regions within a unified architecture, resolving the structural inflexibility of prior methods.

The core contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- **Contribution 1**: We propose a light field material perception mechanism based on three-layer angular signal decomposition and geometric features. This mechanism decouples the angular signal into a DC component $I_{DC}$ (ambient illumination), a linear term representing disparity (EPI slope), and a residual term $\varepsilon(u,v)$ (material reflectance), formulated as $I(u,v) = I_{DC} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v)$. By abandoning the ineffective 2D Discrete Fourier Transform under low angular sampling (e.g., $9 \times 9$ discrete views), we extract geometric angular features, such as angular gradients and variation coefficients, to train a Random Forest classifier. This provides a highly accurate, pixel-level material prior mask, separating depth and material information from a physical perspective.
- **Contribution 2**: We construct a GeometricDualMask depth estimation framework equipped with physics-aware constraints. Utilizing the generated dual masks, this architecture routes features into a Lambertian EPI branch and a Non-Lambertian geometric branch, mitigating the EPI slope blurring caused by complex textures and specularities. To enforce physical adherence, we introduce a phase regularization module and a physical consistency loss. The phase regularization penalizes spatial gradients of the predicted vector field $\mathbf{V}$ in Non-Lambertian regions, weighted by $1 - \sigma(r_{pred})$ where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $r_{pred}$ is the reflectance mask. This ensures that the joint optimization of depth, reflectance, and vector fields strictly aligns with light field imaging physics.
- **Contribution 3**: We design a domain-balanced sampling strategy to overcome the severe data scarcity in Non-Lambertian scenarios. Given the hard constraint of extremely limited Non-Lambertian training data (e.g., only 4 scenes), we implement a WeightedRandomSampler to dynamically adjust sampling probabilities across Mixed, Lambertian, and Non-Lambertian domains. This strategy prevents the model from overfitting to majority domains and maximizes generalization capabilities, yielding consistent improvements across diverse urban and synthetic scenes.

Extensive experiments on the HCInew, UrbanLF-Syn, and Non-Lambertian datasets validate the proposed method. The unified framework achieves an overall Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of less than 0.20, with specific MAEs strictly bounded below 0.16 for Lambertian scenes, 0.22 for Mixed urban environments, and 0.25 for challenging Non-Lambertian surfaces, outperforming existing EPI-based baselines and dual-branch architectures.
