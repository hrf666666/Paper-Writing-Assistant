# 3. Methodology

In this section, we present the overall architecture of the proposed Unified Dual-Mask Physical Model for non-Lambertian light field depth estimation. Different from previous works that predominantly rely on the linear Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) assumption—which inherently fails on complex reflective surfaces—our framework explicitly decouples geometric depth and material reflectance through a physics-driven dual-branch design. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the proposed pipeline integrates an Input Normalization module, a DualMaskDepthModel backbone, and a comprehensive physical constraint mechanism. By leveraging a three-layer angular signal decomposition theory, the network routes features adaptively based on material-aware masks, enabling robust depth recovery across both Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions.

The pipeline begins with the Input Normalization module, which processes the raw light field tensor. The input consists of multi-view light field images, specifically formulated as a 5-view RGB configuration flattened into a 15-channel tensor, denoted as $\mathbf{X}_{in} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 15 \times H \times W}$, where $B$, $H$, and $W$ represent the batch size, height, and width, respectively. The motivation for this preprocessing step is to mitigate the adverse effects of varying illumination conditions and to accelerate network convergence by standardizing the input distribution. Specifically, we apply a channel-wise Min-Max normalization to map the intensity values linearly into the $[-1, 1]$ range. The normalized light field tensor $\mathbf{X}_{norm}$ is computed as:
$$
\mathbf{X}_{norm} = \frac{\mathbf{X}_{in} - \mathbf{X}_{min}}{\mathbf{X}_{max} - \mathbf{X}_{min} + \epsilon} \times 2.0 - 1.0
$$
where $\mathbf{X}_{min}$ and $\mathbf{X}_{max}$ are the minimum and maximum values along the channel dimension for each sample, and $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$ is a small constant to prevent division by zero. This normalization ensures that the subsequent feature extractors operate on a unified scale, enhancing the stability of gradient propagation.

Following preprocessing, $\mathbf{X}_{norm}$ is fed into the DualMaskDepthModel, which serves as the core backbone for feature extraction and physical decomposition. The design motivation stems from the fact that non-Lambertian reflections introduce non-linear residuals that corrupt standard EPI slopes. To address this, we formulate the light field angular signal based on a three-layer decomposition theory:
$$
I(u,v) = I_{DC} + a \cdot u + b \cdot v + \varepsilon(u,v)
$$
where $I(u,v)$ is the pixel intensity at angular coordinates $(u,v)$, $I_{DC}$ represents the ambient illumination (DC component), $a$ and $b$ denote the linear parallax slopes corresponding to depth, and $\varepsilon(u,v)$ captures the material-dependent residual. Guided by this physical prior, the DualMaskDepthModel employs a dual-branch architecture comprising a Lambertian EPI branch and a Non-Lambertian geometric feature branch. The Lambertian branch utilizes a 4-direction EPI extractor to capture linear parallax structures, while the non-Lambertian branch extracts geometric angular features—such as angular gradients and variation coefficients—to characterize complex reflectance properties. A material-aware mask, generated via a pre-trained geometric random forest classifier, dynamically routes and fuses features from both branches. This dual-mask routing mechanism effectively alleviates the assumption conflicts inherent in single-branch EPI architectures, allowing the network to isolate depth cues from specular or scattering artifacts.

The DualMaskDepthModel ultimately yields three core physical predictions: a depth map $\mathbf{D}_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$, a reflectance mask $\mathbf{R}_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$, and a disparity vector field $\mathbf{V}_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$. To ensure these outputs strictly adhere to the underlying optical physics, we design a comprehensive optimization scheme involving multiple loss functions. The depth and reflectance predictions are supervised by Mean Squared Error (MSE) losses against their respective ground truths, denoted as $\mathcal{L}_{depth}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{r}$. To enforce spatial coherence, a Phase Regularization module is introduced to compute the spatial gradients of $\mathbf{V}_{pred}$ and penalize them using an inverted sigmoid weight derived from $\mathbf{R}_{pred}$:
$$
\mathcal{L}_{phase} = \text{mean}\left( \left( \left(\frac{\partial V_x}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial V_y}{\partial y}\right)^2 \right) \odot (1.0 - \sigma(\mathbf{R}_{pred})) \right)
$$
where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function, and $V_x, V_y$ are the spatial components of the vector field. This regularization enforces smoothness in Lambertian regions while relaxing the constraint in non-Lambertian areas. A Physical Consistency Loss $\mathcal{L}_{phys}$ is also applied to $\mathbf{V}_{pred}$ and $\mathbf{R}_{pred}$ to guarantee that the predicted geometry and material properties satisfy light field imaging constraints. The total loss is formulated as a weighted sum: $\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{depth} + 0.5\mathcal{L}_{r} + 0.1\mathcal{L}_{phase} + \lambda_{phys}\mathcal{L}_{phys}$, which is optimized via backpropagation with a gradient clipping max norm of 1.0 to ensure stable training across diverse data domains.

## 3.2 输入预处理模块 (Input Normalization)

The pipeline begins with the Input Normalization module, which processes the raw light field tensor. The input consists of multi-view light field images, specifically formulated as a 5-view RGB configuration flattened into a 15-channel tensor, denoted as $\mathbf{X}_{in} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times H \times W}$, where $B$, $C=15$, $H$, and $W$ represent the batch size, number of channels, height, and width, respectively. The primary motivation for this preprocessing step is to mitigate the adverse effects of varying illumination conditions and to accelerate network convergence by standardizing the input distribution. In non-Lambertian scenes, specular highlights and complex reflections introduce extreme intensity variations across different views. Conventional global normalization techniques, such as Z-score standardization, often suffer from sensitivity to these outliers, which suppresses the subtle radiometric differences in shadowed or diffuse regions. To address this, we employ a sample-wise, channel-wise Min-Max normalization strategy that preserves local contrast while mapping the intensity values to a standardized symmetric range.

Specifically, for each sample $b \in \{1, \dots, B\}$ and each channel $c \in \{1, \dots, C\}$, we first compute the spatial minimum and maximum intensity values across the height and width dimensions:
$$ x_{min}^{(b,c)} = \min_{h,w} \mathbf{X}_{in}^{(b,c,h,w)} $$
$$ x_{max}^{(b,c)} = \max_{h,w} \mathbf{X}_{in}^{(b,c,h,w)} $$
where $h \in \{1, \dots, H\}$ and $w \in \{1, \dots, W\}$ denote the spatial coordinates. The raw tensor is then linearly scaled to the $[0, 1]$ interval to form an intermediate normalized tensor $\hat{\mathbf{X}}$:
$$ \hat{\mathbf{X}}^{(b,c,h,w)} = \frac{\mathbf{X}_{in}^{(b,c,h,w)} - x_{min}^{(b,c)}}{x_{max}^{(b,c)} - x_{min}^{(b,c)} + \epsilon} $$
Here, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$ is a small constant introduced to prevent division by zero in cases where a channel exhibits uniform intensity. To align with the optimal input distribution for deep convolutional networks and to facilitate symmetric gradient updates in the subsequent dual-branch architecture, we further linearly map the values to the $[-1, 1]$ interval. The final normalized light field tensor $\mathbf{X}_{norm}$ is computed as:
$$ \mathbf{X}_{norm}^{(b,c,h,w)} = 2.0 \cdot \hat{\mathbf{X}}^{(b,c,h,w)} - 1.0 $$
The output $\mathbf{X}_{norm} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C \times H \times W}$ retains the original spatial and angular dimensions but possesses a unified, zero-centered distribution.

This normalized tensor $\mathbf{X}_{norm}$ serves as the direct input to the DualMaskDepthModel backbone. By providing a standardized and zero-centered feature space, the Input Normalization module ensures that the subsequent Lambertian EPI branch and Non-Lambertian geometric branch operate on a consistent numerical scale. This consistency is critical for the mask-based feature fusion mechanism, as it prevents scale-induced biases during the adaptive weighting of dual-branch features.

Different from previous light field depth estimation methods that typically apply fixed global scaling or dataset-level mean-variance normalization, our module explicitly adapts to the dynamic range of each individual sample and channel. In non-Lambertian environments, the relative radiometric contrast between specular highlights and diffuse surfaces carries important cues for material-aware mask prediction. By performing normalization strictly within the spatial extent of each channel for every sample, the proposed approach preserves these critical high-frequency material signatures. Meanwhile, the symmetric $[-1, 1]$ mapping yields a balanced gradient flow, which significantly improves the training stability and convergence speed of the physics-driven dual-branch network.

## 3.3 双掩码深度估计主干网络 (DualMaskDepthModel)

Following the normalization of the input light field tensor, the data is processed by the DualMaskDepthModel. Traditional light field depth estimation methods rely heavily on the photo-consistency assumption, which posits that a 3D point projects to a linear structure in the Epipolar Plane Image (EPI). Non-Lambertian surfaces, such as specular highlights and complex reflections, violate this assumption, causing severe depth artifacts. To address this limitation, the DualMaskDepthModel is designed to explicitly decouple the geometric structure from material properties. The primary objective is to predict accurate depth maps while simultaneously identifying non-Lambertian regions, enabling robust scene reconstruction in complex lighting environments.

The architectural design is guided by the three-layer angular signal decomposition theory. The observed intensity $I(u,v)$ of a light field ray at angular coordinates $(u,v)$ can be formulated as:
$$ I(u,v) = I_{DC} + \alpha u + \beta v + \varepsilon(u,v) $$
where $I_{DC}$ represents the ambient illumination (direct current component), $\alpha$ and $\beta$ denote the linear parallax slopes along the horizontal and vertical angular dimensions, respectively, and $\varepsilon(u,v)$ captures the high-frequency residual caused by non-Lambertian reflectance and sensor noise. This physical prior dictates that the geometric depth is intrinsically linked to the linear slopes $(\alpha, \beta)$, while the material properties are encoded in the residual $\varepsilon(u,v)$ and the ambient term $I_{DC}$.

The module takes the normalized light field tensor $\mathbf{X}_{norm} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 15 \times H \times W}$ as input. To extract distinct physical attributes, a dual-branch architecture is employed, consisting of a Lambertian EPI branch and a Non-Lambertian geometry branch. The Lambertian EPI branch, denoted as $\mathcal{F}_{EPI}$, utilizes 3D convolutions to capture the linear parallax slopes $(\alpha, \beta)$ across the angular and spatial dimensions. The Non-Lambertian geometry branch, denoted as $\mathcal{F}_{Geo}$, employs 2D spatial convolutions to extract contextual structures and high-frequency residual features $\varepsilon(u,v)$. The feature extraction processes are defined as:
$$ \mathbf{F}_{EPI} = \mathcal{F}_{EPI}(\mathbf{X}_{norm}) $$
$$ \mathbf{F}_{Geo} = \mathcal{F}_{Geo}(\mathbf{X}_{norm}) $$
where $\mathbf{F}_{EPI}, \mathbf{F}_{Geo} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times C_f \times H \times W}$ represent the extracted feature maps, and $C_f$ is the number of feature channels.

To integrate these complementary features, a reflectance mask is predicted to indicate the probability of a pixel exhibiting non-Lambertian behavior. The mask $\mathbf{M}_r \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ is generated by concatenating the dual-branch features and applying a $1 \times 1$ convolutional layer $\mathcal{H}_r$ followed by a sigmoid activation $\sigma$:
$$ \mathbf{M}_r = \sigma(\mathcal{H}_r(\mathbf{F}_{EPI} \oplus \mathbf{F}_{Geo})) $$
where $\oplus$ denotes channel-wise concatenation. The values in $\mathbf{M}_r$ range from 0 to 1, where higher values correspond to stronger non-Lambertian reflections. The fused feature map $\mathbf{F}_{fused}$ is then computed by weighting the branch features using the predicted mask:
$$ \mathbf{F}_{fused} = (1 - \mathbf{M}_r) \odot \mathbf{F}_{EPI} + \mathbf{M}_r \odot \mathbf{F}_{Geo} $$
where $\odot$ represents element-wise multiplication with spatial broadcasting. This masking mechanism ensures that the network relies on EPI slope features in diffuse regions and switches to geometric context features in specular regions.

The final physical quantities are regressed from the fused feature map $\mathbf{F}_{fused}$. The depth prediction $\mathbf{D}_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ and the disparity vector field $\mathbf{V}_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$ are obtained through dedicated convolutional heads $\mathcal{H}_d$ and $\mathcal{H}_v$:
$$ \mathbf{D}_{pred} = \mathcal{H}_d(\mathbf{F}_{fused}) $$
$$ \mathbf{V}_{pred} = \mathcal{H}_v(\mathbf{F}_{fused}) $$
The vector field $\mathbf{V}_{pred}$ contains the horizontal and vertical disparity components, providing dense correspondence information. The module outputs $\mathbf{D}_{pred}$, $\mathbf{M}_r$ (denoted as $r_{pred}$ in the system pipeline), and $\mathbf{V}_{pred}$, which are subsequently utilized by the Phase Regularization and Physical Consistency Loss modules to enforce physical constraints during training.

Different from previous works that implicitly learn feature representations without physical constraints, our module explicitly incorporates the three-layer angular signal decomposition prior to decouple geometry and material properties. This explicit formulation prevents the network from overfitting to specular highlights, thereby improving robustness in non-Lambertian regions. To support dynamic light field video processing and system-level deployment, the dual-branch design maintains a streamlined computational footprint by restricting 3D convolutions exclusively to the EPI branch, ensuring efficient inference without compromising depth accuracy.

## 3.4 相位正则化模块 (Phase Regularization)

Following the extraction of the decoupled geometric and material representations by the DualMaskDepthModel, the predicted parallax/vector field $V_{pred}$ and reflectance mask $r_{pred}$ are forwarded to the Phase Regularization module. In light field depth estimation, the parallax field inherently exhibits spatial smoothness within Lambertian regions due to the continuous nature of physical surfaces. However, in non-Lambertian regions characterized by specular highlights and complex reflections, the view-dependent appearance variations induce severe gradient fluctuations and artifacts in the estimated vector field. Conventional regularization techniques, such as Total Variation (TV), apply uniform smoothing across the entire image, which either blurs genuine geometric discontinuities at object boundaries or fails to suppress noise in highly reflective areas. To address this limitation, we design a physics-aware adaptive regularization mechanism that dynamically modulates the smoothness constraint based on the underlying material properties.

The primary objective of the Phase Regularization module is to enforce spatial coherence on the vector field exclusively within Lambertian regions while relaxing the constraint in non-Lambertian areas. The module takes the predicted vector field $V_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$ and the reflectance mask $r_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ as inputs. The vector field $V_{pred}$ comprises the horizontal and vertical parallax components, denoted as $V_x$ and $V_y$, respectively.

First, we compute the spatial gradients of the vector field components along their corresponding dimensions. The partial derivatives of $V_x$ with respect to the horizontal spatial coordinate $x$, and $V_y$ with respect to the vertical spatial coordinate $y$, are calculated using discrete difference operators:
$$ \nabla_x V_x = \frac{\partial V_x}{\partial x}, \quad \nabla_y V_y = \frac{\partial V_y}{\partial y} $$
where $\nabla_x$ and $\nabla_y$ represent the discrete gradient operators along the $x$ and $y$ axes, capturing the local variations of the parallax field.

Next, an adaptive weight map is generated to distinguish between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. The reflectance mask $r_{pred}$ encodes the intensity of non-Lambertian reflectance; a higher value indicates a stronger non-Lambertian component. To map these values into a valid weighting range, we apply the Sigmoid function $\sigma(\cdot)$ and invert the result:
$$ W = 1.0 - \sigma(r_{pred}) $$
where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ is the adaptive weight map, and $\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$. In this formulation, pixels with high reflectance (large $r_{pred}$) yield weights close to zero, effectively suppressing the regularization penalty. Conversely, Lambertian pixels (small $r_{pred}$) produce weights approaching one, enforcing strong spatial smoothness.

Finally, the phase regularization loss is computed by taking the weighted mean of the squared spatial gradients. The mathematical formulation is expressed as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{phase} = \frac{1}{B \cdot H \cdot W} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \sum_{i=1}^{H} \sum_{j=1}^{W} \left( (\nabla_x V_x^{(b,i,j)})^2 + (\nabla_y V_y^{(b,i,j)})^2 \right) \odot W^{(b,i,j)} $$
where $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication, and the superscript $(b,i,j)$ indexes the batch, height, and width dimensions. The output of this module is the scalar loss $\mathcal{L}_{phase}$, which is subsequently integrated into the overall objective function alongside the depth regression loss and the physical consistency loss.

Different from previous works that rely on generic edge detectors or isotropic smoothing priors to regularize depth maps, our module explicitly leverages the physically derived reflectance mask to guide the vector field optimization. By integrating the material decomposition prior directly into the regularization process, the proposed approach prevents the over-smoothing of non-Lambertian boundaries and effectively mitigates highlight-induced depth artifacts. This physics-aware design ensures that the geometric structure remains consistent with the underlying material properties, yielding more accurate and robust depth estimations in complex lighting environments.

## 3.5 物理一致性损失模块 (Physical Consistency Loss)

While the Phase Regularization module effectively modulates local spatial smoothness based on material properties, it does not explicitly constrain the joint physical distribution of the predicted geometry and material representations. In physical light field imaging, depth (parallax) and reflectance are intrinsically coupled through the surface rendering equation. Treating them as independent regression targets often leads to physically implausible predictions, such as spurious depth discontinuities within homogeneous material regions or non-conservative parallax fields in purely Lambertian areas. To address this limitation, we design the Physical Consistency Loss module. The primary objective of this module is to enforce joint physical constraints on the predicted vector field and reflectance mask, ensuring that the decoupled outputs strictly adhere to the physical laws of light field formation.

The Physical Consistency Loss module receives the predicted vector field $V_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$ and the reflectance mask $r_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 1 \times H \times W}$ as inputs. The vector field $V_{pred}$ consists of horizontal and vertical parallax components, denoted as $V_x$ and $V_y$, respectively. The core operations are encapsulated within the `physical_consistency_loss` function, which computes a scalar physical consistency loss, denoted as $\mathcal{L}_{phys}$. This loss is formulated as a combination of a conservative field constraint and a geometry-material boundary alignment constraint.

The first component of the loss function enforces a conservative field constraint on the parallax field. According to epipolar geometry, the parallax field in purely Lambertian regions corresponds to the gradient of a scalar depth map, which mathematically requires the field to be conservative (i.e., its curl must be zero). In contrast, non-Lambertian regions, such as specular highlights, introduce view-dependent parallax shifts that violate this conservative property. We define the two-dimensional curl of the vector field as:
$$ \text{curl}(V_{pred}) = \frac{\partial V_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial V_x}{\partial y} $$
where $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}$ denote the spatial partial derivatives along the horizontal and vertical axes, respectively. To enforce the physical prior, we penalize the curl magnitude exclusively in Lambertian regions by weighting it with the inverted reflectance mask. The conservative field loss $\mathcal{L}_{curl}$ is computed as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{curl} = \frac{1}{B \cdot H \cdot W} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \sum_{i=1}^{H} \sum_{j=1}^{W} \left( \text{curl}(V_{pred})_{b,i,j} \right)^2 (1 - r_{pred, b,i,j}) $$
where $b$, $i$, and $j$ index the batch, height, and width dimensions, respectively, and $r_{pred, b,i,j}$ denotes the scalar reflectance value at spatial location $(i, j)$ for the $b$-th sample (the singleton channel dimension is omitted for brevity). This formulation guarantees that the parallax field remains conservative in diffuse regions while permitting non-zero curl in highly reflective areas.

The second component establishes a geometry-material boundary alignment constraint. In physical scenes, material transitions (e.g., object boundaries or texture changes) frequently coincide with geometric discontinuities. Conversely, within regions of homogeneous reflectance, the surface geometry typically varies smoothly, and abrupt depth changes are physically unjustified unless caused by occlusions. To suppress spurious depth edges in uniform material areas, we constrain the spatial gradients of the vector field using the reflectance gradients. Let $\nabla V_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times 2 \times H \times W}$ represent the Jacobian matrix of the vector field, and $\nabla r_{pred} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times 2 \times H \times W}$ denote the spatial gradient of the reflectance mask. The boundary alignment loss $\mathcal{L}_{edge}$ is defined as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{edge} = \frac{1}{B \cdot H \cdot W} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \sum_{i=1}^{H} \sum_{j=1}^{W} \|\nabla V_{pred}\|_{F, b,i,j}^2 \exp\left(-\alpha \|\nabla r_{pred}\|_{2, b,i,j}^2\right) $$
where $\|\cdot\|_F$ denotes the Frobenius norm of the Jacobian matrix, measuring the magnitude of parallax variations, and $\|\cdot\|_2$ represents the $L_2$ norm of the reflectance gradient. The parameter $\alpha$ is a sensitivity scalar that controls the suppression strength. When the reflectance gradient is minimal (indicating a homogeneous material), the exponential term approaches unity, heavily penalizing any parallax gradients. When a significant material boundary exists, the exponential term decays towards zero, allowing the vector field to exhibit corresponding geometric discontinuities.

The overall physical consistency loss is obtained by linearly combining these two components:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{phys} = \lambda_{curl} \mathcal{L}_{curl} + \lambda_{edge} \mathcal{L}_{edge} $$
where $\lambda_{curl}$ and $\lambda_{edge}$ are hyperparameters balancing the respective constraints. Different from previous works that treat depth estimation and material segmentation as isolated tasks optimized by independent loss functions, our module explicitly models the joint physical distribution of geometry and reflectance. By integrating $\mathcal{L}_{phys}$ into the total objective function alongside the depth regression loss, reflectance regression loss, and the aforementioned phase regularization term, the network is compelled to generate physically plausible predictions. This design substantially reduces structural artifacts and improves the robustness of depth estimation in complex non-Lambertian environments. The computed scalar $\mathcal{L}_{phys}$ is subsequently forwarded to the joint optimization solver to update the network parameters via backpropagation.

## 3.6 Training Objective

### 3.6 Training Objective

The training objective of the proposed GeometricDualMask framework is formulated to jointly optimize depth estimation and material-aware angular signal decomposition. Unlike previous works that rely solely on geometric reconstruction, our objective explicitly incorporates physical reflectance properties to resolve the assumption conflicts inherent in unified Epipolar Plane Image (EPI) processing. The total loss function comprises four distinct terms: a depth estimation loss, a reflectance mask loss, a phase regularization term, and a physical consistency constraint.

#### 3.6.1 Depth Estimation Loss
**Motivation:** The primary task of the framework is to recover accurate disparity maps across diverse surface types, ranging from purely Lambertian to highly specular regions. A robust geometric supervision signal is required to guide the dual-branch architecture.

**Design:** We employ the Mean Squared Error (MSE) to measure the discrepancy between the predicted depth map $\hat{D}$ and the ground truth $D$. The depth loss is defined as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{depth} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| D_i - \hat{D}_i \|_2^2 $$
where $N$ denotes the number of valid pixels in the training batch, and $i$ indexes the spatial coordinates. 

**Expected Effect:** This term provides the foundational geometric supervision, ensuring that the dual-branch feature routing effectively captures the underlying parallax structures and yields precise depth estimates across the mixed domains.

#### 3.6.2 Reflectance Mask Supervision
**Motivation:** To prevent the network from degenerating into a single-branch EPI extractor, the material perception mechanism requires explicit supervision to distinguish between Lambertian and non-Lambertian regions. Without this constraint, the network struggles to decouple depth from material-induced angular variations.

**Design:** The predicted reflectance and scattering mask $r_{pred}$ is supervised using the material priors generated by the geometric random forest classifier. We utilize the MSE loss to align the predicted mask with the pseudo-ground truth $r_{gt}$:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{r} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| r_{gt, i} - r_{pred, i} \|_2^2 $$
where $r_{gt, i} \in [0, 1]$ indicates the probability of the $i$-th pixel belonging to a non-Lambertian surface.

**Expected Effect:** This supervision forces the network to learn decoupled material features. Consequently, the subsequent dual-mask routing can process complex textures and specular highlights appropriately, thereby alleviating the assumption conflicts in unified EPI processing.

#### 3.6.3 Phase Regularization for Angular Decomposition
**Motivation:** In the proposed three-layer angular signal decomposition, the light field angular signal is decoupled into a DC component, a linear parallax term, and a residual phase term $V_{pred}$. The phase term captures high-frequency material variations. Without proper constraints, $V_{pred}$ may absorb low-frequency geometric information, leading to mode collapse and inaccurate depth derivation.

**Design:** We derive a phase regularization term to enforce spatial smoothness and physical plausibility on $V_{pred}$. Assuming that material reflectance properties vary continuously within local neighborhoods, we apply a Total Variation (TV) penalty to the predicted phase map:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{phase} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| \nabla V_{pred, i} \|_1 $$
where $\nabla$ denotes the spatial gradient operator. 

**Expected Effect:** This regularization restricts $V_{pred}$ to represent only localized angular residuals. It preserves the integrity of the linear parallax term and stabilizes the angular decomposition process, ensuring that the geometric depth cues are not erroneously attributed to material reflectance.

#### 3.6.4 Mask-Guided Physical Consistency
**Motivation:** Standard photometric consistency losses fail on non-Lambertian surfaces because specularities and scattering violate the constant brightness assumption across viewpoints. Applying a uniform photometric penalty across all pixels introduces severe pseudo-gradients in highlight regions.

**Design:** We formulate a mask-guided physical consistency loss that leverages the predicted reflectance mask $r_{pred}$ to modulate the photometric error. Let $I_c$ be the central view and $I_t$ be a target view. The warping function $W(\cdot, \hat{D})$ maps pixels based on the predicted depth. The physical consistency loss is defined as:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{pc} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (1 - r_{pred, i}) \left\| I_c(\mathbf{x}_i) - I_t\left(W(\mathbf{x}_i, \hat{D}_i)\right) \right\|_2^2 $$
By weighting the photometric error with $(1 - r_{pred, i})$, the network dynamically adjusts the strictness of the geometric constraint based on the local material properties.

**Expected Effect:** This design explicitly embeds the physical imaging model into the optimization process. It ensures strict geometric consistency in Lambertian regions while preventing pseudo-gradients from non-Lambertian artifacts from corrupting the depth estimation, which is particularly important given the severe data scarcity in non-Lambertian training scenes.

#### 3.6.5 Overall Objective and Optimization Strategy
The composite training objective is formulated as a weighted sum of the individual loss terms:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{depth} + \lambda_r \mathcal{L}_{r} + \lambda_{phase} \mathcal{L}_{phase} + \lambda_{pc} \mathcal{L}_{pc} $$

Based on extensive empirical validation, the hyperparameters are set to $\lambda_r = 0.5$, $\lambda_{phase} = 0.1$, and $\lambda_{pc} = 0.1$. The weight $\lambda_r = 0.5$ balances the primary depth task with the auxiliary material classification, preventing the mask branch from dominating the gradient updates. The smaller weight $\lambda_{phase} = 0.1$ provides sufficient regularization without over-constraining the high-frequency residuals.

The network is optimized using the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of $10^{-4}$ and an initial learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-4}$. To maintain stable training dynamics across the mixed domains (Lambertian, Mixed/Urban, and Non-Lambertian), we employ a domain-balanced sampling strategy via a `WeightedRandomSampler`. This ensures equitable exposure to all surface types and prevents the model from overfitting to the majority Lambertian domains. Gradient clipping with a maximum norm of $1.0$ is applied to prevent gradient explosion during the optimization of the complex multi-term objective.
